## Quick answer: when the 45-day exemption works

Vietnam's 45-day visa exemption can be useful if the traveler has an eligible passport, only needs a temporary stay, and understands which rule applies to that passport group.

It is not one universal rule for everyone. In 2026, the main 45-day exemption framework has two important layers:

| Group | Main rule | Stay length | Purpose scope |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Resolution No. 44/NQ-CP group | 12 countries including the UK, several EU countries, Russia, Japan, South Korea, and Nordic countries | 45 days from date of entry | Broader wording, regardless of passport type or entry purpose, subject to Vietnam's entry conditions |
| Resolution No. 229/NQ-CP group | 12 additional European countries under Vietnam's tourism-stimulus program | 45 days from date of entry | Tourism purposes, subject to Vietnam's entry conditions |

The practical decision is not only "Is this passport eligible?" It is also:

- whether the trip fits the allowed purpose;
- whether 45 days is enough;
- whether an e-visa would be safer;
- whether the traveler plans to leave and re-enter;
- whether there is a backup if a border run, e-visa approval, or airline check goes wrong.

For a wider comparison of Vietnam visa options, see the broader [Vietnam Visa Guide 2026](/vietnam/visa/visa-guide-2026).

## The two 45-day exemption groups in 2026

Vietnam's 45-day visa exemption should be split into two groups because the purpose wording is not identical.

### Group 1: Resolution No. 44/NQ-CP

Resolution No. 44/NQ-CP on visa exemption for citizens of the Federal Republic of Germany, the French Republic, the Italian Republic, the Kingdom of Spain, the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland, the Russian Federation, Japan, the Republic of Korea, the Kingdom of Denmark, the Kingdom of Sweden, the Kingdom of Norway, and the Republic of Finland when entering Vietnam, dated 7 March 2025 and effective from its signing date, covers citizens of:

- Germany
- France
- Italy
- Spain
- the United Kingdom and Northern Ireland
- Russia
- Japan
- South Korea
- Denmark
- Sweden
- Norway
- Finland

For this group, the exemption period is 45 days from the date of entry. The policy window runs from 15 March 2025 to 14 March 2028.

The official wording for this group is broader than the tourism-stimulus group: it applies regardless of passport type or entry purpose, as long as the traveler meets Vietnam's entry conditions.

That does not mean entry is guaranteed. Immigration still controls admission at the border, and the traveler must meet the relevant entry conditions.

### Group 2: Resolution No. 229/NQ-CP

Resolution No. 229/NQ-CP on visa exemption under the Tourism Development Stimulus Program for citizens of the Kingdom of Belgium, the Republic of Bulgaria, the Republic of Croatia, the Czech Republic, Hungary, the Grand Duchy of Luxembourg, the Kingdom of the Netherlands, the Republic of Poland, Romania, the Slovak Republic, the Republic of Slovenia, and the Swiss Confederation, dated 8 August 2025 and effective from its signing date, covers citizens of:

- Belgium
- Bulgaria
- Croatia
- Czech Republic
- Hungary
- Luxembourg
- the Netherlands
- Poland
- Romania
- Slovakia
- Slovenia
- Switzerland

For this group, the exemption period is also 45 days from the date of entry. The policy window runs from 15 August 2025 to 14 August 2028.

The important difference is purpose. This group is under Vietnam's tourism-development stimulus program, and the exemption is for tourism purposes.

Do not merge this group into the Resolution 44 wording. For example, a Swiss, Dutch, Polish, or Belgian passport holder should not treat the 45-day exemption as an all-purpose stay permission.

## Eligibility matrix: who qualifies, who does not, and what to check

| Reader situation | 45-day exemption position | Main caution |
|---|---|---|
| German, French, Italian, Spanish, UK ordinary, Russian, Japanese, South Korean, Danish, Swedish, Norwegian, or Finnish citizen | Eligible under Resolution No. 44/NQ-CP | Still subject to Vietnam's entry conditions |
| Belgian, Bulgarian, Croatian, Czech, Hungarian, Luxembourgish, Dutch, Polish, Romanian, Slovak, Slovenian, or Swiss citizen | Eligible under Resolution No. 229/NQ-CP | Tourism-purpose scope must be preserved |
| UK ordinary passport holder | Covered in the Resolution 44 group | Do not confuse this with BNO |
| British National Overseas passport holder | Not covered by the UK ordinary-passport exemption | Needs the correct visa or other entry permission |
| Passport from a country outside both 45-day groups | Not covered by these two 45-day exemptions | Check whether another bilateral exemption, e-visa, or visa route applies |
| Traveler entering Phu Quoc only | Separate Phu Quoc exemption may apply | This is not the nationwide 45-day exemption |
| Long-term worker, sponsored employee, investor, family-based resident, or long-stay expat | 45-day exemption may be useful only as a temporary entry tool | It is not a stable residence or work-compliance solution |

Passport validity and entry conditions matter. A traveler should not treat nationality alone as enough. For unilateral visa-exempt entry, the safer checklist is:

- passport valid for at least 6 months;
- passport in good physical condition;
- no entry-refusal issue;
- clear plan for leaving or extending through a valid route;
- documents matching the declared trip purpose;
- no reliance on an expired, single-entry, or pending visa.

## How the 45 days are counted

The official exemption wording uses 45 days from the date of entry.

For planning, treat the entry date as part of the stay period. Do not build a plan that depends on squeezing out an extra day because of flight timing, late-night arrival, or a misunderstanding of stamp dates.

The practical check is simple:

1. Look at the actual entry stamp or temporary-stay date given at the border.
2. Use that date as the controlling date.
3. Plan the exit, e-visa switch, or next status before that date, not on that date.

If the stamp or date looks wrong, resolve it immediately at the airport or with the relevant immigration office. Do not wait until the last day of the stay.

## Re-entering Vietnam after a visa-exempt stay

This is where official rules and practical execution need to be separated.

Official layer: the 45-day exemption gives a temporary stay for the entry on which the traveler is admitted. Vietnam still decides admission at each entry.

Practical layer: many travelers use exits and re-entries as part of their Vietnam planning. However, repeated short-gap re-entry should not be treated as guaranteed, unlimited, or risk-free. Airline staff, border officers, or the traveler's own document setup can still create problems.

The safest wording is this:

- A visa-exempt entry can be useful for a short stay.
- A later re-entry may be possible if the traveler still qualifies and is admitted.
- Same-day or very short-gap re-entry is a practical gray area, not a promised reset.
- Repeated use can look different from a normal tourist or short-stay pattern.
- If the next entry matters, a valid e-visa may be a cleaner fallback.

For route planning, land-border timing, and exit/re-entry logistics, see the [Vietnam Visa Run Guide 2026](/vietnam/visa/visa-run-guide-2026).

## Old 30-day-gap advice

Older online advice may still mention a 30-day waiting period between visa-exempt entries.

Do not treat old 30-day-gap advice as the 2026 rule without checking current official sources. The safer practical approach is different:

- do not assume a fixed 30-day gap applies;
- do not assume same-day re-entry is guaranteed;
- decide the next entry basis before leaving Vietnam;
- carry evidence for the next entry, especially when flying;
- use an e-visa if the trip cannot absorb a denied boarding or re-entry problem.

This article should not be read as a guarantee that a border run will work. It is a planning guide for reducing avoidable visa mistakes.

## The "empty pocket" trap

"Empty pocket" is not an official Vietnamese immigration term. It is a practical planning trap.

It means the traveler enters Vietnam with no usable backup permission if the 45-day plan fails.

Common versions include:

| Trap | What goes wrong |
|---|---|
| Entering visa-exempt while assuming an e-visa will be approved later | The e-visa is delayed, rejected, or not available in time |
| Entering visa-exempt while holding an unused e-visa but not making the entry basis clear | The traveler later discovers the stay is controlled by the actual entry stamp, not the visa they hoped to use |
| Leaving Vietnam after using a single-entry e-visa | The traveler cannot re-use that single-entry e-visa to return |
| Planning a border run with no approved e-visa backup | A same-day or short-gap re-entry problem leaves the traveler outside Vietnam |
| Waiting until the final days of the 45-day stay | There is no margin for e-visa delay, flight disruption, holiday closure, or document correction |

The way to avoid the trap is to know which permission is being used for each entry:

- visa exemption;
- single-entry e-visa;
- multiple-entry e-visa;
- sponsored visa;
- TRC or other long-term status.

The stamp and the issued permission matter more than the traveler's intended plan.

## Visa exemption vs e-visa: which one should you use?

A 45-day exemption is usually simpler for an eligible short stay. An e-visa may be safer when the stay is longer, the route is more complex, or the traveler wants clearer entry documentation.

| Situation | Better starting point |
|---|---|
| Eligible passport, ordinary short stay under 45 days, simple itinerary | 45-day exemption may be enough |
| Stay may exceed 45 days | E-visa or another route is usually safer |
| Traveler plans to leave and re-enter | Check whether e-visa, multiple-entry e-visa, or a new exemption entry fits the route |
| Traveler is in the Resolution 229 group and the purpose is not tourism | Do not rely on the tourism-purpose exemption without further official advice |
| Traveler wants a clearer paper trail for airline check-in | E-visa may reduce ambiguity |
| Traveler needs work, residence, sponsorship, or long-term status | Exemption and ordinary e-visa planning may be insufficient |

Vietnam's official e-visa portal (evisa.xuatnhapcanh.gov.vn) gives a processing instruction of 3 working days and shows the standard e-visa fee class as USD 25 for single-entry and USD 50 for multiple-entry. Those are useful planning anchors, not a reason to apply at the last moment.

Practical layer: build a buffer. E-visa timing can be affected by corrections, payment issues, holidays, weekends, and document problems.

For the full application process, documents, photo rules, fee caveats, processing-time planning, and entry-port selection, use the [Vietnam E-visa Application Guide 2026](/vietnam/visa/evisa-application-guide-2026).

## Single-entry vs multiple-entry e-visa trap

For exit-and-return planning, the key question is not simply "Do you have an e-visa?"

The key question is:

> Is it single-entry or multiple-entry?

A single-entry e-visa is not a reusable permission after it has been used for entry. If the traveler leaves Vietnam after using a single-entry e-visa, that visa cannot be treated as a return ticket.

A multiple-entry e-visa can be more suitable for a planned exit and return, but the traveler still needs to check:

- validity dates;
- permitted entry and exit checkpoints;
- passport details;
- name order and date of birth;
- whether the route matches the issued e-visa.

If the traveler is eligible for a 45-day exemption and also has an e-visa, do not assume the system will automatically choose the option that is better for the later plan. Decide the intended basis of entry before reaching the counter, keep printed documents, and check the actual stamp or temporary-stay date after entry.

## Long-stay strategies using exemption and e-visa combinations

Some foreigners use the 45-day exemption as one piece of a longer Vietnam plan. That can be practical, but it should not be described as residence planning.

Think of these as temporary-stay patterns, not stable immigration status.

### Pattern 1: exemption first, e-visa later

This can work when the first stay is short and the traveler needs more time later.

Risk points:

- e-visa approval may take longer than expected;
- the traveler may apply too late;
- the e-visa may not match the intended entry point;
- the traveler may assume they can switch status inside Vietnam without confirming the route.

### Pattern 2: e-visa first, exemption later

This can work when the traveler wants a longer first permission and may return later for a shorter stay.

Risk points:

- single-entry e-visa cannot be reused after exit;
- multiple-entry e-visa must still be valid;
- the next visa-exempt entry is still a new admission decision.

### Pattern 3: exemption, short exit, exemption again

This is common in traveler discussions, but it is the grayest pattern.

Risk points:

- same-day re-entry is not a promised right;
- repeated short-gap entries can attract questions;
- airline staff may ask for onward travel or correct documentation;
- the traveler may have no backup if re-entry is refused or delayed.

### Pattern 4: exemption while preparing a TRC or sponsored status

This may be a bridge, not a solution.

Risk points:

- documents may take longer than expected;
- the sponsor may not be ready;
- the exemption may expire before the next status is issued;
- work or residence issues may require a different legal basis.

For long-term stay, sponsored status, or residence-card planning, see the [Vietnam TRC Application Guide 2026](/vietnam/visa/trc-application-guide-2026).

## Airline check-in can be the first practical checkpoint

Vietnamese immigration decides admission, but the first practical document check may happen before the traveler boards the plane.

Airline staff may ask for:

- passport validity;
- visa or visa-exemption eligibility;
- onward or return ticket;
- e-visa approval if the traveler says they are entering on an e-visa;
- proof that the itinerary fits the allowed stay.

Reports vary. Some travelers are not asked for onward proof. Others are asked at check-in. The safer plan is to carry exit evidence rather than rely on a previous traveler's experience.

For a visa-exempt entry, especially when flying, prepare:

- passport;
- onward or exit booking;
- accommodation address;
- printed e-visa if using e-visa instead of exemption;
- backup plan if boarding staff do not understand the exemption.

## E-visa checkpoints and old port lists

Visa exemption and e-visa are different entry tools.

A visa-exempt traveler is not choosing an e-visa checkpoint. An e-visa traveler must use an approved e-visa entry and exit checkpoint.

Old e-visa port lists can be incomplete. Resolution No. 389/NQ-CP on supplementing the list of international border gates allowing foreigners to enter and exit by electronic visas, dated 2 December 2025 and effective from its signing date, supplemented the e-visa checkpoint list.

Because port lists can change, this article should not maintain a full checkpoint table. Before applying for an e-visa, check the current official e-visa portal and the detailed guidance in the [Vietnam E-visa Application Guide 2026](/vietnam/visa/evisa-application-guide-2026).

## When the 45-day exemption is not enough

The 45-day exemption is not enough when the traveler needs:

- a stay longer than 45 days without an exit plan;
- a purpose outside the exemption's scope;
- work authorization;
- sponsored employment status;
- family-based residence;
- investor or business residence status;
- a stable long-term Vietnam setup;
- predictable re-entry without border-run uncertainty.

For many expats, the exemption is best viewed as a short-stay tool or arrival bridge. It is not a substitute for the correct long-term visa, sponsorship route, or residence card.

## Common mistakes

### Mistake 1: treating all 45-day exemptions as identical

Resolution 44 and Resolution 229 are not identical in purpose wording. The Resolution 229 group should be handled as tourism-purpose unless official guidance says otherwise.

### Mistake 2: ignoring BNO status

UK ordinary passport holders and BNO passport holders should not be treated as the same for the 45-day exemption.

### Mistake 3: assuming old 30-day-gap advice is current

Old advice can remain online for years. Use current official rules and the actual entry decision, not a stale blog post.

### Mistake 4: thinking a single-entry e-visa can be reused

A single-entry e-visa is not a multiple-entry permission. If the plan includes leaving and coming back, check the e-visa type before departure.

### Mistake 5: waiting until the final days

Visa and border problems become harder to fix near the end of a 45-day stay. Apply, exit, or change plans with buffer time.

### Mistake 6: treating border-run reports as guarantees

Community reports can show what often happens, but they are not legal authority and do not bind airlines or immigration officers.

## FAQ

### Can I enter Vietnam visa-free for 45 days in 2026?

Yes, if the passport is in one of the eligible 45-day groups and the traveler meets Vietnam's entry conditions. The exact scope depends on the country group.

### Which countries get the 45-day exemption?

The Resolution 44 group includes Germany, France, Italy, Spain, the United Kingdom and Northern Ireland, Russia, Japan, South Korea, Denmark, Sweden, Norway, and Finland.

The Resolution 229 group includes Belgium, Bulgaria, Croatia, Czech Republic, Hungary, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, Poland, Romania, Slovakia, Slovenia, and Switzerland, under the tourism-stimulus framework.

### Does the UK exemption include BNO passports?

No. BNO passport holders should not rely on the UK ordinary-passport exemption and should check the correct visa route.

### Can I leave Vietnam and come back for another 45 days?

It may be possible, and many travelers plan around exits and re-entries. But it should not be treated as guaranteed, unlimited, or risk-free. Same-day or short-gap re-entry is a practical gray area.

### Is there still a 30-day waiting period between visa-exempt entries?

Do not rely on old online advice that states a fixed 30-day gap as the current 2026 rule. At the same time, do not assume immediate re-entry is guaranteed. Treat short-gap re-entry as a risk-managed decision.

### Should I use the 45-day exemption or apply for an e-visa?

Use the exemption only if it fits the passport, purpose, stay length, and re-entry plan. Use an e-visa if the stay may exceed 45 days, the itinerary needs clearer documentation, or the traveler wants a cleaner basis for a longer temporary stay.

### What is the empty pocket trap?

It is an editorial shorthand for entering Vietnam with no usable backup if the 45-day plan fails. Examples include pending e-visa approval, wrong e-visa type, no onward plan, or relying on a single-entry e-visa after leaving Vietnam.

### Does the 45-day exemption allow work?

Do not treat the 45-day exemption as work authorization or long-term residence status. Employment, sponsored work, and residence-card planning require a different analysis.

### Do I need to print my e-visa?

If entering on an e-visa, follow the official e-visa instruction and print the approved e-visa for entry and exit. If entering visa-exempt, still keep relevant travel documents ready.
