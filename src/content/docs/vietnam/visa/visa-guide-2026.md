## Before You Apply: The 6-Month Passport Rule

This catches people every single week. Vietnam enforces a strict rule: **your passport must be valid for at least 6 months from your date of entry.** This is checked by both the airline at check-in and by immigration on arrival.

Even if you have an approved E-visa in hand, airlines will deny you boarding if your passport has 5 months and 29 days of validity left. Check your expiration date before you book your flight.

## The 2026 Visa System: What Are Your Options?

Vietnam overhauled its visa system in late 2023 under **Resolutions No. 127 and 128/NQ-CP**, and those rules remain the standard today.

### 1. Visa Exemption (The 45-Day Free Entry)

Citizens from certain countries can enter Vietnam without applying for anything. As of early 2026, the following countries have unilateral visa exemption agreements with Vietnam:

**Eligible countries include:** UK, Germany, France, Spain, Italy, Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Finland, Russia, Japan, South Korea, and Belarus.

This list has changed multiple times in recent years. **Always confirm the current list on the Vietnamese immigration website before booking your flight**, as countries can be added or removed with relatively short notice.

- **Duration:** Up to 45 days per entry.
- **Cost:** Free.
- **Who it's for:** Short-term tourists, snowbirds escaping the winter, or anyone testing the waters before committing to a longer stay.
- **Money-saving tip:** If you're from one of these countries and staying less than 45 days, you do not need an E-visa. Don't waste $25-50 on one — just show up with your passport.

### 2. The 90-Day E-Visa

Vietnam does not have an official "Digital Nomad Visa." When people talk about living here as a remote worker, they're using the 90-day E-visa. This is the workhorse visa for most expats.

- **Duration:** Up to 90 days from the date of issue.
- **Types:** Single Entry or Multiple Entry.
- **Cost:** $25 USD (single entry) or $50 USD (multiple entry).
- **Who it's for:** Remote workers, long-term backpackers, expats in between visa types, and anyone who doesn't qualify for visa exemption or needs to stay longer than 45 days.

**Critical: Understanding Single Entry vs. Multiple Entry**

This confuses a lot of people. Both types have a **total validity of 90 days from the date of issue** — not 90 days per entry. The difference is:

- **Single Entry:** You enter Vietnam once. The moment you leave the country (for any reason), the visa is used up and invalid — even if you've only been in Vietnam for 10 days out of 90.
- **Multiple Entry:** You can leave and re-enter Vietnam as many times as you want within the 90-day validity window. This is essential if you plan to take side trips to other countries during your stay.

If you're staying in Vietnam the entire time and not leaving, a Single Entry E-visa is all you need. Save the $25 difference.

### 3. Long-Term Visas: Work Permits and TRC

If you want to stay for years without doing visa runs every 90 days, you need a legitimate sponsor.

- **Work Permit + TRC (Temporary Residence Card):** A Vietnamese employer sponsors your work permit, which you then use to apply for a 2-to-3-year TRC. This is the standard path for employed expats.
- **Business Visa (DN):** Sponsored by a Vietnamese company for business activities. Valid for various durations. Be very careful about the sponsor — see the "Ghost Sponsors" warning below.

### 4. The 5-Year Visa Exemption Certificate

This is a lesser-known option specifically for:

- Foreign spouses of Vietnamese citizens
- Overseas Vietnamese (Viet Kieu)

You apply for a small certificate booklet (about $10 USD in government fees) that gives you unlimited entries for 5 years, with each stay lasting up to 180 days.

**Where to apply:** Your local Immigration Department (Phòng Quản lý xuất nhập cảnh). In Hanoi, this is the Immigration Office at 44-46 Tran Phu, Ba Dinh District.

**Basic documents needed:**

- Your passport (original + copy)
- Marriage certificate (notarized Vietnamese translation if the original is in another language)
- Spouse's Vietnamese ID card or Viet Kieu documentation
- Application form (provided at the office)
- 2 passport-sized photos

Processing typically takes 5-7 working days. Many expats hire an agent ($100-150 USD) to handle it, but you can do it yourself if you bring a Vietnamese-speaking friend.

For expats who've married a Vietnamese partner but don't want or need a work permit, this is often the most practical long-term solution.

### 5. Visa on Arrival: No Longer Available for Regular Tourists

Pre-pandemic, you could buy an "Approval Letter" online and get your visa stamped at the airport. **Visa on Arrival is no longer available for regular tourists.** The system has not been restored for ordinary travelers since COVID.

If an agency is currently selling you an "approval letter" for a tourist VoA, they are either scamming you or operating in a gray area. The airline may deny you boarding.

## Step-by-Step: Applying for the Vietnam E-Visa Yourself

This takes about 15 minutes. Do not pay an agency $50+ for something you can do for $25.

1. **Go to the ONLY official website:** [`evisa.xuatnhapcanh.gov.vn`](https://evisa.xuatnhapcanh.gov.vn/). Any site that does not end in `.gov.vn` is a third-party agency that will charge you extra. This includes sites ending in `.govt.vn`, `.org`, or `.com` — those are not government sites.
2. **Prepare your documents:**
    - A clear photo/scan of your passport data page
    - A recent portrait photo (white background, no glasses — applications with glasses in the photo get rejected)
3. **Fill out the form carefully:** Your name must exactly match the Machine Readable Zone (MRZ) — that's the two lines of code at the bottom of your passport page. First name vs. surname mix-ups are the #1 reason for processing delays.
4. **Choose Single Entry or Multiple Entry:** If you're staying in Vietnam the entire time, pick Single Entry ($25). If you plan to take side trips to other countries and re-enter Vietnam during your 90-day window, pick Multiple Entry ($50). Remember: both types expire 90 days from the date of issue regardless.
5. **⚠️ Select your Port of Entry — and get it right:** You must choose your entry point from a dropdown menu (e.g., Noi Bai Airport, Tan Son Nhat Airport, Moc Bai land border). **This choice is locked once approved.** If you select Noi Bai Airport but later decide to enter via the Moc Bai land border, immigration will deny you entry. You'll have to apply and pay for a completely new E-visa.
6. **Pay and wait:** Official processing time is 3 working days, but allow 5-7 days around Vietnamese national holidays (Tet, Liberation Day, National Day).

## Visa Runs: How to Reset Your Stay

In-country extensions for tourist visas have become extremely unreliable and expensive. Some agents claim they can extend your E-visa without you leaving, but this typically involves gray-area visa type conversions that may cause problems later. Most expats find visa runs cheaper and more predictable.

**The good news:** Vietnam's revised immigration law (Law No. 23/2023/QH15) abolished the old rule requiring a 30-day gap between visa-free entries. You can now leave and re-enter the same day with a new E-visa.

### How It Works

1. Apply for your new 90-day E-visa **before** your current visa expires. Make sure to select the correct land border or airport as your Port of Entry.
2. Travel to the border. The most popular routes:
    - **From Ho Chi Minh City:** Bus to Moc Bai border crossing (Cambodia). About 3-4 hours each way. The most popular and cheapest visa run route in the south.
    - **From Hanoi:** Flight to Bangkok or Vientiane (most common). Alternatively, bus to the Huu Nghi / Friendship Gate border crossing (China/Guangxi), about 4-5 hours, but only practical if you have a valid Chinese visa. Some expats also fly to Luang Prabang or Kuala Lumpur for a mini-trip.
    - **From Da Nang / Central Vietnam:** Bus to Lao Bao border crossing (Laos), about 4-5 hours. Or a short flight to Bangkok or Kuala Lumpur.
3. Exit Vietnam (get your departure stamp).
4. Enter the neighboring country (you'll need a Cambodia Visa on Arrival at $30 USD, or a Laos visa, or your existing visa for the destination country).
5. Turn around and re-enter Vietnam on your new E-visa.

If you're doing frequent visa runs, make sure your travel medical insurance covers you while in transit across borders — some policies have gaps during overland crossings.

## Overstaying Your Visa: The Penalties

Do not gamble with overstaying. Penalties under Decree 144/2021/ND-CP are enforced and escalate quickly:

| Overstay Duration | Fine (VND) | Fine (Approx. USD) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 - 15 days | 500,000 - 2,000,000 | $20 - $80 |
| 16 - 29 days | 3,000,000 - 5,000,000 | $120 - $200 |
| 30 - 59 days | 5,000,000 - 10,000,000 | $200 - $400 |
| 60 - 89 days | 10,000,000 - 15,000,000 | $400 - $600 |
| 90+ days | 15,000,000+ | $600+ plus deportation risk |

For overstays under 15 days, you can usually pay the fine directly to immigration officers at the airport before your departure flight. Bring Vietnamese dong in cash.

**Severe overstays (90+ days)** carry consequences beyond fines: forced deportation, a ban on re-entering Vietnam, and a permanent mark on your immigration record that other countries can see.

**Scam warning:** If someone offers to "clear" a short overstay for $300 USD, ignore them. Go to the airport early, pay the official fine at the immigration counter, and leave. It's simple.

## How to Find a Reliable Visa Agent

You don't need an agent for a standard E-visa — do it yourself and save money. But for Work Permits, TRC applications, or urgent airport situations, a good agent is worth the fee.

**Where to find one:**

**Red flags — walk away if an agent:**

- Has no physical office (or won't tell you where it is)
- Demands payment entirely in cryptocurrency
- Promises they can extend your tourist visa for 90 days without you leaving the country
- Asks for your original passport to "hold" during the process (copies are fine, originals are not)
- Quotes a price dramatically below the market range in the table above (they may cut corners or use fake sponsors)

## Ghost Sponsors: The TRC Trap

This deserves its own section because the consequences are severe.

Some agencies offer to "sponsor" your Business Visa (DN) or Temporary Residence Card through a shell company — you pay them, they file paperwork through a company you'll never work for, and you get your visa. This is illegal.

There have been increasing reports of crackdowns by Vietnamese immigration police targeting these arrangements. If your fake sponsor company gets investigated, your visa is immediately invalidated. The consequences include:

- Visa cancellation
- Fines
- Deportation
- Potential ban on re-entering Vietnam

If you want a legitimate long-term visa, either find real employment or explore the 5-Year Exemption route if you're married to a Vietnamese citizen.

## FAQ

**Can I change my port of entry on an approved E-visa?**
No. You must apply and pay for a completely new E-visa with the correct port of entry selected.

**Does Vietnam have a Digital Nomad Visa?**
No. There is no official digital nomad visa. Remote workers use the 90-day multiple-entry E-visa and do visa runs to reset their stay.

**What's the difference between Single Entry and Multiple Entry E-visa?**
Both are valid for 90 days total. Single Entry means you can only enter Vietnam once — if you leave, the visa is used up. Multiple Entry lets you exit and re-enter as many times as you want within the 90-day window. If you're not planning to leave Vietnam during your stay, Single Entry is fine and saves you $25.

**Can I get a bank account on a tourist visa?**
No, not in any meaningful sense. As of 2025–2026, major banks — Vietcombank, BIDV, Techcombank, and HSBC — have stopped accepting tourist visas entirely, in line with State Bank of Vietnam anti-money laundering regulations. A handful of smaller banks (TPBank, ACB) may open a heavily restricted payment account at individual branch discretion, but the success rate is low, the account will not issue a physical debit card, cannot receive cash deposits, and will be suspended the moment your visa expires. The "try a different branch" approach that worked a few years ago is largely no longer reliable.

For day-to-day spending on a tourist visa, two options work well in practice: use your home-country debit card for ATM withdrawals, or use a [Wise multi-currency card](https://wise.com/invite/dic/ganqiuh) — registered outside Vietnam — to hold a VND balance and spend or withdraw locally at mid-market rates without excessive FX fees. See our [Banking Guide](/vietnam/banking/bank-account-guide-2026) for the full breakdown by visa status.

**I overstayed by 2 days. Am I in serious trouble?**
Not serious trouble, but you will pay a fine (500,000-2,000,000 VND). Head to the airport early, go to the immigration counter, explain the situation, pay the fine in cash, and you'll be allowed to leave.

**Can I do a visa run and come back the same day?**
Yes. Since the 2023 law change (Law No. 23/2023/QH15), the old 30-day gap requirement between entries has been abolished. You can exit and re-enter on the same day with a valid new E-visa or visa exemption.

**How far in advance should I apply for my E-visa?**
At least 7-10 days before you need it. Official processing is 3 working days, but delays happen, especially around holidays. Don't leave it to the last minute.

**Is the visa exemption list the same as last year?**
Not necessarily. Vietnam has adjusted this list several times in recent years. Always verify the current list on the official Vietnamese immigration website before relying on visa-free entry.
