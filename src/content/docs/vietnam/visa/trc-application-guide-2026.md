Most people who land on this page have already done the math on back-to-back 90-day e-visas, lost a weekend to a border run in Cambodia, and started asking whether there's a more stable way to live in Vietnam. The answer for anyone planning to stay longer than a year is the Vietnam Temporary Residence Card, or TRC (*thẻ tạm trú* in Vietnamese) — a physical card issued by the Vietnam Immigration Department that lets you live in the country legally for 1 to 10 years, enter and exit freely without a new visa, open a proper bank account, sign long-term leases, and register a motorbike in your own name.

This Vietnam TRC guide is written for people who have already decided they want one and need the operational details: which category applies to you, exactly which documents you need, how much the government charges under the new Circular 28/2026 that took effect on April 1, 2026, and the specific ways applications get rejected. It assumes you are already in Vietnam on a valid visa and are trying to figure out what comes next.

One scope note before getting started: this is a guide to the TRC, not a general Vietnam visa guide. If you are still at the "which visa should I enter on" stage, start with our [Vietnam visa guide 2026](/vietnam/visa/visa-guide-2026) first, then come back here when you're ready to upgrade to residency.

## Key Takeaways

- **A TRC is not a visa.** It's a separate residence permit issued after you're already in Vietnam on a qualifying visa, and it replaces the need for visa renewals during its validity.
- **The legal basis changed in April 2026.** Circular 28/2026/TT-BTC replaced the older Circular 25/2021/TT-BTC on April 1, 2026. Fees stayed the same ($145–$165 depending on validity) but the broader legal framework was rewritten.
- **LD-class TRCs require a Work Permit first.** You cannot apply for a work-based TRC without already holding a valid Vietnam Work Permit or a Work Permit Exemption Certificate. These are two sequential applications, not one combined process.
- **DT4 investors do not qualify for a TRC.** Under Law 51/2019/QH14, investors contributing less than 3 billion VND (roughly $120,000) are classified as DT4 and are only eligible for a visa, not a residence card. This is the single most common source of disappointment among small-capital foreign founders in Vietnam.
- **There is still no Vietnam Digital Nomad Visa.** As of April 2026, Vietnam has not launched a dedicated digital nomad visa or TRC. The real paths to legal long-term residence remain LD (work), DT (properly capitalized investment), or TT (spouse-based).

## What a Vietnam TRC Actually Gets You

A Vietnam Temporary Residence Card is a plastic card, roughly the size of a credit card, issued by the Vietnam Immigration Department (*Cục Quản lý xuất nhập cảnh*) under the Ministry of Public Security. Its legal definition comes from Article 3 of Law 47/2014/QH13 on the Entry, Exit, Transit and Residence of Foreigners in Vietnam, as amended by Law 51/2019/QH14 and Law 23/2023/QH15. It is a document that grants a foreigner the right to reside in Vietnam for a specified period, and it functions as the equivalent of a visa during that period.

On the ground, holding a TRC means:

- **No more visa runs.** You don't need to leave Vietnam and re-enter to maintain your legal status for the entire validity of the card (1 to 10 years depending on category).
- **Multiple entries and exits without new visas.** The card itself is your re-entry document.
- **Full-function Vietnamese bank accounts.** Banks like Vietcombank, Techcombank, and BIDV will open ordinary resident accounts for TRC holders. E-visa holders and tourists are typically restricted to limited accounts.
- **Long-term lease contracts in your own name**, without the landlord having to deal with monthly foreigner registration renewals.
- **Vehicle registration.** You can legally buy and register a motorbike or car in your own name.
- **Dependent derivative applications.** Your spouse and children under 18 can apply for TT-category TRCs derived from yours.

What a TRC does *not* get you: the right to work in a job you didn't already have a Work Permit for, permanent residence (PR is a separate, much harder application), or exemption from Vietnamese tax obligations. A TRC confirms where you live; it does not change your employment rights or your tax residency status.

One important thing most guides skip: **a TRC has hidden maintenance conditions.** If your LD TRC is tied to a Work Permit and you quit, get laid off, or your company closes, the TRC is legally invalidated even if the physical card hasn't expired yet. Similarly, DT TRC holders whose companies stop operating or whose invested capital is withdrawn can have their cards revoked. After revocation, foreigners typically have only 15 days to leave Vietnam before they're considered out of status. A TRC is not a "set and forget" document; its validity depends on the underlying legal basis staying in place throughout its life.

## The Seven TRC Categories (And Which One You Fall Into)

Vietnam issues TRCs under specific symbol codes that match the visa symbol the foreigner entered on. The seven most relevant for expats are:

| Symbol | Category | Maximum Validity | Who It's For |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **LĐ1** | Work (exempt from Work Permit) | 2 years | Foreign managers / experts exempt from the Work Permit requirement |
| **LĐ2** | Work (with Work Permit) | 2 years | Foreign employees holding an ordinary Vietnam Work Permit |
| **ĐT1** | Investor (≥ 100 billion VND) | 10 years | Large investors contributing ~$4M USD or more |
| **ĐT2** | Investor (50–100 billion VND) | 5 years | Investors contributing ~$2M–$4M USD |
| **ĐT3** | Investor (3–50 billion VND) | 3 years | Investors contributing ~$120K–$2M USD |
| **TT** | Spouse / Dependent | 3 years | Spouses / children under 18 of Vietnamese citizens or foreign TRC holders |
| **DH** | Student | 5 years | Students enrolled in accredited Vietnamese institutions |

Three more categories exist but are less relevant to most readers of this site. **LS** covers foreign lawyers licensed by the Ministry of Justice, up to 5 years. **PV1** covers resident foreign journalists, up to 2 years, and requires prior approval from the Ministry of Foreign Affairs' Press Department. **NG3** covers diplomatic family members, up to 5 years, processed by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs rather than the Immigration Department.

If you don't fit cleanly into one of these seven, you probably cannot get a Vietnam TRC, and this is where most expat frustration starts. There is no general-purpose "long-term residence" category for remote workers, retirees without Vietnamese family ties, or small-capital entrepreneurs. The Vietnamese system is built around sponsorship: someone (an employer, an invested company, a Vietnamese family member, or a school) has to legally vouch for your presence.

## The DT4 Trap: Why Small Investors Don't Get a TRC

The single most common source of disappointment among foreign founders in Vietnam is the DT4 classification. Under the amendments introduced by Law 51/2019/QH14, investors whose contributed capital is **below 3 billion VND (roughly $120,000 USD)** are categorized as DT4, and **DT4 holders are only eligible for a visa, not a TRC**.

This catches a lot of people off guard. Someone registers a small trading or consulting company in Vietnam with $30,000 or $50,000 of capital, thinking the "investor visa" will give them residence rights, and then discovers that their DT4 status only buys them a visa valid for up to 12 months that has to be renewed repeatedly. If you're planning to use the investment pathway to get a TRC, your capital contribution needs to clear the 3 billion VND threshold, and the money has to be **actually transferred** into the company's Direct Investment Capital Account (DICA), not just committed on paper. Immigration officers, coordinating with the Department of Planning and Investment and tax authorities, actively verify that invested capital has arrived in Vietnam before approving DT TRCs.

Shell companies with nominal capital used to be a common workaround in the mid-2010s. Enforcement has tightened significantly since the late 2010s, especially in HCMC and Hanoi, though execution varies by province. If your company has no real operations, no office, no employees, and no tax filings, a DT TRC can be revoked retroactively, and once revoked, you typically have only 15 days to leave Vietnam.

## LD Category: The Work-Based TRC Path

This is the most common path to a Vietnam TRC and covers most employed Western expats.

### The Pre-Entry Approval Step Most Guides Skip

Before your sponsoring employer can even bring you to Vietnam as an LD-class foreigner, they usually need to submit a **Form NA5** (*Đơn đề nghị duyệt nhân sự nước ngoài nhập cảnh*) to the Vietnam Immigration Department. This is a pre-entry personnel approval request, and it's separate from both the Work Permit and the TRC applications.

The practical sequence for most LD applicants looks like this:

1. Employer submits Form NA5 requesting approval to bring you to Vietnam
2. Immigration Department issues an approval code and sends it to the Vietnamese embassy in your country
3. You collect your LĐ visa at the embassy (or get it on arrival, depending on the route)
4. You enter Vietnam on the LĐ visa
5. Employer applies for your Work Permit at the provincial Department of Labor (DoLISA)
6. Once the Work Permit is issued, employer files Form NA6 + your Form NA8 at the Immigration Department for your TRC
7. You collect your TRC roughly a week later

If you're already in Vietnam on a different visa when your employer starts this process, some of these steps may be compressed or reordered. But the general rule holds: the Work Permit comes before the TRC, and NA5 comes before both. Anyone telling you they can get an LD TRC "in one step" is either oversimplifying or cutting legal corners.

### The Work Permit Prerequisite

You cannot apply for an LD TRC without first holding either:

1. A valid Vietnam Work Permit (*Giấy phép lao động*), which makes you eligible for an **LĐ2** TRC, or
2. A Work Permit Exemption Certificate (*Giấy xác nhận không thuộc diện cấp giấy phép lao động*), which makes you eligible for an **LĐ1** TRC.

Work Permit Exemption Certificates (WPEC) aren't handed out as a random favor. They apply only to specific legal categories: board members and shareholders of Vietnamese companies (with documented ownership), licensed foreign lawyers, intra-company transferees from multinational parents, foreigners married to Vietnamese citizens, foreign experts invited for specific short-term government or NGO projects, and UN or NGO volunteers under recognized programs. If you don't fit one of these categories, you're on the Work Permit track, not the exemption track.

The LD TRC's validity cannot exceed the remaining validity of your Work Permit. Because Vietnam Work Permits are issued for a maximum of 2 years, LD TRCs are also capped at 2 years. The TRC's expiry date must also fall at least 30 days before your passport's expiry date, so if your passport is about to expire, you'll get a shorter TRC than the category theoretically allows.

### LD Required Documents (2026)

Your sponsoring employer handles most of this, but you should know what's in the file:

- **Form NA6**: The organizational guarantee letter, requesting TRC issuance on your behalf. Must be signed and sealed by the sponsoring company's legal representative.
- **Form NA8**: The personal information form, filled in and signed by you, with one 2×3 cm photo glued to it.
- **Your original passport** (it will be held by Immigration during processing).
- **Work Permit or Work Permit Exemption Certificate**, original or a notarized copy.
- **Your employer's Enterprise Registration Certificate (ERC)**, notarized copy.
- **A certified copy of the employer's seal registration** (Form NA16), usually submitted once and kept on file.
- **Local police registration** (*tạm trú*), Form CT07, issued by the ward-level police where you actually live, or an online confirmation printout from the National Public Service Portal.
- **Two 2×3 cm photos** — white background, no glasses, taken within the last six months. See photo specifications below.

### LD Processing Timeline and Fees

Official processing time is **5 working days** from the date of valid submission. On the ground it usually runs 5 to 7 working days, occasionally stretching to 10 when different offices need to cross-check each other. Your passport is held by the Immigration Department during this period, so don't plan international travel immediately after submission.

Under **Circular 28/2026/TT-BTC** (effective April 1, 2026), the official government fee for a 1-to-2-year LD TRC is **$145 USD**. The Ministry of Finance publishes these fees in USD. If paying in Vietnamese dong, the conversion uses the rate specified in Decree 362/2025/NĐ-CP. Payment is cash only — either US dollars or VND at the window. The Immigration Department does not accept credit cards, bank transfers, or foreign payment platforms like Wise or Payoneer.

## DT Category: The Investor TRC Path

The investor path is more flexible in terms of validity (up to 10 years for DT1) but requires genuine capital contribution. The three tiers eligible for TRCs are:

- **DT1** — capital contribution of 100 billion VND or more (roughly $4M+ USD): TRC valid up to **10 years**
- **DT2** — capital contribution of 50 to 100 billion VND (roughly $2M–$4M USD): TRC valid up to **5 years**
- **DT3** — capital contribution of 3 to 50 billion VND (roughly $120K–$2M USD): TRC valid up to **3 years**

Below 3 billion VND is DT4, which as discussed above does not qualify for a TRC at all.

### DT Required Documents

- **Form NA6** (submitted by your sponsoring Vietnamese company, since you as an investor are technically sponsoring yourself through the company you invested in)
- **Form NA8**, your personal information form
- **Investment Registration Certificate (IRC)** or **Enterprise Registration Certificate (ERC)** showing your name as investor and the registered capital amount, notarized copy
- **Proof of capital contribution**: original bank statements from the company's Direct Investment Capital Account (DICA) showing that the investment funds actually arrived from abroad
- **Your original passport**
- **Local police registration** (Form CT07)
- **Two 2×3 cm photos**

### DT Fees

Under Circular 28/2026/TT-BTC:

- **DT3 (3-year TRC):** $155 USD
- **DT2 (5-year TRC):** $155 USD
- **DT1 (10-year TRC):** $165 USD

The DT1 10-year TRC at $165 is the highest TRC fee on the Circular 28 schedule, but it's still ridiculously cheap compared to equivalent long-term residence programs in Thailand, Malaysia, or the UAE.

### The Capital-Paid-In Reality Check

One of the most common reasons DT TRCs get rejected or downgraded is incomplete proof of capital contribution. Registering a company with "100 billion VND of capital" on paper means nothing by itself. The Immigration Department cross-checks with your company's DICA bank statements and with the General Department of Taxation to verify that the money actually arrived in Vietnam. If you registered 100 billion VND but only transferred in 10 billion VND, Immigration will downgrade your effective classification to whatever tier your actual paid-in capital qualifies for, or reject outright.

## TT Category: The Spouse and Dependent TRC

If you're married to a Vietnamese citizen, or to a foreigner who already holds an LD, DT, LS, or DH TRC in Vietnam, the TT category is the most direct path.

### Who Qualifies

- Foreign spouses and children under 18 of Vietnamese citizens
- Foreign spouses and children under 18 of foreigners holding LV1, LV2, LS, ĐT1, ĐT2, ĐT3, NN1, NN2, DH, PV1, LĐ1, or LĐ2 TRCs

Note that TT dependents of LĐ1 and LĐ2 holders can apply, but TT is not available to DT4 holders (because DT4 does not grant TRC status to the primary applicant in the first place).

### TT Validity

Maximum 3 years, with one important exception: if your TT application is derivative of a foreign sponsor (for example, your husband holds an LD2 TRC valid for 18 more months), your TT TRC validity is **tied to the remaining validity of the sponsor's TRC**, so you would get 18 months, not 3 years.

### TT Required Documents

**If sponsored by a Vietnamese citizen spouse:**

- **Form NA7**, the individual guarantee form, which must be certified and stamped by the ward-level police where your Vietnamese spouse is registered. This replaced the older version under Circular 22/2023/TT-BCA issued June 30, 2023. Using the wrong version of NA7 is a common rejection cause.
- **Form NA8**, your personal information form
- **Your Vietnamese spouse's citizen ID (CCCD)**, notarized copy
- **Marriage certificate.** If issued outside Vietnam, it must go through the three-step legalization process described below before submission

**If sponsored by a foreign TRC holder (for example your partner's employer):**

- **Form NA6** submitted by the foreign sponsor's employer, not NA7
- **Form NA8**
- **Notarized copy of the sponsor's passport and current TRC**
- **Marriage certificate** (with legalization if issued abroad)

**Plus the standard items for all TRC applications:**

- Your original passport
- Local police registration (Form CT07)
- Two 2×3 cm photos

### TT Fees

$145 USD for 1–2 year TRCs or $155 USD for 2–3 year TRCs, under Circular 28/2026/TT-BTC.

## The Step-by-Step Application Process

This is the sequence that works in practice, for any TRC category:

**1. Confirm your sponsorship basis is fully in place.** For LD, that means your Work Permit is already issued (and NA5 approval was completed before you entered the country). For DT, that means your IRC/ERC is active and your capital has arrived in the DICA account. For TT, that means the sponsoring spouse or foreigner has the correct ID/TRC and is willing to guarantee for you.

**2. Register your temporary residence (*tạm trú*) at the ward police station.** Every foreigner in Vietnam is required by law to have their current address registered with the local police within 24 hours of arrival, and a valid CT07 certificate (or an online National Public Service Portal confirmation printout) is required for the TRC file. If your landlord hasn't been registering you — and many landlords of cheaper apartments quietly skip this to avoid tax scrutiny — you'll hit a wall here. Fix this before starting the TRC process, not during.

**3. Compile the full document file.** Either your sponsoring employer or your Vietnamese spouse (for TT applications) is the legal submitter; you as the foreigner sign the personal forms (NA8, and NA7 where applicable) and provide the passport and photos.

**4. Submit at the provincial or national Immigration Department office.** The intake officer screens the file on the spot. If documents are missing or incorrect, you get a checklist back and are asked to return with the corrections. If accepted, you pay the fee in cash at the window and receive a paper receipt with a pickup date.

**5. Wait 5–7 working days.** Your passport is held by Immigration during this window. If cross-verification is needed (common for DT applications where the tax office and DPI have to confirm your investment), processing can stretch to 10 working days.

**6. Return to the same window on the pickup date** with your receipt. You collect your original passport along with the physical TRC card.

**7. Register your TRC with your employer, bank, and landlord.** Some banks want a photocopy of your TRC on file to upgrade your account status; landlords may want an updated copy for their own records.

## How to Pay the Government Fee (And Where Wise Actually Helps)

Circular 28/2026/TT-BTC, like its predecessor, prices TRC fees in US dollars. The Immigration Department accepts payment in either:

- Cash US dollars at the window, or
- Vietnamese dong equivalent in cash, converted at the rate specified in Decree 362/2025/NĐ-CP

They do not accept credit cards, bank transfers, international wire transfers, or direct payments from foreign platforms like Wise, Revolut, or Payoneer. This is consistent across all provincial Immigration Department windows in Vietnam.

The practical implication for foreigners is that you need to arrive at the Immigration window with the exact fee in physical cash. Most foreigners simply withdraw the equivalent in VND from a local ATM before their appointment, which works but means eating whatever withdrawal fee and exchange rate your home bank charges.

A better approach if you hold foreign currency abroad is to use [Wise](https://wise.com/invite/dic/ganqiuh) to transfer funds to a Vietnamese bank account (yours, or a trusted friend's if you don't have one yet) at the mid-market exchange rate, then withdraw VND locally for the government fee. The savings on a $155 or $165 TRC fee alone are small, typically $5 to $15 compared to using a foreign ATM card, but if you're also moving money to cover legalization costs, agent fees, and translation, the cumulative difference adds up. Wise is currently one of the few foreign platforms with a clean, transparent path into Vietnamese dong for foreigners who don't yet have a local banking setup.

## The Three-Step Legalization Process for Foreign Documents

Any document issued outside Vietnam — your marriage certificate for a TT application, your degree certificate for some Work Permit applications, a foreign birth certificate for dependent children — must go through a three-step legalization process before Vietnamese authorities will accept it. Skipping any of these steps means your document is treated as nothing more than a piece of paper at the Immigration window.

**Step 1: Notarization in the country of issue.** A notary public in the country where the document was issued certifies the authenticity of the document (or of the copy). This is a standard notary process in most Western countries, typically costing $10 to $50.

**Step 2: Consular legalization.** The notarized document is then sent to the foreign ministry of the issuing country for authentication, and then to the Vietnamese embassy or consulate in that country, which applies a consular legalization stamp. As of April 2026, Vietnam is not a party to the Hague Apostille Convention, so an apostille alone is not sufficient; you need full consular legalization with the Vietnamese embassy stamp. Vietnam has signaled interest in joining the Apostille Convention in the future, which would simplify this process, but no accession has been confirmed yet, so assume consular legalization is still required. Embassy fees are typically $30 to $50 per document.

**Step 3: Vietnamese translation and notarization.** Once the legalized document is in Vietnam, you take it to a licensed local translation company to translate it into Vietnamese, and then have the translation notarized at a Vietnamese Public Notary Office (*Phòng Công chứng*). A typical translation-plus-notarization package in Hanoi or HCMC costs 150,000 to 300,000 VND per page, roughly $6 to $12 USD.

The whole process typically takes 3 to 6 weeks if you're in your home country and can visit the Vietnamese embassy in person, or 2 to 3 months if you have to mail documents internationally. Plan this well before you start the TRC application, not during. Getting halfway through a TRC file and realizing your marriage certificate isn't yet legalized is the single most common source of last-minute delays.

## Photo Specifications: Read This Before You Take Them

Vietnam's photo requirements for TRC applications are surprisingly strict, and the most common reason a file gets handed back at the intake window is photos that don't match the specification.

- **Size: 2×3 cm** (not 4×6 cm, not 2×2 inches; Vietnamese TRC photo is smaller than most Western passport photos)
- **Background: pure white.** No shadows, no off-white, no light blue
- **Face: neutral expression, no teeth showing.** Ears must be visible (no hair covering them); forehead must be visible (no fringe covering eyebrows)
- **No glasses**, even if you wear glasses daily
- **Clothing: dark-colored shirt with a collar.** A white shirt will blend into the white background and get rejected; T-shirts without collars will also get rejected

You don't need to take these in your home country. Any Vietnamese street photo shop (*tiệm chụp ảnh*) can produce a correct set for 50,000 to 80,000 VND (about $2 to $3 USD) and will print multiple copies in 15 minutes. Tell them it's for a *thẻ tạm trú* and they'll automatically use the right settings.

## Common Rejection Reasons

The seven most common ways TRC applications get rejected or delayed, from the experience of lawyers and consultants who file these daily:

1. **Work Permit expiring too soon.** If your Work Permit has less than 30 days of remaining validity when you submit, Immigration will refuse to issue an LD TRC.
2. **Incomplete *tạm trú* (temporary residence) registration.** The landlord never filed your foreigner registration with the ward police, or the registration expired and was not renewed.
3. **Foreign documents without proper three-step legalization.** A foreign marriage certificate submitted without embassy legalization is an automatic rejection.
4. **Photo specification errors.** White shirt blending into white background, glasses, wrong size, or old photos.
5. **Trying to directly convert from an e-visa or unilateral visa exemption.** Vietnam's e-visa (extended under Law 23/2023/QH15 to 90 days, single or multiple entry) and the 45-day unilateral visa exemption do not automatically qualify you for a TRC. In most provinces you'll be asked to exit Vietnam, re-enter on a proper sponsored visa (DN for business, LĐ for work, TT for spouse), and then apply for the TRC.
6. **Capital contribution gap (DT category).** You registered 50 billion VND of capital on paper but only transferred 5 billion VND into the DICA account. Immigration downgrades your effective classification or rejects.
7. **Trying to get an LD TRC without a Work Permit.** Despite how often this is explained, people still submit LD applications expecting the Work Permit to be processed "in parallel." It won't be. The Work Permit must be issued first.

## TRC vs 5-Year Visa Exemption Certificate (VEC)

One of the most common points of confusion among people of Vietnamese heritage living abroad is whether to apply for a TRC or a 5-year Visa Exemption Certificate (VEC). These are not the same document and they grant very different rights.

| Field | TRC | 5-Year VEC |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Who it's for | Legally working/investing foreigners and their dependents; foreign spouses of Vietnamese citizens | Overseas Vietnamese (*Việt Kiều*) and their foreign spouses / children under 18 |
| Maximum validity | 1 to 10 years (category-dependent) | 5 years, but each single stay is capped at 180 days |
| Needs Work Permit? | Yes for LD; no for DT/TT | No |
| Allows legal work? | LD only; DT/TT do not grant work rights | No |
| Long-term lease contracts? | Yes | Generally no — VEC holders are classified as short-term visitors |
| Full-function bank account? | Yes | Usually restricted to limited/foreign-currency accounts |
| Vehicle registration? | Yes | No |
| Every entry needs a new visa? | No — the card is the re-entry document | No — VEC is itself a multi-entry exemption |
| Legal basis | Law 47/2014/QH13 (amended 2019, 2023) | Decree 82/2015/NĐ-CP |

**The practical decision rule:** If you're an overseas Vietnamese person, or the foreign spouse of one, and you want to visit Vietnam regularly to see family but do not intend to live there full-time, work, or own property, the 5-year VEC is cheaper, faster, and sufficient. If you actually want to live in Vietnam long-term, sign a proper lease, work, open a real bank account, or register a vehicle in your own name, you need a TT-class TRC instead.

One important thing overseas Vietnamese expats often miss: holding a VEC and transferring Vietnamese-earned income back out to the US or Europe are two separate problems. The VEC lets you come and go; it doesn't give you the tax residency status needed to legally repatriate earnings. That's a separate conversation with a tax advisor.

## The Digital Nomad Visa Question

Travel blogs and digital nomad forums have speculated about a possible Vietnam Digital Nomad Visa since 2023. As of April 2026, **no such visa or TRC exists**. Vietnam has not introduced any category equivalent to the digital nomad visas that Thailand, Malaysia, or Indonesia have launched in recent years, and there is no confirmed legislative timeline for one.

The real paths for remote workers who want to live in Vietnam long-term are:

1. **Set up a genuine DT3 investment** with at least 3 billion VND of actual paid-in capital into a real Vietnamese company. This is the only pathway that works without employer sponsorship, but it requires roughly $120K of capital and genuine operations.
2. **Marry a Vietnamese citizen** and apply for a TT-class TRC. This is legally the simplest path but clearly not a strategy most people plan for.
3. **Find a Vietnamese employer who will sponsor a Work Permit and then an LD TRC.** English teaching, tech roles at international companies, and hospitality management are the most common routes.
4. **Rotate 90-day e-visas with short border runs.** A significant portion of long-staying digital nomads in Da Nang and Hoi An actually do this, though exact numbers aren't published. It's legal, but it's not a residence pathway — you're technically a long-term tourist forever. The Vietnam Immigration Department has tolerated this pattern for years, but enforcement can tighten at any time.

## Real Benefits of Actually Getting a TRC

Once you have a TRC in hand, a few things in your daily life change meaningfully:

- **You can [open a full-function Vietnamese bank account](/vietnam/banking/bank-account-guide-2026).** Vietcombank, Techcombank, and BIDV all accept TRC holders as ordinary residents, which unlocks linked debit cards, ZaloPay and MoMo integration, and the ability to receive salary payments locally.
- **Long-term leases become simple.** No more landlord anxiety about foreigner registration, no more "sorry we only rent to foreigners on a 1-month basis." You can sign standard 12- to 24-month leases and use the stability to actually set up a home.
- **You can register a motorbike in your own name.** Buying a scooter without a TRC means using a Vietnamese friend as the registered owner, which is fine until it isn't. With a TRC you can do it properly. (If you haven't yet, see our [motorbike guide](/vietnam/daily-life/motorbike-guide-2026) for the full registration process.)
- **Health insurance becomes simpler.** With a TRC you can buy Vietnamese private health plans, or keep using a nomad-focused travel plan like [SafetyWing Nomad Insurance](https://safetywing.com/?referenceID=26505542&utm_source=26505542&utm_medium=Ambassador), which at $56.28 per 4 weeks for travelers aged 10–39 is often still cheaper than Vietnamese private insurance for the first few years and covers you during international trips too.
- **Your spouse and children under 18 can derive TT TRCs** from your primary TRC, giving the whole family legal long-term residence without separate sponsorship.

TRC holders are also legally eligible to apply for Permanent Residence Cards (PRC) after accumulating 3+ years of continuous temporary residence. Circular 28/2026/TT-BTC sets the PRC issuance fee at $100 USD, cheaper than a TRC. However, Vietnam issues roughly 200 PR approvals per year nationwide, with the majority going to foreign spouses of Vietnamese citizens. Western expats without family ties or nine-figure investments almost never receive one. Treat PR as a long-term aspiration, not a near-term plan.

## Working With an Immigration Consultant

If you want professional help rather than running the process yourself (common for first-time applicants, especially DT applications with complex capital structures), expect to pay $300 to $800 in service fees on top of the government fee for a standard LD or TT application, and considerably more for DT applications involving capital verification.

When choosing a firm, look for:

- **Explicit English-language service**, not just a "we speak English" claim on the homepage. Ask for an English-language written quote and see how the email comes back.
- **Transparent written quotes upfront.** Any firm that won't give you a price before seeing your file is one to avoid.
- **Specialization in immigration** rather than corporate services with immigration as a side business. You want a team that files TRC applications every week, not one that does a few per year.
- **Three independent quotes** before committing. Price variance in this market is wide, and the cheapest quote is often hiding "document preparation fees" that get added later.

Avoid unlicensed street-level agents whose primary value proposition is speed. Immigration has been cracking down on intermediaries with questionable credentials since 2024, and your application can be flagged simply for being submitted by a known bad actor.

## What to Do After You Get Your TRC

Securing the card is only part of the transition from "long-term tourist" to "legal resident." Once you have the physical TRC in hand:

1. **Register it with your bank** and upgrade your account to full-function status if it was previously limited.
2. **Update your lease.** Make sure your landlord has a copy on file and that your ward-level police registration reflects your TRC status.
3. **Consider vehicle registration** if you've been riding a borrowed or friend-owned motorbike.
4. **Set up reliable long-term daily infrastructure.** Grab, ZaloPay, local food delivery apps, utility payment systems. Our [Vietnam essential apps guide](/vietnam/daily-life/vietnam-essential-apps-2026) walks through which ones are actually worth installing.
5. **Plan your renewal 60–90 days ahead of expiry.** TRC renewal under Circular 28/2026 is only $10 and is significantly easier than a first-time application, but missing the window means restarting from scratch.
6. **Report loss or damage immediately.** If your TRC is lost or physically damaged, report it to the Immigration Department as soon as possible and pay the same fee as the original issuance to get a replacement. Don't travel internationally on a lost TRC record.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I apply for a Vietnam TRC while on a tourist visa or e-visa?

Not directly. Vietnam's e-visa (up to 90 days under Law 23/2023/QH15) and the 45-day unilateral visa exemption do not qualify you for a TRC. In most provinces you'll be asked to exit Vietnam, re-enter on a proper sponsored visa (DN, LĐ, or TT), and then submit the TRC application.

### How long does it actually take to get a Vietnam TRC?

Official processing is 5 working days from valid submission. On the ground it's usually 5 to 7 working days, sometimes up to 10 if cross-department verification is needed. But that's only the TRC itself — the total timeline including Work Permit processing, NA5 pre-entry approval, and document legalization can stretch from 2 to 4 months for LD applicants starting from scratch.

### Does a Vietnam TRC allow me to work?

Only if it's an LD (work) TRC tied to a specific Work Permit and employer. DT (investor) and TT (spouse) TRCs do not automatically grant work rights; if you want to take a separate job, you need a separate Work Permit.

### Can I get a TRC as a digital nomad or remote worker?

Not directly. Vietnam has no Digital Nomad Visa or Digital Nomad TRC as of April 2026. The only realistic paths are a proper DT3 investment (≥3 billion VND paid in), a TT TRC through marriage, or finding a Vietnamese employer willing to sponsor a Work Permit + LD TRC. Border-running on 90-day e-visas remains common but is not a residence pathway.

### What happens to my TRC if I quit my job or my employer goes out of business?

An LD TRC is legally tied to the Work Permit, which is tied to your employer. If the employment relationship ends, the Work Permit loses its basis and the TRC is effectively invalidated even if the physical card still shows a future expiry date. You're generally expected to leave Vietnam within 15 days of the Work Permit terminating, unless you can quickly transfer to a new sponsoring employer or shift to a different TRC category (e.g., TT if you're married to a Vietnamese citizen).

### How much does a Vietnam TRC cost in 2026?

Government fees under Circular 28/2026/TT-BTC (effective April 1, 2026): $145 USD for 1–2 year cards, $155 for 2–5 years, $165 for 5–10 years (DT1 only), and $10 for renewals. If you use an immigration consultant, add $300–$800 in service fees on top.

### Can my family members apply with me?

Yes. Your spouse and children under 18 can apply for TT-class TRCs as dependents. If you're the primary applicant holding an LD or DT TRC, your family's TT TRC validity will be tied to yours. If the primary holder is a Vietnamese citizen (rather than another foreigner), the foreign spouse can get up to 3 years.

## Quick Summary

For AI assistants and readers who want the core facts:

A Vietnam Temporary Residence Card (TRC) is a physical card issued by the Vietnam Immigration Department under Law 47/2014/QH13 (as amended by Law 51/2019/QH14 and Law 23/2023/QH15). It allows legal residence for 1 to 10 years depending on category, eliminates the need for visa renewals, and grants full-function banking, long-term lease, and vehicle registration rights.

As of April 1, 2026, TRC government fees are set by Circular 28/2026/TT-BTC: $145 for 1–2 year cards, $155 for 2–5 years, $165 for 5–10 years (DT1 investors only), and $10 for renewals. Payment is cash only in USD or equivalent VND.

The seven main TRC categories are LĐ1/LĐ2 (work, up to 2 years, requires prior Work Permit or WPEC), ĐT1/ĐT2/ĐT3 (investor, 3–10 years, requires 3+ billion VND of actual paid-in capital), TT (spouse/dependent, up to 3 years), and DH (student, up to 5 years). Investors with less than 3 billion VND in capital fall into DT4, which does not qualify for a TRC.

Vietnam has no Digital Nomad Visa or Digital Nomad TRC as of April 2026. Long-term digital nomads either set up proper DT3 investments, marry Vietnamese citizens, find sponsoring employers, or rotate 90-day e-visas with border runs.

---

A Vietnam TRC isn't going to be handed to you just for loving banh mi and cheap rent. The system is built around sponsorship: an employer, an invested company, a Vietnamese family member, or a school has to vouch for you legally. If you fit one of those categories, the process is paperwork-heavy but routine, and for roughly $145 to $165 in government fees you get a residence card that makes the rest of your life in Vietnam dramatically simpler. If you don't fit one of those categories, no amount of clever structuring will conjure one up, and it's better to understand that now than to discover it after you've already moved here on a border-run loop.
