This is not a cost-of-living guide, and it is not a neighborhoods guide. If you want to know what rent costs or which districts to live in, those questions are answered better in dedicated articles — see [Cost of Living in Vietnam](/vietnam/daily-life/cost-of-living-vietnam-2026) and the neighborhoods guides for [Hanoi](/vietnam/housing/best-neighborhoods-hanoi-expats-2026), [HCMC](/vietnam/housing/best-neighborhoods-hcmc-expats-2026), and [Da Nang](/vietnam/housing/best-neighborhoods-danang-expats-2026).

This guide answers a narrower question: as a remote worker, which of Vietnam's three main cities gives you the best work setup? That means internet that holds up under pressure, enough coworking and café options that you can actually rotate, a time zone that works for your clients, a visa pathway that does not break every 90 days, and a nomad community dense enough that you can find people without trying too hard.

The short version is below. The rest of the article is the substance behind it.

**Quick Take**
- **Saigon (HCMC):** Most mature coworking infrastructure, most stable power and internet, best for nomads with Asia-Pacific clients or who travel often for work.
- **Hanoi:** Strong fixed broadband and growing coworking scene, ideal time zone overlap with European clients, but August 2025 made the summer power risk real again.
- **Da Nang:** Highest concentration of nomad-specific cafés and coworking per capita, best community density, best for async work and self-directed product builders who do not need frequent international travel.

## Internet Reality Check

Vietnam's national internet has improved dramatically. Vietnam's average fixed Internet speed in August 2025 reached 261.8 Mbps, rising sharply from 33rd place in March 2025, and as of September 2025, Vietnam ranks 13th globally for average mobile download speed and 11th for fixed broadband speed. That national-level number is the headline, but it does not tell you what your apartment Wi-Fi will actually do at 9 PM on a Tuesday. Three things matter more than the rank.

**Fixed broadband is reliably fast in all three cities.** A standard residential fiber package from Viettel, VNPT, or FPT in Hanoi, Saigon, or Da Nang will give you speeds in the 200–400 Mbps range. Viettel raised its minimum broadband speed for customers from 150 Mbps to 300 Mbps in April 2025, and the other major providers followed. For anything short of professional video editing on a remote server, this is more than enough. If you are picking between cities based on raw download speed alone, there is no meaningful difference.

**Mobile data is excellent and matters more than people expect.** Mobile download speeds in Vietnam nearly doubled in the past year, reaching 102.29 Mbps in the first half of 2025. Average 5G speeds hit 428.93 Mbps. As of Q2 2025, 5G covered 26% of the population, with strong coverage in the central areas of all three cities. For nomads, this means a 4G or 5G hotspot is a viable backup when your apartment fiber goes down — and it will go down, because something always goes down.

**The submarine cable problem is the one infrastructure issue that affects all three cities equally.** Vietnam's international internet traffic depends on a small number of undersea cables, and they break often. Vietnam operates five major international submarine cable systems: AAG, AAE-1, APG, SMW3, and IA. However, the submarine cable systems face significant challenges, averaging 15 incidents annually. Before 2022, repairs typically took 1-2 months per incident. Recently, repair times have extended further. The most recent major event was in early 2025: the international APG undersea cable, which suffered a breakdown on February 3, 2025, was restored just ahead of the April 30 holiday — meaning roughly three months of degraded international routing. The good news is that in April 2025, the Asia Direct Cable (ADC) — Vietnam's highest-capacity undersea cable — officially went into operation, with a maximum capacity of 50 Tbps, equal to 125% of the total combined capacity of Vietnam's existing five active cables. ADC should reduce the severity of future incidents, but the underlying pattern of periodic international slowdowns is not going away in 2026.

What this means in practice: domestic-routed traffic (Vietnamese websites, regional CDNs, anything cached on Cloudflare or AWS Singapore) is fast and consistent. International traffic to US-based services can become noticeably slower for days or weeks at a time when a cable is down, with VPN connections and large file uploads taking the biggest hit. This affects all three cities — the cables come ashore at landing stations in Da Nang, Quy Nhon, and Vung Tau, and from there the traffic feeds the entire country.

**Power stability is where the cities diverge.** Saigon has the most stable grid of the three. Hanoi's August 2025 was a wake-up call: a record heatwave drove Hanoi's power demand to a peak of 5,992 MW on Monday, nearly 14% higher than the previous record set in 2024, and a fault in the 220kV Hoa Binh–Ha Dong transmission line forced load shedding, cutting electricity in some areas to prevent a system-wide failure. Outages affected residential towers across the city, including in Tay Ho. If you are committing to a Hanoi summer, budget for either a UPS for your work setup or a co-working space membership as a fallback. Da Nang's risk is different and seasonal: typhoon season from September through November regularly brings multi-hour outages and sometimes multi-day disruptions to specific neighborhoods.

*Source: Ookla Speedtest Global Index, EVN Hanoi, Vietnam Ministry of Science and Technology (2025)*

## Coworking and Café Infrastructure

Raw internet speed only matters if you have somewhere good to use it. This is where the three cities are most different from each other, and where the differences matter most for remote work.

**Saigon has the most mature coworking market in Vietnam.** International chains and large local operators have been here longest. WeWork operates in Lim Tower 3 in District 1. The Hive runs three locations — one in District 1 and two in Thao Dien — with a global membership network that lets you work from Hive locations across Asia. Dreamplex has multiple locations including its flagship in District 1, which it has operated since 2015. Toong has multiple Saigon branches. CirCO and The Executive Centre fill out the higher end. Most of these operate on monthly memberships with day-pass options, and a hot desk runs roughly 1.5–3 million VND per month depending on the brand and location. The practical implication: in Saigon you can probably find a coworking space within a 10-minute Grab ride of wherever you live, with 24/7 access available at the larger chains.

**Hanoi has a smaller but credible coworking scene concentrated in two clusters.** Toong is the dominant local chain with multiple locations across the city, including a Tay Ho branch tied to the SOHO Heritage West Lake development. COGO operates at Viet Tower in Dong Da district and is one of the few Hanoi spaces with 24/7 access. Dreamplex has expanded into Hanoi as well. Beyond these chains, the Hanoi scene leans more heavily on café work than Saigon does — partly because the chains are fewer, and partly because Hanoi café culture is well-suited to long laptop sessions if you pick the right spots. Xofa Café & Bistro is one of the few 24/7 cafés popular with nomads on non-Asia time zones. The two main expat clusters for coworking are around Tay Ho (West Lake) and the central Hoan Kiem / Old Quarter area.

**Da Nang has the highest density of nomad-specific work infrastructure per capita.** This is the surprise finding of 2024–2025. The An Thuong neighborhood, where most foreign remote workers cluster, has more coworking spaces per square kilometer than either Hanoi or Saigon, plus an unusually high concentration of cafés that are explicitly designed for laptop work rather than tourism. The current notable spaces include Coworking Danang (run by Tiffany and Anton, with an active community programme of weekly events), Hana's Coworking and Seaview Coworking (both run by Hana, Seaview is on the 9th floor of Waikiki Beach Hotel in Son Tra with 24/7 access), Enouvo (now operating as Enosta Space, an established brand with multiple amenities), Hi4 Workspace (a 24/7 coworking-café hybrid that can fit over 100 people and charges by food and drink purchases rather than membership fees), DNC, Nomad Space, and Space A. Several of these opened in early 2025. The cafés are different in character from Hanoi's: unlike neighbouring Hoi An and the capital Hanoi, where cafes are more geared towards tourism, the cafes in Da Nang are often targeted at the huge community of digital nomads, with reliable Wi-Fi, plenty of plugs, and staff who do not flinch when you sit for four hours.

The trade-off: Da Nang's coworking spaces are smaller than Saigon's chains, and turnover is high. A space that was the favorite six months ago may have moved or rebranded. Always check current operating status before committing to a monthly membership.

## Time Zone and Async Work Reality

Vietnam runs on UTC+7 year-round, with no daylight saving time. This single fact determines more about whether Vietnam works for your remote job than any other variable on this page, and most digital nomad guides skip it entirely.

**For European clients:** Vietnam is genuinely good. UTC+7 puts you 5–6 hours ahead of Central European Time in winter, 4–5 hours ahead in summer. A 9 AM start in Berlin is your 3 PM, and a 5 PM end in Berlin is your 11 PM. That gives you a comfortable 4–5 hour overlap window without working antisocial hours, and your morning is fully your own for deep work before European clients come online. This is the sweet spot for Vietnam as a remote work base, and it is the main reason European nomads — especially from Germany, the Nordics, and France — disproportionately base themselves here.

**For UK clients:** Slightly more constrained but still workable. UTC+7 is 7 hours ahead of London in winter, 6 hours in summer. Your overlap window is 3–4 hours in the late afternoon and early evening Vietnam time. Manageable for client calls and async-heavy collaboration, painful if your job requires constant real-time interaction with London-based teammates.

**For US East Coast clients:** Difficult. UTC+7 is 12 hours ahead of New York in winter, 11 hours in summer. Your only meaningful overlap is roughly 8 PM to midnight Vietnam time, which corresponds to 9 AM to 1 PM in New York. This works for one or two evening calls per week, but if your job requires daily synchronous collaboration with US East Coast colleagues, expect to live a nocturnal schedule. Many people try this and burn out within three months.

**For US West Coast clients:** Effectively no overlap at all. UTC+7 is 15 hours ahead of San Francisco in winter, 14 hours in summer. The only way Vietnam works for US West Coast remote work is if your role is fully asynchronous — you handle tickets, ship code, write documentation, and your colleagues read it when they wake up. For most W-2 employees of US West Coast companies, this is not realistic. For independent contractors, freelancers, and product builders with no fixed meeting schedule, it can work very well.

**For Australia, Singapore, Hong Kong, and other Asia-Pacific clients:** Vietnam is essentially same-zone or one hour off. This is the best fit and the most underrated reason to base yourself here.

The honest summary: Vietnam works best for self-directed product builders, freelancers serving European clients, async-friendly contract workers, and anyone whose income comes from Asia-Pacific. It works worst for full-time employees of US companies with mandatory synchronous meetings.

## Visa Reality for Nomads

There is one thing every "Vietnam digital nomad" search result keeps trying to bury, so it is worth saying clearly: **Vietnam does not have a digital nomad visa, and as of April 2026 there is no firm timeline for one**. Multiple recent industry sources confirm the current status. As of January 2026, Vietnam does not offer a dedicated digital nomad visa or remote work visa. Currently, nomads use e-visa (up to 90 days), or if you are from an eligible country, you can visit Vietnam visa-free. The government has discussed a remote work visa category — the Ministry of Public Security circulated proposals in late 2025 — but no launch date has been announced.

There is one piece of recent news that has caused confusion. In August 2025, Vietnam enacted Decree 221/2025/ND-CP, which established a Special Visa Exemption Card — sometimes marketed as a "5-year talent visa." Several news outlets framed this as a digital nomad win. It is not. The talent visa is targeted towards top professionals in specific fields, such as academics, experts, scientists, and professors from universities and research institutes, and invited guests of recognised research institutes, universities, and large enterprises. If you are a regular freelancer or remote employee, you do not qualify. The 10-year "Golden Visa" that was proposed by the Tourism Advisory Board in April 2025 is also still just a proposal — no legislation, no application process, no timeline.

What this leaves you with, in practice, is four real pathways:

The **multiple-entry e-visa** is what the vast majority of remote workers in Vietnam actually use. It costs $50, lasts up to 90 days, allows multiple entries, and you apply at the official portal at evisa.gov.vn before arrival. When it expires, you fly out and back in with a new one. This is the visa run model, and most nomads run it 3–4 times per year.

The **business visa (DN visa)** requires a Vietnamese sponsoring company and gets you up to 12 months. This is more paperwork and usually involves a visa agent, but it removes the every-90-day flying requirement.

The **investor visa (DT visa)** requires you to register a Vietnamese company and meet capital thresholds. Setup costs typically run several thousand dollars. For nomads planning to stay multi-year, this can be more economical than perpetual visa runs.

The **Temporary Residence Card (TRC)** is the long-term endgame for nomads who commit to Vietnam. It is granted on top of an existing long-term visa (typically the DN or DT) and gives you 1–10 years of residence depending on category. The full process and the recent Circular 28/2026 fee structure are covered in [TRC Application Guide](/vietnam/visa/trc-application-guide-2026), and the full visa pathway breakdown is in [Vietnam Visa Guide](/vietnam/visa/visa-guide-2026).

One important caveat that does not get discussed enough: working remotely for foreign clients on a tourist e-visa exists in a legal gray area. It is not explicitly permitted, but enforcement against nomads doing this has been historically rare. That said, Vietnam raised overstay fines significantly in 2026, and the broader enforcement direction is tightening rather than loosening. Da Nang in particular is known for more active foreigner documentation checks because of its high tourist throughput. Treat the gray area as gray, not green.

## Nomad Community Density

Community density is the variable that decides whether your first month feels lonely or feels like a soft landing. It is also the hardest thing to measure objectively, so what follows is based on consistent patterns across community surveys, Facebook group sizes, and on-the-ground reports from 2024–2025.

**Da Nang has the densest and most accessible nomad community of the three cities.** This is partly geography — almost everyone clusters in or near the An Thuong neighborhood, so you literally bump into the same people at the same cafés and beaches — and partly because the spaces themselves actively build community. Coworking Danang runs weekly Monday meetings where members plan group activities together (shared meals, pickleball, frisbee on the beach). Several other Da Nang spaces run regular meetups, language exchanges, and skill shares. The Da Nang Digital Nomads Facebook group is the most active of any Vietnamese city. The trade-off, as with the coworking scene, is turnover: most people stay one to three months, so you are constantly making new friends and saying goodbye to old ones. It is a community that is easy to join and harder to keep.

**Hanoi has a smaller but more long-term-stable community.** The expat scene here skews toward people who came for jobs (English teachers, NGO workers, embassy staff) and stayed, with a smaller but growing layer of pure digital nomads. The community is concentrated around Tay Ho and is less centralized than Da Nang's An Thuong scene — you have to make more deliberate effort to find people. The upside: friendships in Hanoi tend to last longer because the people you meet are more likely to still be there in a year.

**Saigon has the largest absolute number of foreign remote workers but the lowest community density per square kilometer.** The city is too big for a single neighborhood scene to dominate the way An Thuong does in Da Nang. Thao Dien is the closest equivalent to a nomad cluster, but the broader expat community in Saigon is fragmented across professions, neighborhoods, and interest groups. You will find more people doing more things at any given moment, but you will work harder to connect with them. Saigon is best for nomads who arrive with an existing professional network or who are extroverted enough to do their own community-building.

If raw social access is your top criterion, Da Nang wins clearly. If you want a community that is more diverse in profession and longer in horizon, Hanoi or Saigon will serve you better.

## Verdict by Work Profile

The standard "which city is best" question is the wrong question for remote work. The right question is which city fits which work profile.

**Choose Saigon if:** You serve clients in Asia-Pacific time zones, you travel internationally for work more than once per quarter, you need enterprise-grade infrastructure and the most professional coworking environment, or you want maximum optionality in food, transport, and after-hours activity. Saigon is the most expensive of the three but the most forgiving of edge cases.

**Choose Hanoi if:** You work primarily with European clients and want the best time zone overlap of any Vietnamese city, you value cultural depth over convenience, you can budget for a serious air purifier and dehumidifier setup for the December-to-April months (Hanoi's winter air pollution and Mùa Nồm humidity season are real and documented), and you can absorb the August power-grid risk that the 2025 heatwave revealed. Hanoi rewards nomads who go deep on one place rather than collecting cities.

**Choose Da Nang if:** You build your own products or run async-friendly contract work, you do not need frequent international travel, you want the densest nomad community in Vietnam, and you can plan your year around typhoon season (September through November will eat at least a few work days per year in lost connectivity and power). Da Nang is the easiest soft landing for first-time nomads in Vietnam, and the most sustainable long-term base for anyone whose work does not require frequent international flights.

The cities are not interchangeable, and the right answer depends entirely on what your work actually looks like — not on what the cost-of-living tables say.
