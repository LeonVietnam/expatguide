## The short answer

For most renters in Vietnam, the safest order is not brand first. It is contract and approval first, then cleaning and inspection, then repair, and only then replacement.

A good default looks like this:

- if the apartment already came with an AC, check whether it is landlord-supplied and whether the lease says anything about repairs or replacements
- if the unit is dirty, smelly, leaking lightly from a clogged drain, or just cooling worse than expected, clean and inspect it before talking about a full replacement
- if the AC runs for long hours most days and a replacement is actually allowed, inverter is usually the better buy for a main bedroom or living room
- if the room is only cooled occasionally, or the lease is short, spending heavily on a new split unit often makes less sense

*Public retail, service, and EVN guidance reviewed in April 2026. Prices below are planning ranges based on visible public references, not fixed national rates, and can move by city, promotion, pipe length, access difficulty, installer, and unit condition.*

## Start with the rental question, not the brand question

In Vietnam rentals, the first practical question is often not “Which AC should go in?” but “Who controls this decision?”

This is mainly a lease, landlord-approval, and property-alteration issue rather than a special AC-specific rule. In practice, many apartment AC decisions are handled through the lease, the landlord, the agent, or sometimes building management rather than through any special formal process. That means routine cleaning is one thing, but replacing a wall-mounted split unit, moving the outdoor unit, or drilling a new run should usually be agreed first.

That matters for cost as well. If the unit came with the apartment, the landlord may treat a failed AC as a repair item. If the current unit still works but the tenant wants a quieter or cheaper-to-run model, the landlord may treat that as an upgrade instead.

## When inverter is worth paying more for

Inverter is usually the better fit when the AC runs for long stretches, especially in Vietnam’s hotter months.

A renter should lean toward inverter when most of these are true:

- the AC runs for many hours most days
- the room is a main bedroom or living room, not a spare room
- the electricity bill is paid directly, or at least rebilled in a way the tenant can actually see
- the unit will stay in use long enough for lower running cost to matter
- the room gets strong afternoon sun, sits on a hot upper floor, or stays occupied through the day

A non-inverter unit can still make sense when usage is lighter and the real priority is lower upfront cost. That usually means a guest room, a short lease, or a landlord who only agrees to the cheapest acceptable replacement.

Before comparing brands, size the room first. A simple starting point is:

- around 1 HP for smaller rooms under about 15m²
- around 1.5 HP for many rooms around 15-20m²

That is only a starting point. Hot top-floor rooms, west-facing windows, high ceilings, and long daytime use can push the real need higher.

## Daikin vs Panasonic vs LG: how current public pricing reads

Visible April 2026 public retail listings for common 1 HP inverter models do show a useful pattern, even though promotions and sales channels can move the gap:

- LG often shows up around roughly 8-10 million VND
- Daikin often appears around roughly 10.5-12 million VND
- Panasonic often appears around roughly 12-14.5 million VND

That does **not** mean one brand is automatically the best choice for every renter, or that these are nationwide averages.

A more useful reading is:

- LG often gives the easier lower-upfront entry into inverter
- Daikin often sits in the middle of the visible public price spread for common 1 HP models
- Panasonic often asks a higher upfront price on current public listings, so the gap needs to make sense for the room, usage, and who is actually paying

All three brands have official Vietnam support paths, which matters more once the unit is new, still in warranty, or showing an error instead of just looking dirty.

If the landlord is paying, a higher-priced mainstream brand can be easier to accept. If the tenant is paying and the lease is not long, the cheaper decent inverter option often becomes more attractive than chasing a premium feature list.

## What installation usually costs, and why quotes jump

The first installation number is often not the real number.

Visible public installation references for common wall-mounted split units still separate labor from materials. For a typical 1 HP to 1.5 HP wall-mounted unit, labor often starts around a few hundred thousand VND before add-ons, depending on the installer and the job. After that, the total moves based on the actual site conditions.

The main extra lines are usually:

- copper pipe: roughly 100,000-250,000 VND per meter
- power cable: roughly 15,000-30,000 VND per meter
- drain line: roughly 10,000-25,000 VND per meter
- outdoor-unit bracket: roughly 50,000-150,000 VND per set
- extra gas: charged separately when genuinely needed

That is why a short, easy replacement using an existing pipe run can look reasonable, while a new install with a long pipe run or awkward outdoor-unit access can rise much faster.

For renters, this creates one simple rule: do not approve the job based only on the labor number. Ask what is being reused, what is being replaced, how many meters of pipe and wiring are included, and whether any drilling or outdoor-unit relocation needs owner approval.

## How often an apartment AC should be cleaned in Vietnam

For normal apartment use, a practical Vietnam apartment-use schedule is:

- wash or vacuum the basic filters every 2-4 weeks if the unit runs heavily
- arrange a professional clean about every 3-4 months for heavy daily use
- stretch that to around every 6 months for lighter use

Shorten the interval if the apartment collects road dust, has pets, gets kitchen grease into the room air, or starts smelling stale sooner than expected.

Visible public service-platform pricing for wall-mounted household units commonly lands around roughly 160,000-200,000 VND per unit, with lower per-unit pricing when several machines are booked together. City, quantity booked, and unit type can move that. Cassette or commercial-style units are often much more expensive.

Where to start:

- for in-warranty or newer branded units, check the official Daikin, Panasonic, or LG Vietnam service channel first
- for a simple out-of-warranty clean, large public service platforms such as Thợ Điện máy XANH give an easy benchmark for pricing and booking flow
- local technicians can still be the practical choice for basic cleaning, but the quote and scope should be clear before work starts

## Electricity-saving settings that matter more than marketing

A sensible EVN-style baseline is still a good one: start around 26-27°C and use a fan, then adjust for the room and comfort.

That usually works better than setting the AC very low and hoping the room cools faster. It also reduces the “too cold, then off, then too hot” cycle that makes a room feel worse instead of better.

The settings and habits that matter most are usually these:

- set the AC around 26-27°C first, then adjust from there
- pair it with a standing or ceiling fan
- keep doors and windows shut while cooling
- close curtains in rooms that take strong afternoon sun
- keep filters clean so airflow does not drop
- use timer or sleep mode at night when that actually matches the room
- if the room never reaches a comfortable temperature, check sizing, dirt buildup, and leakage before pushing the set temperature lower and lower

If electricity is rebilled by the landlord, a dirty or badly sized AC can hide inside a monthly total that already feels high. For city-wide budget context, see our [cost of living guide](/vietnam/daily-life/cost-of-living-vietnam-2026).

## AC red flags: bad unit signs and bad technician signs

The unit itself needs attention when there is:

- weak cooling that does not improve after cleaning
- a moldy or sour smell
- indoor dripping or repeated drain blockage
- unusual noise or vibration
- icing, repeated shutdowns, or an unexplained jump in power use without a clear change in hours used

Those signs do not all mean the same fault. They mean the unit needs inspection.

The technician or quote needs caution when:

- the problem is diagnosed before the unit is even checked
- the quote is a lump sum with no breakdown for materials
- extra pipe, wiring, or gas charges appear with no explanation
- the job would change the wall, drainage path, or outdoor-unit position but nobody has discussed landlord approval
- the suggested fix jumps directly to replacement even though the unit has not had a basic clean and inspection first

Treat those as warning signs, not automatic proof of a scam. The point is to slow the decision down before money is committed.

## What to do in the three most common renter situations

### 1) The apartment-supplied AC is dirty but probably usable

Start with cleaning and inspection, not replacement. If the lease implies the AC was part of the apartment handover, ask the landlord first. A basic clean is usually the cheapest way to find out whether the problem is dirt, drainage, or a deeper fault.

### 2) The current unit is old, weak, and used every day

If owner approval is clear and the stay is long enough, this is the case where inverter replacement makes the most sense. Size the room first, then compare current public price bands instead of buying by brand reputation alone.

### 3) The lease is short, approval is weak, or the landlord is non-committal

Do not rush into paying for a full split-unit replacement that mainly benefits the property. Push for repair first. If that stalls, a fan, dehumidifier, or a modest cleaning spend may be the safer short-stay move. Portable ACs exist, but in Vietnam rentals they are usually a fallback rather than the first answer.

## A simple renter rule for apartment AC in Vietnam

If the unit came with the apartment, treat it as a housing decision before treating it as an appliance purchase.

If the room is used heavily and replacement is genuinely on the table, inverter usually wins.

If the current unit has not been cleaned or inspected, do that first.

If the quote looks cheap at the top but vague underneath, stop and ask what is actually included.
