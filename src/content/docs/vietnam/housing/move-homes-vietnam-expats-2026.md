## The short answer

Most expats in Vietnam do not need the same moving setup.

- For one room, a studio, or a controlled partial move, a hired xe tải or an app truck with extra labor is usually the cheapest answer.
- For a furnished apartment with fragile items, tight building rules, or no time to supervise packing, a full-service mover is usually worth the extra money.
- For a Hanoi-to-HCMC move, compare the quote against replacement cost before committing. Cheaper furniture often makes more sense to sell and rebuy.
- For leaving Vietnam, parcel carriers are usually the better fit for documents and selected boxes. Full international household relocation only makes sense for higher-value goods, employer-paid moves, or items that are hard to replace.
- If move-out and move-in dates do not match, short-term storage can save a rushed handover.

*Source note: public mover pages, app-truck pricing, carrier tools, and storage pages were checked in April 2026. All prices below are approximate and can change by city, distance, floor count, elevator access, waiting time, packing level, and destination customs.*

## Same-city full-service movers: best when friction matters more than the lowest price

A full-service mover is the safer pick when the move includes a real amount of furniture, fragile electronics, or a building that will punish mistakes.

This option usually makes sense for:

- furnished one-bedroom and two-bedroom apartments
- high-rises with service-elevator booking rules
- moves with beds, wardrobes, desks, TVs, or glass items
- renters who need packing, loading, transport, and room placement handled in one scope

Public same-city price pages still show useful planning anchors even if no single Vietnam-wide tariff exists. Hanoi mover pages currently show small-truck opening fees around **250,000-550,000 VND**, then **13,000-32,000 VND/km**, with waiting often around **80,000-150,000 VND/hour**. Public labor references on the same style of pages are often around **200,000-400,000 VND per worker**. In HCMC, current partial-service mover pages also still show truck-only starting points in the mid-hundreds of thousands of VND, with examples around **400,000-600,000 VND** for common truck sizes and labor often added separately at roughly **400,000-500,000 VND per worker**.

That means a small but real same-city apartment move can start below **1 million VND** if the scope is controlled, but a fuller apartment move with packing, disassembly, building friction, or longer distance can move into the low millions quickly. On Ahamove’s currently published trọn gói move packages, the under-5km opening price is already around **2.36 million VND** for the standard package and around **3.30 million VND** for the larger one before extra distance.

Where to start:

- larger corporate-style mover: Crown quotes local and domestic moves from HCMC, Hanoi, and Da Nang
- city mover pages: local Hanoi and HCMC movers usually quote by Zalo, hotline, or site form
- app-linked moving-package option: Ahamove has a published move page, but the service scope is still narrower than a white-glove relocation company

The practical trap is not the truck. It is the building. Service elevators, loading bays, carry distance from the parking point, and move-hour windows can change both cost and timing. A quote that looks cheap on paper can stop being cheap when the crew has to wait, carry farther than planned, or work around a strict condo schedule.

## DIY truck or app truck plus extra labor: the budget answer when the move is controlled

This is the best fit for:

- room moves
- studio apartments
- partial moves
- furniture-only jobs
- renters who can pack on their own and supervise the loading

Ahamove’s public truck pages currently show vehicle options from **500kg to 2 tons**. On its current truck page, the opening price runs from roughly **117,818 VND for the first 2km** on a 500kg truck up to about **245,455 VND** on a 2-ton truck, with per-km rates increasing by vehicle size. If using older-style local mover truck tariffs instead of an app truck, public Hanoi and HCMC reference pages still sit around the mid-hundreds of thousands of VND to open the trip, then add distance, labor, and waiting.

That is truck transport pricing. It is not the same thing as Ahamove’s moving packages, which add labor, floor limits, and bundled wait time, and it is still a narrower service model than a white-glove relocation company.

This is usually the cheapest route when three things are true:

- the building access is easy
- the packing is already done
- no technical work is needed

It becomes a bad fit when the move includes any of the following:

- AC disassembly or reinstallation
- furniture that needs real dismantling and reassembly
- narrow stairs over multiple floors
- long carry distance from alley or basement
- a landlord or management office that wants one crew to take responsibility for the whole job

In practice, this option works best when the move can still be finished as a controlled same-day job. Before booking, confirm the helper count, the floor count, any extra stop, and how waiting time is charged. If those points stay vague, the cheap quote is not actually cheap.

## Hanoi to HCMC or other intercity moves: compare the quote against replacement cost first

Intercity moving should not be treated like a bigger local move. It is a different decision.

Public mover pages and relocation companies generally handle long-distance jobs by inventory, volume, distance, and service scope rather than by one fixed national tariff. That is the right way to think about it. The wrong way is to look for one magic “Hanoi to HCMC price.”

There are still useful public planning anchors, but they should stay in the right lane. One current intercity reference page from Hanoi shows roughly **1.5-1.7 million VND** for a 500kg truck over about **100km** and around **3-3.5 million VND** for a 2-ton truck over about **100km**, before the job turns into a fuller apartment move with more labor, packing, and real building friction. Those are planning examples, not a Hanoi-to-HCMC apartment benchmark.

Use intercity movers when the items are worth moving:

- better desks and office chairs
- newer appliances
- solid furniture that would be expensive to replace
- monitors, work gear, and other items that matter to daily use

Sell and rebuy when the load is mostly:

- cheap wardrobes
- low-end particle-board furniture
- bulky items near end-of-life
- things that cost less to replace than to carry across the country

Treat Hanoi-to-HCMC as a multi-day logistics job, not as something to line up with a same-day apartment handover. If dates are tight, build buffer into the plan or consider temporary storage.

If the move also means changing neighborhoods or replacing part of the furniture mix, it may be cheaper to move less and rebuy more. See [How to Furnish an Apartment in Vietnam as an Expat](/vietnam/housing/furnish-apartment-vietnam-expats-2026) before paying to drag low-value furniture across the country.

## Leaving Vietnam: parcel shipping for selected boxes, full relocation only for narrow cases

Expats leaving Vietnam often combine three different decisions into one:

- send documents or a few boxes
- pay airline excess baggage
- hire an international household-goods mover

Those are not the same thing.

For documents, gifts, clothes, books, or a limited number of cartons, start with:

- Vietnam Post
- J&T International
- SF International
- DHL Express
- FedEx
- UPS

Vietnam Post’s English site still gives a practical first stop because it exposes **International Delivery**, **Find Post Office**, and **Fee Estimate** tools from one place. FedEx Vietnam’s rate page is current for **January 5, 2026** pricing and still points users to a live rate tool and export-rate PDFs. UPS Vietnam still explains commercial-invoice requirements and DDP vs DDU clearly. J&T International still markets international service from Vietnam and points users to a price list and nearby post office lookup. SF International still exposes a Vietnam-facing time-and-cost tool.

Price reality matters here. A very small outbound shipment can still land anywhere from the **several-hundred-thousand-VND** range to well above **1 million VND** for low weights, depending on the carrier, destination zone, and service level. Once the shipment becomes several heavy cartons instead of one small parcel, stop guessing and run the actual quote tools.

For furniture, many cartons, or anything that feels like “part of an apartment,” the job changes. That is household relocation, not normal parcel shipping. The paperwork, inventory handling, customs risk, and agent coordination are heavier. For most departing expats, this only makes sense when one of these is true:

- the move is employer-paid
- the goods are genuinely high-value
- replacement at destination would be much more expensive
- the items have personal or work value that is hard to replace

Do not read “door to door” as “nothing else can go wrong.” UPS still makes clear that the invoice and duty setup matter, and that receiver-side charges can still appear. That matters if the shipment is going to family or to a future address that is not ready for surprise costs.

For a narrower outbound-box job, see [International Shipping from Vietnam (2026)](/vietnam/daily-life/international-shipping-vietnam-2026). If customs or destination paperwork means printing, scanning, or copying supporting documents before departure, [Print, Scan, and Courier Documents in Vietnam](/vietnam/digital-nomad/print-scan-courier-documents-vietnam-2026) covers the easiest starting points.

## Packing supplies and short-term storage: small details that save bad move days

Packing materials are easy to leave until late, then overpay for.

Current public mover add-on pages still show moving cartons around **18,000 VND each** on some city mover price lists, while bubble-wrap rolls can show up around **150,000 VND** on the same style of pages. Prices move around, so treat those as planning anchors, not fixed shelf prices.

Where to buy:

- from the mover as part of the quote
- from carton shops in major cities
- from marketplaces if there is enough lead time
- from office-supply sellers for tape, markers, labels, and document sleeves

Storage is the other adjacent option people forget until the handover goes wrong. In Hanoi, currently visible self-storage examples include small or shared options from roughly **600,000-660,000 VND/month**, while luggage-storage style options can sit around **120,000 VND per bag per day**. That is city-specific, not a national benchmark, but it is enough to justify checking storage instead of forcing a same-day key handover.

If the move spans a humid period or the stored items include electronics, papers, shoes, or textiles, ask about humidity control and access hours before paying.

## What to prepare before move day

A cleaner move usually comes from better prep, not a better slogan on the truck.

Do this first:

- photograph the larger items and any existing damage
- confirm elevator booking, loading bay rules, and move-hour limits with building management
- send the mover or driver photos of the alley, stairwell, and parking situation
- ask in writing what is included: cartons, wrapping, dismantling, reassembly, waiting time, extra stops, and stairs
- separate passports, cash, medicines, laptops, chargers, and contracts into one personal bag that does not go in the truck
- close out utilities and meter photos before handover; [Electricity Bills for Renters in Vietnam](/vietnam/housing/electricity-bills-renters-2026) helps with the final-bill side
- plan internet transfer or a new install early if the move also changes buildings; [Home Internet in Vietnam as an Expat](/vietnam/daily-life/home-internet-vietnam-expats-2026) covers the setup risks
- protect the deposit and handover record the same way as any other move-out; [How to Avoid Rental Scams in Vietnam](/vietnam/housing/avoid-rental-scams-vietnam-2026) covers the red flags that still matter at exit

## When selling and rebuying usually beats moving

Selling and rebuying often wins when:

- the move is North-South
- the furniture is cheap and bulky
- the apartment is mostly standard marketplace furniture
- the new place will need a different layout anyway
- the international leg is the real cost driver

Moving usually wins when:

- the furniture is better than average
- the work setup matters
- replacement would be slow or expensive
- the shipment is small enough that keeping the good pieces still works

The mistake is to ask, “Can this be moved?” The better question is, “Should this be moved?”

## The practical decision rule

Choose the cheapest option that still matches the real friction of the move.

- Easy room move: app truck or truck plus labor
- Furnished same-city apartment: full-service mover
- Hanoi-to-HCMC household move: quote it, then compare against replacement cost
- Leaving Vietnam with selected boxes: carrier tools first
- Leaving Vietnam with real household goods: only do it when the value case is clear
- Handover gap: check storage before forcing a rushed move
