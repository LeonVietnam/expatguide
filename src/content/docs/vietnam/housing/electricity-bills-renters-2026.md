If a landlord or building manager asks for electricity money in Vietnam, the safest move is to identify the billing setup before paying. A renter is usually in one of three situations: paying EVN directly, reimbursing a landlord who holds the main account, or paying a bundled utility charge inside rent or service fees. Once that is clear, ask for the source bill or meter basis, pay through a traceable channel, and treat any flat per-kWh quote with no supporting bill as a warning sign.

*Data note: the official renter-billing rules used here come from Thông tư 60/2025/TT-BCT; bill-reading details come from EVN sample invoice materials; payment workflow and anti-scam advice come from EVN and regional EVN customer-service materials current at drafting.* ([vbpl.vn](https://vbpl.vn/TW/Pages/vbpq-toanvan.aspx?ItemID=184102))

## The first question: what kind of electricity bill is this?

Before arguing about the rate, work out which setup applies.

### 1) Direct EVN billing

This is the cleanest setup. The account is tied to an EVN customer code, and the bill can usually be checked through an official EVN customer-service site, app, bank bill-payment screen, or invoice record.

Signs this is your setup:

- there is a customer code
- the bill looks like an EVN invoice, not a landlord-made spreadsheet
- the amount due is tied to a billing period and meter reading
- payment can be matched to an official EVN channel

### 2) Landlord pass-through billing

This is common in mini-apartments, room houses, and many landlord-managed rentals. The landlord receives the main electricity bill and then collects each room’s share.

This setup is not automatically unfair. The problem starts when the landlord collects money as if it were a pass-through utility charge but refuses to show the source bill, the meter basis, or the calculation.

### 3) Bundled utilities or service-fee billing

This is common in serviced apartments and some all-in rentals. Electricity may be rolled into a broader monthly utilities or service line.

This is harder to judge by electricity law alone because the charge may be partly contractual rather than a pure pass-through bill. In these cases, the key question is not just “What is the official electricity tariff?” but also “What did the contract actually say was included, separate, or variable?”

## How to read a real EVN electricity bill

A real EVN invoice gives a renter several points that can be checked.

Look for these fields:

- customer code
- address of electricity use
- purpose of use, usually residential for a normal home account
- number of households if shown
- billing period
- old meter reading
- new meter reading
- total kWh used
- total amount payable
- due date
- QR or payment section

Invoice layouts vary by region, but the same basic logic should still be there: who the account belongs to, what period is being charged, how many kWh were used, and what total amount is due. An EVN sample invoice shows these core fields clearly, including customer details, meter readings, kWh consumed, total amount, due date, and payment section. ([evn.com.vn](https://evn.com.vn/userfile/User/tcdl/files/mauhoadoncodiengiai_ver2.pdf))

If a landlord says the charge is based on an EVN bill, but can only show a chat message with one final number and no bill details, that is a weak position for the landlord, not for the tenant.

## What the official rule says for renters

The official framework is useful, but it only helps if it is matched to the right rental setup.

### One rental address, one electricity contract

The circular says the electricity seller signs only one electricity sale contract for each rental address, and the landlord must provide residence information for the people renting there. That explains why many renters are billed through a landlord-held main account rather than getting separate EVN accounts for each room. ([vbpl.vn](https://vbpl.vn/TW/Pages/vbpq-toanvan.aspx?ItemID=184102))

### Family rental cases

If the rental is for one household, the landlord can sign the electricity contract directly or authorize the renting household to sign. Each renting household gets one residential quota. ([vbpl.vn](https://vbpl.vn/TW/Pages/vbpq-toanvan.aspx?ItemID=184102))

### Student and worker rentals that are not one household

If the renters are not one household, the rule splits into different cases.

- If the lease is 12 months or more and the tenant has temporary or permanent residence registration, the landlord or a tenant representative can sign directly with the electricity seller, with the landlord’s payment commitment where required.
- If the lease is under 12 months and the landlord does not fully declare the number of users, the official fallback is the residential tier that corresponds to 101-200 kWh for the full metered usage.
- If the landlord does declare the number of users, every four people count as one household quota, with fractional treatment for one, two, or three people. ([vbpl.vn](https://vbpl.vn/TW/Pages/vbpq-toanvan.aspx?ItemID=184102))

### The rule that matters most in overcharging disputes

If the tenant does not sign directly with the electricity seller, the total electricity money the landlord collects from the tenant must not exceed the monthly amount shown on the electricity invoice issued by the retail electricity unit. ([vbpl.vn](https://vbpl.vn/TW/Pages/vbpq-toanvan.aspx?ItemID=184102))

That is the official position.

What renters often see in practice is messier. Flat “market rate” room pricing still shows up in media coverage, including reports of roughly 3,800-5,000 VND per kWh in some rentals. That does not mean every flat rate is automatically unlawful in every bundled setup, but it does mean a renter should ask harder questions when a landlord quotes a high private rate and will not show the source bill. ([tuoitre.vn](https://tuoitre.vn/dien-phong-tro-gia-3-800-5-000-dong-kwh-tra-tien-dien-gan-ngang-tien-phong-20241117134310431.htm?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

## What to ask the landlord for before paying

For landlord pass-through billing, a cautious renter should ask for proof before sending money.

A useful checklist is:

- a screenshot or PDF of the current month’s source electricity bill
- the billing period
- the customer code if the bill is said to be an EVN bill
- the room meter or sub-meter reading, if the building uses room-level meters
- the basis for splitting common-area electricity, if any
- the final calculation from source bill to room charge
- the payment destination and name
- a written confirmation or receipt after payment

This does not need to be aggressive. A simple message is enough:

> Please send the source bill, the meter reading for this room, and the calculation for this month before payment.

If the landlord becomes evasive as soon as the source bill is requested, that is a stronger signal than the price quote alone.

## The safest ways to pay electricity

### Best case: pay through an official EVN channel

If the account is under the tenant’s control, or the tenant is paying a verified EVN bill directly, official EVN customer-service systems and linked payment channels are the safest route. Regional EVN customer-service materials show invoice lookup, payment-history tools, online payment, bank payment, and QR-based payment options tied to the customer code. ([cskh.evnspc.vn](https://cskh.evnspc.vn/?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

### Good case: bank transfer to the landlord with a clear memo

If paying the landlord is unavoidable, bank transfer is usually stronger than cash because it leaves a trail.

Use a memo that identifies:

- room number
- billing month
- what the payment is for

Example: `Room 302 electricity May 2026`

Save:

- the landlord’s request message
- the source bill screenshot
- the transfer receipt
- the reply confirming the bill is settled

### Weakest case: cash with no record

Cash is the hardest setup to prove later. If cash is the only option, ask for a written receipt or a message confirming the amount, month, and room.

## How to avoid fake payment links, fake apps, and pressure tactics

EVN has warned customers about scams involving fake “new electricity apps,” refund offers, fake support calls, video-call instructions, and threats of disconnection if the target does not follow instructions immediately. The safe rule is simple: do not install an electricity app from a caller, a chat message, or a random link. Use only the official EVN or regional EVN customer-service channel already listed on the bill or on EVN’s official customer-service pages. ([evn.com.vn](https://evn.com.vn/d/vi-VN/news/Khuyen-cao-nguoi-dan-canh-giac-truoc-chieu-tro-lua-dao-cai-dat-ung-dung-dien-luc-moi-60-134-500858))

Red flags include:

- a caller says third-party payment channels have been stopped and a new app must be installed
- a caller asks for banking details, OTP codes, or screen-sharing access
- a link is sent in chat and the renter is told to pay there immediately
- the payment destination does not match the claimed bill source
- the caller threatens disconnection within minutes unless payment is made now

## Red flags that suggest overcharging

One red flag alone does not prove a violation, but several together usually mean the renter should stop and ask for proof before paying.

Watch for these patterns:

- one flat amount with no billing period and no kWh figure
- a flat per-kWh rate quoted as “market price” with no source bill
- refusal to show the EVN invoice, customer code, or meter basis
- a bill jump with no old and new reading
- extra “electric loss” or “system” charges that were never mentioned before
- pressure to pay in cash fast
- pressure to install an app or use a link sent by chat

A flat private rate is more worrying when the landlord presents it as a pure pass-through electricity charge. In a bundled serviced-apartment arrangement, the contract may allow a broader utility package, so the dispute becomes partly contractual. In a normal landlord pass-through setup, the lack of bill proof matters more.

If the total feels out of line, sense-check it against your broader household budget using our [Vietnam cost of living guide](/vietnam/daily-life/cost-of-living-vietnam-2026).

## What to do if the numbers do not add up

Use this order.

### 1) Ask for proof in writing

Ask for the source bill, room meter reading, and calculation. Keep the exchange in chat or email.

### 2) Match the room charge against the source bill

If this is landlord pass-through billing, compare what the landlord is collecting against the actual invoice amount and the stated split method.

### 3) Check whether the dispute is with EVN billing or landlord rebilling

If the account is a direct EVN account, use the official EVN customer-service channel for your region to verify the bill.

If the problem is landlord rebilling, the issue may sit partly with local electricity guidance and partly with local authorities supervising price implementation. For a Vietnam-wide article, the safest practical advice is to start with the local EVN customer-service center for bill explanation and ask which local authority handles landlord electricity overcollection complaints in that area, because complaint routing can vary by place and by the exact billing setup. EVN’s customer-service center page lists the regional channels for the North, Central region, South, Hanoi, and Ho Chi Minh City. ([evn.com.vn](https://www.evn.com.vn/vi-VN/trung-tam-cskh/Trung-tam-cham-soc-khach-hang-60-144))

### 4) Keep the evidence together

Save:

- lease clause on utilities
- landlord messages
- source bill screenshot
- room meter photos
- transfer receipts
- any later correction or refusal

That file is what gives a renter leverage.

## FAQ

### Can a landlord charge electricity separately from rent?

Yes. The real question is what kind of charge it is. A true pass-through electricity charge should have a source bill and a clear calculation. A bundled utility or service charge depends more on the contract wording.

### Can a renter insist on direct EVN billing?

Only sometimes. It depends on the rental type, lease term, residence registration status, and whether the setup fits the cases where direct contracting is allowed.

### Is a flat per-kWh quote always illegal?

No. It is riskier to treat it as normal when the landlord says it is a pass-through electricity bill but refuses to show the source invoice. In a bundled arrangement, the analysis can be different.

### Is cash ever okay?

Only if there is no better option and the renter gets a receipt or written confirmation plus a copy of the source bill. A traceable transfer is usually stronger.

### The practical bottom line

A renter does not need to memorize the full tariff system to protect against overcharging. The useful order is simpler: identify the billing setup, ask for the source bill or meter basis, pay through a traceable channel, and do not trust links, apps, or pressure tactics sent by strangers. If a landlord says the charge is a pass-through electricity bill, the landlord should be able to show where the number came from.
