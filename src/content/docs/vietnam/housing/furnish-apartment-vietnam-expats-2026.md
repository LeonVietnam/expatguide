## The short answer

Most expats furnishing an apartment in Vietnam should not use just one channel.

For a smoother full-room setup, local furniture chains and larger showrooms usually work best because delivery and assembly are often clearer than on open marketplaces. For smaller add-on buys such as desks, storage, lamps, chairs, bedding, and kitchen basics, Tiki, Lazada, Shopee, and similar platforms are often the fastest route. For the lowest upfront spend, secondhand channels such as Chợ Tốt and Facebook Marketplace can work well, but only if transport, cleaning, and missing-part risk are manageable. For a short bridge period, furniture rental can make sense. IKEA is the exception that needs the most caution: Vietnam still does not have the normal local IKEA store setup many renters expect, so official IKEA access is an international-sales route aimed at bulk orders, not a simple domestic checkout path.

*Price and delivery examples below are approximate, based on public retailer, marketplace, and rental pages visible in April 2026. Stock, promotions, building rules, and seller service vary by city and by item.*

## Start by classifying the apartment before buying anything

The wrong furnishing plan usually starts with misreading the apartment.

Most rentals fall into one of three buckets:

- **Unfurnished:** only fixed kitchen, bathrooms, lights, and maybe air conditioners.
- **Partly furnished:** bed frame, sofa, or dining set is present, but storage, desk space, mattress quality, or kitchen setup is weak.
- **Marketing-furnished:** the listing says furnished, but the unit still lacks the items that matter for daily life, such as a real desk, usable wardrobe, blackout curtains, shoe storage, or a second chair.

That first classification decides the channel.

If the apartment is mostly empty, the safer move is usually one chain or showroom for the biggest pieces, then marketplaces for fill-in items. If the apartment is already usable but awkward, e-commerce and secondhand often beat a full showroom purchase. If the lease is short or the move-in date is uncertain, rental furniture or secondhand is often easier to unwind at move-out.

## Local furniture chains and malls: best for the big pieces

For beds, sofas, wardrobes, dining tables, and larger matching-room pieces, local chains and furniture-center style retailers are usually the lowest-friction route.

Nha Xinh, UMA, and Phố Xinh each fit this bucket, but not in the same way.

- **Nha Xinh** can be a cleaner choice when delivery, assembly, and placement matter and the budget is not entry-level.
- **UMA** can work better when the goal is practical home-office pieces, smaller-room furniture, or made-to-order adjustments, but the buyer still needs to read the service terms carefully.
- **Phố Xinh** often fits better when the apartment needs larger showroom shopping and the renter wants to compare more design-led or higher-ticket pieces in person.

Current public pricing still shows how wide this bucket is. Smaller tables, chairs, and accent pieces can sit in the low single-digit millions of VND, while larger sofas, dining tables, and other premium pieces can rise into the tens of millions. For work-from-home furniture, visible public desk and office-chair pricing on current chain and showroom pages also runs from the low millions into the low tens of millions, depending on finish, size, and seller.

That sounds expensive, but this route buys more than the item itself. It often buys better control over scheduling, clearer contact with the seller, and less guesswork about who is supposed to assemble what.

This route is strongest when:

- the apartment is mostly empty
- the lease is at least around one year
- the building has awkward lift or delivery rules
- mattress, sofa, or dining-table quality matters more than getting the lowest sticker price

This route is weaker when:

- the budget is tight
- the stay may end quickly
- the apartment already has the big pieces and only needs top-up items

*Where to start:* Nha Xinh has current public showroom coverage in HCMC and Hanoi. Phố Xinh publicly lists showroom presence in HCMC, Hanoi, Da Nang, and Nha Trang. UMA is often easier to treat as a direct online-first or order-by-contact route unless a specific local showroom has been confirmed at the time of purchase.

## Delivery and assembly are better here, but they still have limits

This is the main official-versus-actual gap in the topic.

Seller pages can say **delivery**, **assembly**, **placement**, or **installation**, but those words do not always mean the same thing once a truck reaches a condo building.

A useful current example is UMA. Its public purchase and FAQ pages say it can deliver to the home and assemble products in ready-to-use conditions at home. But the same public material also says technicians do not decide on their own to drill, cut, or paint on site, and wall-mounting or wall drilling is outside normal assembly except for certain project categories.

That is the right way to read almost every seller promise in this category.

Before paying for large items, confirm all of the following in writing or by message:

- Is delivery to the building entrance, lobby, unit door, or inside the final room?
- Does assembly include only standard fitting, or also wall fixing and drilling?
- Do extra building or access-related costs still sit with the buyer depending on seller terms and condo rules?
- Will packaging be removed after setup?
- What happens if the item cannot fit inside the lift?
- Is there a return path for damaged or wrong-size furniture after it reaches the unit?

## IKEA in Vietnam: official access exists, but not in the way most renters mean

A renter asking “Can IKEA deliver to Vietnam?” usually means one of two different things:

1. Can a normal consumer order IKEA to a Vietnam apartment the way they would in a country with local IKEA retail?
2. Can someone in Vietnam still get IKEA-style products, or even some IKEA items, through another route?

The first answer is the important one: **not in the normal local-store sense**.

For Vietnam, IKEA’s official public route is International Sales for countries without an IKEA store, and the page is framed around bulk quantity needs rather than casual one-off consumer apartment shopping. That makes it a poor default route for a renter who only needs a desk, bed frame, shelf, and dining set.

The more realistic path for most expats is not “order IKEA directly,” but one of these substitutes:

- imported flat-pack items on marketplace cross-border sections
- local resellers carrying IKEA-branded or IKEA-style pieces
- Vietnamese brands and factory sellers offering the same small-space, flat-pack, light-wood look

That means the right conclusion is not “Vietnam has IKEA,” and it is also not “IKEA is impossible.” The accurate answer sits in the middle: official IKEA access exists, but in a bulk-order format that does not fit most apartment renters, while practical substitutes may still exist for selected pieces.

## E-commerce: best for fill-in items, small-room fixes, and budget desks

For many expats, the most useful furniture channel in Vietnam is not a furniture mall at all. It is the marketplace layer.

Tiki, Lazada, Shopee, and similar platforms are often the best answer when the apartment already works, but needs these kinds of items fast:

- compact desks
- foldable tables
- office chairs
- storage carts
- shoe racks
- lamps
- shelving
- small bedside tables
- bedding, curtains, hangers, and kitchen basics

Current public marketplace listings show why this route is so useful. Visible desk and foldable-table listings often sit from the low hundreds of thousands of VND into a few million, while office chairs commonly run from the high hundreds of thousands into the low single-digit millions on public listings, with larger or more specialized pieces higher.

Tiki and Lazada both clearly support the home-goods route. But this channel should still be treated as **seller-by-seller**, especially for bulky items. A live category page or search result does not mean every seller offers the same indoor delivery, assembly support, return handling, or scheduling quality.

The marketplace route is strongest when:

- the apartment only needs a few pieces
- delivery speed matters more than matching-room design
- the renter wants low to mid spending on desks, organizers, or dining pieces
- move-out resale value is not a major concern

It is weakest when:

- the renter wants one seller to take responsibility for the whole room
- the building has strict access rules
- the item is large, heavy, or easy to damage
- the buyer cannot deal with self-assembly or seller-by-seller after-sales support

*Where to start:* Tiki House & Living, Tiki Cross Border, Lazada Home & Living, and the equivalent furniture sections on Shopee. For Shopee in particular, treat seller ratings, assembly notes, and delivery dimensions as even more important than the platform itself.

## What to check on marketplace listings before paying

This is where many cheap orders stop feeling cheap.

Check these points on every larger listing:

- exact assembled dimensions
- package dimensions if the building lift is small
- whether tools or assembly hardware are included
- whether the seller offers installation or only ships the box
- whether the return policy still applies after partial assembly
- whether the listing promises indoor delivery or only courier drop-off
- whether the delivery estimate is domestic stock or cross-border stock

If the item is large and the listing is vague, assume extra friction rather than a clean setup.

## Secondhand: best for low upfront spend and short leases

For a renter who wants the apartment to function next week without sinking too much money into furniture that may be sold again in six months, secondhand is often the best-value route.

Chợ Tốt is the main national starting point. Facebook Marketplace and local neighborhood or expat resale groups are the next layer. This is also where the “included-brief” adjacent space matters: local resale groups and apartment-community groups can sometimes produce better last-minute deals than the big platforms, especially when someone is leaving Vietnam quickly, but that should be treated as a practical possibility rather than a guarantee.

Current public Chợ Tốt listings still show a wide price spread. Small shelves, racks, and single pieces can start in the low hundreds of thousands of VND. Used bundles can appear around the low single-digit millions. Larger wardrobes and heavier wood pieces can move much higher depending on condition, material, and city.

That makes secondhand the best route when:

- the lease is short
- the budget is tight
- the renter is willing to organize transport
- resale at move-out matters

It becomes a worse route when:

- the item must match exactly
- the building has difficult access rules
- there is no local transporter ready
- cleaning, repair, or missing screws will create delay

A cheap wardrobe is not actually cheap if it needs a truck, two extra workers, deep cleaning, and replacement hardware on the first day.

*Where to start:* Chợ Tốt city filters first, then Facebook Marketplace, then building or neighborhood groups. In larger expat areas, the private-group layer can sometimes be more useful than public listings for desks, shelves, mattresses, and kitchen bundles.

## Rental furniture: useful for bridge periods, not always the cheapest long-term move

Furniture rental exists in Vietnam, but it still works best as a specific-use-case option, not as the default answer for every renter.

The strongest fit is:

- a short assignment
- a serviced-apartment or corporate relocation period
- a delayed container or delayed air shipment
- a lease that may not last long enough to justify buying everything new

Current public WeNest pricing keeps the category concrete. Its listed monthly combo rates currently run around:

- **5.25 million to 8.75 million VND per month** for 1-bedroom packages, depending on term
- **5.75 million to 10 million VND per month** for 2-bedroom packages
- **7 million to 12.5 million VND per month** for 3-bedroom packages

The public page also lists 3, 4, 9, 12, 24, and 36-month terms and says the service includes delivery and setup.

That makes rental furniture easiest to defend for a bridge period, but harder to defend as the best long-stay value if the apartment will be kept for a year or more and the renter is happy to own the pieces.

In other words, rental is often paying for speed, convenience, and reversibility, not for the lowest total cost.

## What sits just outside the main scope

A few adjacent routes matter, but they should stay brief in this article.

### Custom carpentry and built-ins

If the apartment is large, missing storage, or likely to be kept for years, custom carpentry can solve wardrobe, shelving, and kitchen-storage problems better than movable furniture. But that is no longer just a furniture-buying decision. It becomes a landlord-permission, apartment-modification, and workmanship decision.

### Appliance bundles

Some landlords, agents, and local sellers will offer furniture plus appliances as one package. This can be useful if the unit is nearly empty, but it often hides weak quality on one side of the bundle. Judge the washing machine, mattress, sofa, and desk separately rather than assuming the package is good value as a whole.

### Delivery-only sellers

A low price from a factory seller or online-only seller may still leave the renter arranging part of the real work. If the offer is “delivery only,” ask who will carry, assemble, fix, and remove packaging before treating the quote as complete.

## A practical setup path by lease length

### If the lease is 3 to 6 months

The cleanest first check is rental furniture or secondhand.

Buying a full new-room setup for a short stay is often hard to justify unless the apartment will later be reassigned to a friend, staff member, or another long-stay use.

### If the lease is 6 to 12 months and the budget is tight

Use secondhand for the biggest functional pieces, then marketplace orders for what is missing.

That usually means:
- bed or wardrobe secondhand
- desk, lamp, kitchen basics, storage, and soft goods online
- only one or two new chain-store purchases if quality matters

### If the lease is 12 months or longer

Use a chain or showroom for the pieces that are hardest to replace later, especially:
- mattress and bed frame
- sofa
- dining set
- main wardrobe

Then use marketplaces for the rest.

That mixed route is usually the best balance of cost, speed, and move-in friction.

## What to check before paying for any large item

Before sending a deposit or placing a big order, confirm:

- exact unit measurements
- lift measurements and corridor turns
- landlord permission for drilling or wall fixing
- building delivery hours
- extra management charges
- stock status
- return path if the item does not fit
- whether the invoice and warranty are issued clearly
- whether the seller removes packaging
- whether the item can be resold easily later

## The best default for most expats

For most expats moving into a normal one-bedroom or two-bedroom rental in Vietnam, the best default is this:

- use one local chain or showroom for the big pieces
- use e-commerce for the smaller functional pieces
- use secondhand only where the savings are real after transport
- use rental furniture only when time, uncertainty, or reversibility matters more than total cost
- do not treat IKEA as a normal local retail option in Vietnam

That route is usually cheaper than buying everything from one showroom, but far less chaotic than trying to source the entire apartment from scattered sellers.
