Vietnam's rental market is accessible, affordable, and full of traps that catch even experienced expats off guard. Overcharged electricity bills, ghost landlords, contracts signed in a language you cannot read, and apartments in buildings that face forced closure under new fire safety laws — these are not hypotheticals. They happen regularly.

This guide covers what you actually need to know before signing anything.

---

## Can Foreigners Legally Rent in Vietnam?

Yes, with no formal restrictions on visa type. Any foreigner who has entered Vietnam legally can sign a rental contract. There is no minimum visa duration required by law to rent.

In practice, the situation is more complicated. Most landlords want your visa to cover a meaningful portion of the lease — typically at least one to three months of remaining validity, and preferably the full lease term. Managed buildings and formal agents will often require a work permit or TRC before issuing a 12-month contract. If you arrive on a 30-day e-Visa, expect resistance on anything beyond a month-to-month arrangement. For longer leases, a business visa, work permit, or TRC gives you significantly more bargaining power in negotiations.

## The Tạm Trú Registration: Why It Matters More Than Most Expats Realise

*Tạm Trú* (temporary residence declaration) is the mechanism by which your landlord registers your presence with local authorities. It is mandatory under Article 33 of Vietnam's Law on Foreigners' Entry, Exit, Transit and Residence, and the primary legal responsibility sits with your landlord. That said, failure to register still creates real risk for you as the tenant — your visa renewal, TRC application, and bank KYC all depend on having a valid registration on file.

**How it works in 2026:** Registration is done digitally through online immigration portals — either the national system at `evisa.xuatnhapcanh.gov.vn` or provincial equivalents such as `hanoi.xuatnhapcanh.gov.vn` or `hochiminh.xuatnhapcanh.gov.vn`. In practice, many landlords — particularly in smaller buildings — still use a local police station process or are simply unfamiliar with the online system entirely. The valid proof of registration is a system-generated PDF confirmation (*Xác nhận tạm trú*), which is what banks and immigration offices will ask for.

**Why it matters for you:**
- Without it, you cannot renew or extend your visa
- Banks require it for account opening and KYC updates
- Police in cities like Da Nang conduct regular building checks
- If your landlord never files it, your legal residence status is technically non-compliant

**What to do:** On the day you move in, tell your landlord explicitly that you need the Tạm Trú confirmation within 24 hours. The law requires the declaration within 12 hours in urban areas. If they are unfamiliar with the online system, walk them through the relevant provincial portal. Follow up in writing on Zalo — this creates a timestamped record. Many landlords in smaller buildings do not file unless pushed.

## The Chung Cư Mini Risk: Cheap Studios That Can Get You Evicted

Vietnam's Housing Law 2023 (Law No. 27/2023/QH15), which took full effect on 1 August 2024 via implementing Decree 95/2024/ND-CP, introduced strict fire safety standards for small multi-unit buildings — what locals call *chung cư mini*. The immediate trigger was a September 2023 fire in a Hanoi mini apartment building that killed dozens of residents.

The law requires larger multi-unit buildings (commonly 20+ units or similar density) to meet formal fire safety standards, including independent emergency escape routes. Many cheap studio apartments popular with budget-conscious expats — particularly those in narrow alleys (*ngõ* or *hẻm*), single-staircase buildings — do not and cannot meet these standards.

**The practical risk:** Authorities can cut power and order evacuation of non-compliant buildings. Enforcement is uneven and ongoing rather than a single coordinated wave, but it is real and has happened. If you are budgeting under $300/month and looking at a studio in a deep alley, ask the building manager directly: *Has this building passed its fire safety inspection (PCCC) under the 2023 Housing Law?*

If they cannot show you documentation, factor the eviction risk into your decision.

## The Electricity Trap: Vietnam's Most Reliably Exploited Expat Loophole

This is the single issue that comes up most consistently in expat forums, and most new arrivals learn it the hard way.

**How it works:** Vietnam's state electricity provider EVN uses a tiered pricing structure. For residential tenants, the applicable household rate currently sits around 2,200–2,400 VND/kWh following the May 2025 price adjustment (Decision 1279/QD-BCT). For reference, check EVN's official rate schedule at `en.evn.com.vn`.

**What landlords charge instead:** The majority of serviced apartments charge a fixed rate of 3,500–4,500 VND/kWh, often written directly into the lease. MoIT pricing guidelines stipulate that landlords reselling household electricity should not exceed Tier 3 rates plus a maximum 10% for line losses. In practice, however, many serviced apartment arrangements operate in a legal grey area — particularly where electricity is bundled as part of a service package — and enforcement is almost nonexistent. Do not expect to win a legal argument about this. The more practical approach is to avoid the situation entirely.

**How to protect yourself:**
1. **Prioritise apartments with an independent electricity meter (đồng hồ điện riêng).** This means you pay EVN directly through the EVN app, MoMo, or your banking app — the landlord never touches your bill. Only property owners can hold an EVN account, so this requires the landlord to have set up a sub-meter specifically for your unit.
2. **If the meter is shared**, negotiate the per-kWh rate in writing before signing, and ask to see recent bills. Some landlords will move closer to the official rate if asked directly.
3. **Always photograph the meter reading** on your move-in date and keep monthly photos. Without a baseline reading, disputes are difficult to resolve.
4. **Ask what the previous tenant paid monthly.** This is the most practical data point available.

Air conditioning is by far the largest electricity cost in Vietnam. In HCMC and Da Nang during peak summer months, a one-bedroom apartment with AC running most of the day can generate a bill of 1.5–2.5 million VND. At an inflated rate, you are overpaying by 30–50% on top of that.

## Before You Sign: The Verification Checklist

**1. Verify the actual owner**

Vietnam has a significant sub-letting problem. The person showing you the apartment and signing your contract is frequently not the owner — they are a sub-lessor who rented from the owner and is now re-letting at a markup. If they disappear, your deposit goes with them and your contract may be unenforceable.

Ask to see the *Sổ Hồng* (Land Use Rights Certificate). The name on the Sổ Hồng must match the person signing your lease, or you need to see a notarised power of attorney (*Giấy ủy quyền*) from the registered owner.

**2. Do a Zalo video walkthrough before committing**

Property listings in Vietnam routinely use outdated photos, photos from different units in the same building, or heavily edited images. Before travelling across a city to view a place, ask the agent or landlord to do a live Zalo video call starting from the street entrance, through the building lobby, and into the actual unit. Natural light, real floor condition, and the actual view cannot be faked in a live call.

**3. Check the surrounding 50 metres**

Look out any window that faces neighbouring land. Blue corrugated metal fencing around a cleared plot means a construction site is either active or imminent. In Vietnam's major cities, construction can begin with minimal notice and run with no regard for noise restrictions. This is one of the most common reasons expats break leases early.

**4. Test everything in the unit**

Run every air conditioning unit for five minutes — check the temperature output and listen for abnormal noise. Run every tap. Flush the toilet. Check mobile signal inside the unit. Check the water pressure. A building that looks premium from the lobby can have chronic pressure problems on upper floors.

## Contract Negotiation: What to Change Before Signing

Vietnamese lease contracts are typically binding in their Vietnamese version. Where a bilingual contract exists, the Vietnamese text typically prevails in the event of a dispute under the Civil Code 2015 (Law No. 91/2015/QH13). An English translation is useful for your understanding but does not override the Vietnamese terms if they conflict. Have a Vietnamese speaker verify the key clauses before you sign.

**Clauses to add or modify:**

**Early termination:** Standard contracts often include "forfeit full deposit if you leave early." Push for: *30 days written notice allows refund of at least 50% of deposit.* Many landlords will accept this.

**Maintenance response time:** Add a clause that if a critical appliance (air conditioning, refrigerator, water heater) is not repaired within 48 hours of written notice, you may engage your own repair service and deduct the cost from the following month's rent.

**Rent escalation:** Fix the rent for the full lease term. Some contracts allow landlords to increase rent with short notice periods.

**Handover documentation:** A signed inventory (*biên bản bàn giao*) listing every item in the unit with its condition. On move-in day, record a 4K video with timestamps of the entire unit and send it to the landlord on Zalo immediately — these records can be used as supporting evidence in disputes. This single step eliminates most deposit conflicts.

## Rental Ranges by City (2025–2026)

One-bedroom / large studio rates for long-term leases in expat-popular areas. Short-term or serviced apartment rates run 30–60% higher.

**Ho Chi Minh City**
- Thảo Điền (District 2 / Thủ Đức): $650–$1,100. Main expat hub. Built on reclaimed marshland — prone to flooding during heavy rain and high-tide events. Always ask about flood history and avoid ground-floor units.
- District 1 (centre): $700–$1,200. Best walkability, most expensive.
- District 4: $450–$750. Walking distance to District 1, local character, very dense.
- District 7 / Phú Mỹ Hưng: $550–$900. Planned, clean, no flooding. Genuinely difficult to commute from.
- Bình Thạnh (Vinhomes Central Park area): $500–$850. Good compromise between price and access to D1.

**Hanoi**
- Tây Hồ / West Lake peninsula: $550–$900. The expat core. Roads into the peninsula are narrow — peak-hour Grab pickups can take 20+ minutes.
- Long Biên: $400–$700. Higher air quality, more space, quieter. Daily commute crosses the Red River.
- Cầu Giấy / Mỹ Đình: $350–$600. University and tech district, strong infrastructure, fewer expat amenities.

**Da Nang**
- An Thượng (near Mỹ Khê beach): $300–$550. Built for short- to mid-term foreigners. Noise complaints from construction and street karaoke are common.
- Sơn Trà peninsula: $350–$600. Quieter, green, slightly removed from the expat service cluster.
- Seasonal note: Da Nang's typhoon season runs September–November. Infrastructure disruptions during this period are real — factor this into lease timing decisions.

**Deposit:** Standard is two months' rent. Some higher-end properties request three. Legally, deposits should be returned within 15 days of contract termination minus documented damage costs. In practice, allow 30 days and expect negotiation.

**Agent commission:** Agents are paid by landlords in most cases. If an agent asks you directly for a commission, clarify the arrangement before proceeding — double-dipping happens.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need a Vietnamese bank account to pay rent?**

Most landlords accept cash initially, then move to bank transfer. Bank transfer is strongly preferable — it creates a payment trail that protects you in deposit disputes. Cash with no receipt leaves you no recourse. Rent must be paid in VND; contracts between Vietnam-based parties must be denominated in dong under Vietnamese foreign exchange regulations.

**What if my landlord never files the Tạm Trú?**

Follow up in writing on Zalo to create a timestamped record. If they continue to refuse, your options are to accept the risk (routine police checks are uncommon in most buildings) or find different accommodation. You cannot file Tạm Trú yourself — the system requires the property owner's account credentials.

**What is the standard lease length?**

Most landlords prefer 12 months. Six-month leases are available but typically come at a 10–15% premium. For long-term residents, a 12-month lease with a 6-month renewal option is the most commonly negotiated structure.

**Is it safe to rent without getting the contract translated?**

No. At minimum, have a Vietnamese speaker walk you through the early termination, deposit, and maintenance clauses before you sign. The Vietnamese text governs in any dispute — a reassuring English summary does not protect you if the Vietnamese says something different.

**Can I rent a house or villa rather than an apartment?**

Yes, and in Tây Hồ (Hanoi) and Thảo Điền (HCMC) there are active villa markets. Expect higher utility bills, personal responsibility for garden or pool maintenance, and even more reason to verify the Sổ Hồng — villa sub-letting is common and the financial exposure is higher.

## Summary

The Vietnamese rental market rewards preparation and punishes assumptions. Verify ownership before paying anything. Negotiate electricity terms in writing or find a unit with an independent meter. Push your landlord to file Tạm Trú the day you move in. Document the unit's condition on video before unpacking.
