## The short answer

Before paying any deposit, a rental viewing in Vietnam should answer three questions:

- Is the unit physically sound enough to live in without immediate repair fights?
- Can the person renting it out clearly show ownership or authority to rent it?
- Do the utilities, exits, and neighborhood reality still make sense after the viewing pitch ends?

If one of those three stays unclear, delay the deposit.

*Source note: the formal housing framework matters for contracts and party identity, but many of the checks that protect renters in Vietnam still happen at the practical viewing stage.*

## Use a three-pass method during the viewing

A good viewing is not just a walk-through.

Use it in three passes:

1. **First pass:** scan smell, heat, light, dampness, and general upkeep in the first two or three minutes.
2. **Second pass:** run the systems that can fail later: shower, sinks, toilet, AC, lights, outlets, and hot water.
3. **Third pass:** ask who controls the unit, how utilities are billed, and what the building and street are like after dark or during rain.

This matters because different rental types hide different problems. A serviced apartment may look easier because utilities are bundled, but that can also make real usage harder to verify. A sublet can work, but only if the chain of permission is clear. An agent-managed condo unit is not automatically worse than a direct-owner deal, but the document trail has to make sense before money moves.

## Start with the unit itself: walls, ceilings, bathrooms, windows

The first physical check is simple: look for signs that water has already been where it should not be.

Check:

- ceiling corners, especially near bathrooms and AC lines
- the lower part of walls near windows and balconies
- cabinet bottoms under sinks
- the bathroom ceiling and floor drain area
- swollen wood, bubbling paint, or a damp smell behind curtains and near wardrobes

Some defects are repair items. Others are warnings.

A small paint crack near a door frame or an old patch job from a previous tenant is usually not the same as a recurring crack near a column, a ceiling stain that still feels damp, or a wall that has clearly been repainted over mold. If the bathroom smells wet even after ventilation, or if the unit has fresh paint in one problem corner but the nearby board, wall, or cabinet base is still swollen, treat that as an active issue rather than a cosmetic one.

Open every window. Check whether they close cleanly, whether rain can blow in, and whether the seal looks damaged. In alley houses and lower-floor units, look at balcony drains and any outside slope toward the door. A dry viewing on a sunny day can still hide a bad rain problem.

## Check wiring, outlets, and heavy-load points

Many rental problems do not look dramatic at first. They show up when the tenant starts using the unit like a home.

Turn on:

- main lights
- bathroom fan if there is one
- water heater
- one AC unit
- at least one kitchen outlet

Then look for:

- burnt marks around outlets
- loose faceplates
- extension strips feeding major appliances
- old or exposed wire sections
- a breaker box that is missing, inaccessible, or badly labeled

If the viewer cannot show where the breaker box is, or says the tenant just has to avoid using too many things at once, that is a real warning. A washing machine, water heater, induction cooker, and multiple AC units create a different load profile from a lightly used short-stay room.

The goal at the viewing is not to certify the wiring. It is to catch the obvious signs that the electrical setup may become the tenant's problem after move-in.

## Test the AC like a tenant, not like a viewer

Run every indoor AC unit, not just the one nearest the sofa.

Check for:

- weak airflow
- delayed cooling
- loud vibration or compressor noise
- indoor dripping
- rusty brackets or heavy corrosion
- missing remote controls
- oily marks around the unit or pipe area

Also read the model sticker if it is still visible. Ask when the unit was last cleaned, whether it has had recent gas or compressor work, and who pays if it stops cooling after move-in.

An older AC is not always a deal-breaker. An older unit that still cools evenly, has been cleaned, and has a believable service history can still be usable. A unit that leaks, struggles, or comes with a vague promise that it will be fixed after deposit is a different category.

For the bigger decision on repair risk, power use, and when an old unit becomes a money sink, see [Air Conditioning in Vietnam Apartments](/vietnam/housing/air-conditioning-vietnam-apartments-2026).

## Verify who actually controls the unit before deposit

This is where official rules and practical reality need to stay separate.

**Formal position:** a residential lease should be in writing and should clearly identify the parties and the property.

**Practical reality:** many viewings are handled by agents, relatives, current tenants, or building staff rather than the named owner.

That does not make the deal automatically bad. It does mean the chain of control has to be clear before money moves.

If the owner is present, ask for the name on the ID and the name on the property document or lease paperwork to line up with the person taking the deposit.

If the viewer is not the owner, ask for a clear explanation of one of these setups:

- agency authorization or management arrangement
- landlord confirmation in writing or in a message thread
- current tenant transfer or sublet with landlord consent
- company-held unit with a named signatory on the lease

A lease not being notarized is not the main risk here. Missing written terms, mismatched names, and confusion over who can legally take the money are bigger problems.

If the answer to "Who owns this unit, who can sign the lease, and who receives the deposit?" stays vague, stop there. That same pattern shows up in the bigger traps covered in [Avoid Rental Scams in Vietnam](/vietnam/housing/avoid-rental-scams-vietnam-2026).

For foreign tenants, one more practical pre-deposit check is who will handle temporary residence declaration after move-in. Ask whether the landlord, building, or management can register a foreign tenant promptly after arrival, and what they usually need from the tenant, such as passport pages, visa or entry-stamp details, and signed lease materials. A unit can look fine at viewing stage and still be a bad fit if the person taking the deposit cannot clearly explain how move-in registration is handled.

## Clarify electricity, water, and internet before leaving

Utilities should never be treated as a minor afterthought in Vietnam rentals.

### Electricity

Ask whether the unit has:

- a direct EVN account
- landlord rebilling based on meter readings
- bundled electricity inside one flat monthly rent figure

Then ask for proof that matches the answer: a recent bill, a meter photo, or the customer code if the setup is direct.

If the landlord rebills electricity, ask:

- what rate is used
- whether there is any added service fee
- how readings are recorded
- whether common-area usage is mixed into the bill

For the full billing logic and the common overcharge patterns renters should screen for, see [Electricity Bills for Renters](/vietnam/housing/electricity-bills-renters-2026).

### Water

Ask where the water reaching the unit actually comes from in practice:

- direct city supply
- building tank and pump
- house tank and pump
- a mixed setup

That matters because a unit can have running water during a short daytime viewing and still have poor pressure, tank smell, pump issues, or upper-floor shortages later.

Useful questions include:

- Does pressure drop during peak shower hours?
- Has the unit had any smell, rust, or sediment issue?
- Who handles pump failure or tank cleaning?
- Does water back up in the bathroom after heavy rain?

For drinking and cooking decisions after move-in, cross-check with [Drinking Water in Vietnam for Expats](/vietnam/daily-life/drinking-water-vietnam-expats-2026).

### Internet

Ask whether internet is:

- included in rent
- shared with other rooms
- already active in the unit
- locked to one building provider
- the landlord's account or the tenant's responsibility

Serviced apartments often make this easy operationally, but they can also hide what the household is really getting. Condo and house rentals can give clearer control, but they usually push more setup responsibility onto the tenant.

## Check the building, stairwell, and actual exit path

Do not judge building safety from the lobby alone.

Look at the route from the unit to the street:

- Is the stairwell usable?
- Are doors or gates locked in a way that would slow exit?
- Are bikes, shoe racks, boxes, or other storage blocking the corridor?
- Are extinguishers visible and accessible?
- Does the parking level feel badly cluttered or poorly maintained?

In a smaller building or alley house, the question is even simpler: if smoke or an electrical fire started below, how would a resident get out?

*Source note: fire-safety duties exist on paper, but renters still need to check the route out with their own eyes. A verbal assurance from the agent is weaker than a visible, usable exit path.*

A polished lobby does not fix a blocked stairwell. A newer tower does not automatically beat an older building if everyday management is weak.

## Screen the neighborhood for noise, flooding, and daily friction

A daytime viewing often hides the things that later make a rental hard to live in.

Before deciding, try to learn three things about the block:

### Noise after dark

Ask the guard, cleaner, or nearest shop:

- Is there karaoke nearby?
- Are there bars, event venues, or loud restaurants on the street?
- Does the building get noisy from short-stay guests?
- Is there nearby construction that starts early?

A building that feels calm at 2 pm can be very different at 10 pm.

### Flooding and drainage

Ask whether the street, alley entrance, or parking area takes on water during heavy rain. In many Vietnam neighborhoods, the weak point is not the unit itself. It is the last 20 meters between the main road and the door, or the ramp into the parking area.

If possible, do a second visit after rain or ask someone who works there every day whether motorbikes, shoes, or delivery access get affected.

### Daily friction

Look for school-gate traffic, market unloading, garbage pickup timing, generator noise, and difficult car access if the household uses taxis often. These are small on a viewing day and large after move-in.

If the building works but the block does not fit the tenant's routine, the apartment is still the wrong apartment.

## Questions to ask the landlord or agent before leaving

A useful viewing usually ends with a short question list, not with a transfer.

Ask:

- Who owns the unit, and whose name will appear on the lease?
- Who receives the deposit, and can a written receipt be issued the same day?
- Who will handle temporary residence declaration after move-in?
- What passport, visa, entry-stamp, or lease documents will be needed from the foreign tenant for that process?
- What exactly is included in rent?
- How is electricity billed, and can the latest bill or meter reading be shown?
- What is the water setup, and has the unit had pressure or smell problems?
- Is internet already active, and whose account is it?
- Which repairs will be completed before move-in, and by what date?
- When were the AC units last cleaned or repaired?
- Are there building rules on pets, guests, parking, or move-in hours?
- Has the street or parking area had flooding, and is the block noisy at night?

If the answers come quickly, consistently, and with supporting proof, that is a good sign. If every answer turns into "no problem, can fix later" or "just trust the building," treat that as information too.

## Walk away now vs negotiate later

Some viewing problems should end the deal. Others can be negotiated if the rest of the setup is strong.

### Walk away now

Walk away if the viewing shows any of these and the explanation stays weak:

- active leak signs or a damp smell that suggests an ongoing water issue
- repeated cracking, sagging, or structural-looking damage
- burnt outlets, missing breaker access, or clear high-load electrical shortcuts
- AC units that leak or fail to cool, paired with a promise to fix them only after deposit
- no clear answer on who owns the unit, who can sign, or who receives the money
- no clear answer on temporary residence declaration support after move-in, or obvious reluctance to handle foreign-tenant registration
- vague electricity billing with no bill, no meter logic, and no proof
- blocked exit routes, locked escape gates, or visibly poor stairwell conditions
- pressure to transfer a deposit before documents or terms are shown

### Negotiate or delay

These problems are usually not automatic deal-breakers:

- repainting
- mattress replacement
- curtain replacement
- deep cleaning
- small hardware fixes
- one old AC that still works but should be cleaned before move-in

The useful question is not whether the unit is perfect. It is whether the remaining problems are visible, priced, and controllable before money changes hands.
