## The short answer

For most foreigners in Vietnam, a serviced apartment makes more sense for the first weeks or first few months, while a regular rental makes more sense once the city, neighborhood, budget, and daily routine are already clear.

The reason is not just furniture. The real trade-off is **convenience and flexibility versus monthly cost and control**.

A serviced apartment usually gives faster move-in, fewer setup tasks, and a higher chance of getting cleaning, internet, basic kitchen equipment, and move-in administration handled in one package. A regular rental usually gives better long-stay value, more choice, and more room to negotiate, but it also pushes more work onto the tenant.

*Data note: price bands below are approximate 2026 market ranges based on public listings and market commentary. They vary by district, building class, size, season, and what is bundled into rent.*

## What the difference really is

A lot of listings make this sound simpler than it is.

In day-to-day market use, a **serviced apartment** usually means a furnished place with at least some bundled services, such as cleaning, internet, front-desk support, linen changes, or easier short-stay terms. A **regular rental** usually means a standard apartment or house lease where the tenant handles more of the setup and ongoing bills.

That is the practical difference readers usually care about.

The legal side is less neat. A regular rental is usually just a housing lease. A serviced apartment may be run more like tourist accommodation, or it may still be a residential lease with extra services attached. That is why the label alone is not enough. The contract, operator, included services, and registration support matter more than the headline on the listing.

## The official layer vs the practical layer

### Official layer

A normal apartment rental in Vietnam is usually a housing lease contract. That matters because it affects the paperwork, deposit structure, and how the relationship with the landlord or agent is handled.

A serviced apartment may sit under a different operating model if it is run as tourist accommodation rather than as a plain residential lease. That can affect the way the property is managed and how routine admin for foreign tenants is handled.

### Practical layer

In real listings, the market blurs those categories.

Some “serviced apartments” are very close to mini hotel products. Some are professionally managed residences built for business travelers and foreign staff. Some are ordinary furnished rentals where the landlord includes Wi-Fi and sends a cleaner twice a week.

On the other side, some “regular rentals” are not bare-bones at all. A furnished condo in Tay Ho, Thao Dien, or My An may come with internet, a cleaner, or building support even though it is still basically a normal lease.

That is why the safer question is:

**What exactly is included, who manages the unit, what contract is being signed, and how easy is it to leave if the place is wrong?**

## Price difference: what the premium usually buys

In most cases, serviced apartments cost more month to month than ordinary long-term rentals in the same broad area.

That premium usually buys some combination of:

- faster move-in
- cleaning or housekeeping
- internet and sometimes water
- furniture and kitchen basics already in place
- lower setup friction
- more flexible stay length
- a management desk or more organized operator

A regular rental usually gives lower monthly cost for the space, especially once the stay moves past a few months.

## Rough 2026 price bands by city

These are decision ranges, not fixed tariffs.

### Hanoi

- **Serviced apartment:** often around **10-18 million VND/month** for simpler studios or small one-bedrooms, with better-known buildings and larger one-bedrooms often moving into roughly **18-35+ million VND/month**
- **Regular rental:** a basic to mid-range furnished one-bedroom often lands around **8-15 million VND/month**, while better buildings and larger units can move into roughly **15-30+ million VND/month**

Serviced demand and supply are especially visible in areas like Tay Ho, Ba Dinh, Cau Giay, and parts of Nam Tu Liem. Regular rental choice is broader across the city. For neighborhood trade-offs, see [Hanoi neighborhoods for expats](/vietnam/housing/best-neighborhoods-hanoi-expats-2026).

### Ho Chi Minh City

- **Serviced apartment:** often around **12-25 million VND/month** for smaller central or expat-area stock, with stronger managed buildings and larger units often running **25-45+ million VND/month**
- **Regular rental:** a furnished one-bedroom in common expat search areas often starts around **10-18 million VND/month**, with newer or better-located stock moving into **18-35+ million VND/month**

Serviced stock is especially common in District 1, parts of District 3, Binh Thanh, and Thao Dien-style expat zones. Regular rentals open up a much larger pool once lease length is flexible enough. For area fit, see [where expats live in HCMC](/vietnam/housing/best-neighborhoods-hcmc-expats-2026).

### Da Nang

- **Serviced apartment:** often around **7-15 million VND/month** for simpler studios and one-bedrooms in beachside expat areas, with higher-end managed residences often moving into **15-30+ million VND/month**
- **Regular rental:** a regular one-bedroom or small two-bedroom can often be found around **6-12 million VND/month**, with better buildings and houses rising from there

Da Nang has a wider mix of low-cost serviced-style mini-buildings and more polished managed stock than many newcomers expect. My An / An Thuong and Hai Chau are common starting points. For area fit, see [best neighborhoods in Da Nang for expats](/vietnam/housing/best-neighborhoods-danang-expats-2026).

## What is usually included

### Serviced apartment: more likely to include

- furniture and appliances already in place
- internet
- basic kitchen equipment
- regular cleaning or light housekeeping
- reception or building support in larger operations
- easier move-in with less shopping on day one

### Regular rental: more often handled separately

- electricity
- water
- internet
- cleaning
- kitchenware, linens, and small appliances
- minor setup tasks after move-in

This is still not automatic. Some serviced listings meter electricity separately. Some regular rentals include internet or occasional cleaning. The line-by-line rent breakdown matters more than the label.

## Lease flexibility: this is where serviced usually wins

This is one of the clearest decision points.

### Best fit for roughly 1-3 months

A serviced apartment is usually the safer choice.

Why:

- much easier to move in quickly
- lower chance of buying household items that will not be used long
- easier to leave if the neighborhood, noise, or building quality is wrong
- less dependence on a single landlord behaving well under time pressure

### Best fit for roughly 3-6 months

This is the overlap zone.

A serviced apartment still makes sense for people who want low friction, travel often, or do not want to furnish anything. A regular rental starts to make more sense for people who already know the city and want to lower monthly cost.

### Best fit for 12+ months

A regular rental usually wins on value.

At that point, the monthly serviced premium often stops making sense unless the tenant:

- wants a highly managed building
- needs cleaning and admin support built in
- is on a company housing budget
- expects frequent travel in and out of Vietnam
- values flexibility more than cost

## Foreigner admin: registration support matters more than many first-time renters expect

This deserves its own decision section.

Foreign tenants are not just comparing kitchens and rent. They are also comparing how smoothly the property handles move-in administration, especially temporary residence declaration.

Officially, the accommodation side is expected to declare a foreign guest or tenant's temporary residence. In practice, the renter experience depends on whether the operator or landlord actually knows the process and handles it properly.

A well-run serviced apartment often does this more routinely because foreign tenants are part of its normal business. That does **not** mean every serviced apartment is reliable. It also does **not** mean a regular rental cannot handle it. Some private landlords do it without trouble. Others delay, avoid it, or hand the problem to the tenant.

That makes this one of the best screening questions before paying a deposit:

- Who will handle temporary residence declaration?
- Has the property handled foreign tenants before?
- How soon after move-in is it done?
- What documents are needed from the tenant?
- Is the answer clear and routine, or vague and improvised?

For wider rental risk checks, see [how to avoid rental scams in Vietnam](/vietnam/housing/avoid-rental-scams-vietnam-2026) and the [rental viewing checklist](/vietnam/housing/rental-viewing-checklist-vietnam-2026).

## Typical lifestyle fit

### Serviced apartment usually fits better if

- this is the first arrival in Vietnam
- the stay may end after a few months
- work hours are heavy and setup time is limited
- a cleaner, building support, and low-friction move-in matter
- the renter is still testing neighborhoods
- company housing support is paying part of the bill
- the tenant wants to avoid buying household basics

### Regular rental usually fits better if

- the city and neighborhood are already known
- the stay is long enough for setup effort to pay off
- monthly budget control matters more than convenience
- the tenant wants more unit choice
- the tenant is comfortable checking contracts, bills, and landlord responsiveness
- the tenant may later buy extra furniture or household items

If the unit will need upgrades after move-in, see [how to furnish an apartment in Vietnam](/vietnam/housing/furnish-apartment-vietnam-expats-2026).

## Where each option is easier to find

### Serviced apartments

Usually easier to find in:

- foreigner-heavy business and embassy zones
- central districts with short-stay demand
- beachside expat areas in Da Nang
- buildings that already market to business travelers or foreign professionals
- agent inventories focused on furnished, move-in-ready stock

### Regular rentals

Usually easier to find in:

- standard condo towers
- larger residential compounds
- local-owner apartment stock
- long-stay family neighborhoods
- buildings where the landlord expects a longer commitment

As a search method, serviced stock is usually easier through agent portfolios, aparthotel websites, and furnished-rental filters. Regular rentals are easier once the search opens to broader condo stock and longer lease terms.

## How to decide: the cleanest rule of thumb

Choose a **serviced apartment** if the answer to several of these is yes:

- the stay may be under six months
- the city or neighborhood is still uncertain
- setup time is limited
- built-in cleaning and easier admin are worth paying for
- flexibility matters more than squeezing rent lower

Choose a **regular rental** if the answer to several of these is yes:

- the stay is likely a year or more
- the neighborhood is already chosen
- monthly budget efficiency matters
- some setup work is acceptable
- the tenant wants more control over bills, furniture, and unit selection

## A useful middle-ground approach

A common pattern for new arrivals is:

1. take a serviced apartment for the first few weeks or first one to three months
2. use that period to test commute, noise, flood risk, and neighborhood fit
3. then move into a regular rental once the long-stay choice is clearer

This costs more at the start, but it often reduces the chance of getting locked into the wrong long-stay lease too early.

For broader budget planning, see [cost of living in Vietnam in 2026](/vietnam/daily-life/cost-of-living-vietnam-2026).

## Questions to ask before signing either type

- What exactly is included in rent?
- Is electricity billed separately, and at what rate?
- Is internet included?
- How often is cleaning provided, if any?
- What deposit is required?
- What is the minimum stay?
- What is the notice period to leave early?
- Who handles temporary residence declaration for foreign tenants?
- Who manages maintenance, and how quickly is it usually handled?
- Is the unit the exact one shown in photos, or only a sample unit?

## The bottom line

For a first landing, uncertain stay, or high-friction work schedule, a serviced apartment often earns its premium.

For a stable long stay, a regular rental usually gives better value.

The best decision is usually not based on the words “serviced” or “regular.” It is based on **stay length, what is bundled, how much setup friction is acceptable, and whether the landlord or operator can handle foreign-tenant admin cleanly.**
