This is a navigation guide to Vietnam's medical system for foreigners. It covers how the three-tier hospital structure actually works, which hospitals expats use in each major city, what care typically costs, what your insurance options look like in practice, and what to do in an emergency. It does not cover specific medical conditions, pregnancy and childbirth, or detailed insurance product comparisons — those will be addressed in separate articles in this section.

A lot has changed in 2025. Vietnam's social insurance law was rewritten and new rules for foreign workers took effect on 1 July 2025. The first major international-standard private hospital began accepting Vietnamese social health insurance for outpatient and emergency care. The administrative reorganisation that abolished the District level on 1 July 2025 also changed how some hospital addresses are written. This article reflects the post-reform situation as of April 2026.

If you remember one thing: Vietnam's medical care is genuinely good and genuinely affordable by international standards, but the system has its own logic that does not match what most foreigners are used to. The cost of not understanding it is usually money and time, occasionally a bad outcome.

## The Three-Tier System

Vietnamese healthcare has three layers, and where you go depends as much on what kind of problem you have as on how much you can pay.

**Public hospitals (Bệnh viện công)** are government-run, heavily subsidised, and where the vast majority of Vietnamese receive care. The top public hospitals — Bach Mai in Hanoi, Cho Ray in Ho Chi Minh City, Da Nang General Hospital — are not bad places to be treated. The doctors are often highly skilled, and many of them moonlight at private hospitals in the evenings. The problem for foreigners is the patient experience around the medicine, not the medicine itself: crowded waiting areas, opaque queue systems, separate windows for registration and payment and pharmacy, signage almost entirely in Vietnamese, and front-line staff who often speak no English. Most expats only use public hospitals in two situations: a true emergency where the closest hospital is the right hospital, or a specialised procedure that only a particular public hospital is equipped to perform. Cho Ray Hospital in HCMC has a dedicated foreigner floor (10th floor) with English-speaking staff for international patients, and a few other top public hospitals have similar arrangements.

**Private hospitals (Bệnh viện tư)** are where the Vietnamese middle class and most resident expats actually go for routine and serious care. The price is meaningfully higher than public hospitals but still a fraction of Western costs, the facilities are modern, the wait times are reasonable, and English support exists at varying levels of fluency. This tier includes the Vinmec network (operated by Vingroup, with two JCI-accredited facilities), Hoan My (the largest private hospital network in Vietnam with over 15 hospitals), Tam Anh, Hong Ngoc, Family Medical Practice, and others. Within this tier there is a wide quality range — Vinmec Times City in Hanoi and Vinmec Central Park in HCMC are essentially equivalent to international hospitals, while smaller regional private hospitals can be inconsistent.

**International hospitals and clinics** are the top tier, explicitly built for expats, medical tourists, and high-income Vietnamese. FV Hospital in HCMC was the first JCI-accredited hospital in Southern Vietnam and is 100% foreign-owned. Hanoi French Hospital (operated as Hanoi French Hospital, formerly Vietnam International Hospital) has been serving Hanoi's diplomatic community since 1997. Raffles Medical (the Singapore group) and Columbia Asia operate clinics in major cities. American International Hospital opened in HCMC's District 2 in 2018. At this tier, English is not just available — it is the default. Insurance direct billing with major international insurers is standard. Prices are roughly 50–70% lower than equivalent care in the US or Europe but 2–4 times higher than top private hospitals in Vietnam.

The line between these tiers is not always clean. Vinmec is technically a private hospital network but operates two facilities at international standards. FV Hospital is technically an international hospital but, as of July 2025, is also recognised as a primary healthcare facility under Vietnam's social insurance system — meaning it now accepts the local BHYT card alongside international insurance. The practical question is not "is this hospital private or international" but "does this hospital have what I need, in a language I can use, at a price I can pay."

## Hospitals by City

The hospitals listed below are the ones expats actually use. Addresses are given by street rather than by ward where possible, because the 1 July 2025 administrative reorganisation changed many ward and district names while leaving street addresses intact. Phone numbers and operating hours change frequently — always confirm via the hospital's official website before relying on this list for an emergency.

### Hanoi

For top-tier care, the two anchor choices are **Vinmec Times City International Hospital** (458 Minh Khai Street, Hai Ba Trung area) and **Hanoi French Hospital** (1 Phuong Mai Street, Dong Da area). Vinmec Times City is part of the Vingroup network, JCI-accredited, with 600 beds and 32 specialties including oncology, cardiology, and organ transplantation. It is the strongest single facility in northern Vietnam for serious or specialised care. Hanoi French Hospital has been operating since 1997 and remains the historical default for the embassy and diplomatic community, with strong reputation for general care, English and French language support, and a long track record of treating foreigners.

For routine outpatient care and minor emergencies, **Family Medical Practice Hanoi** (298 I Kim Ma Street, Ba Dinh area, in the Van Phuc Diplomatic Compound) is the most popular clinic-based option. It is run by foreign doctors, has 24/7 emergency services, and serves as a triage point for many expats — they will treat what they can and refer you onward if needed. **Raffles Medical Hanoi** offers similar clinic-level care under the Singapore group's quality standards.

For mid-range private care, **Tam Anh General Hospital** (108 Hoang Nhu Tiep Street) and **Hong Ngoc Hospital** (with multiple branches across the city) are well-regarded Vietnamese-run hospitals that handle a wide range of specialties at lower prices than Vinmec. Both have international patient departments with at least basic English support.

For backup public-hospital options, **Bach Mai Hospital** (78 Giai Phong Street) is one of Vietnam's most respected public hospitals and the right choice for certain rare conditions, but expect a Vietnamese-language environment and long waits.

### Ho Chi Minh City

HCMC has the deepest expat hospital ecosystem in Vietnam. **FV Hospital** (6 Nguyen Luong Bang Street, in the Phu My Hung area of District 7) is the historic top choice — it was the first JCI-accredited hospital in southern Vietnam, is 100% foreign-owned, and has a 24/7 emergency department staffed with English-speaking paramedics. As of 1 July 2025, FV is also designated as a primary healthcare facility under Vietnam's social insurance system, which is covered in the insurance section below.

**Vinmec Central Park** (208 Nguyen Huu Canh Street, in the Binh Thanh area near Vinhomes Central Park) is JCI-accredited and partnered with Cleveland Clinic. It is generally reported to be 30–40% cheaper than FV for comparable procedures and is particularly popular with the Thao Dien expat community given the proximity. **American International Hospital** (199 Nguyen Hoang Street, District 2) opened in 2018 and is built around US healthcare protocols, with American, Australian, and European patients as its core clientele.

For Hoan My (the largest private network in Vietnam), the HCMC flagship is **City International Hospital (CIH)** in the Binh Tan area, JCI-accredited, with 21 clinical specialties and a foreign patient share of around 25%. It sits one tier below FV and Vinmec on price but is still solid quality.

**Family Medical Practice** has multiple HCMC locations, with the Diamond Plaza branch (34 Le Duan Street, District 1) being the most central. It serves the same triage function it does in Hanoi.

**Cho Ray Hospital** (201 Nguyen Chi Thanh Street, District 5) is the leading public hospital in southern Vietnam. The 10th floor is specifically dedicated to foreign patients with English-speaking staff and is worth knowing about for serious cases where Cho Ray's specialists are the right call.

### Da Nang

Da Nang's hospital ecosystem is much smaller than the two major cities, but it is more than adequate for expats living in central Vietnam. **Vinmec Da Nang International Hospital** (30/4 Street, in the Hai Chau area) opened in 2017 as the sixth hospital in the Vinmec network. It is the clear top choice for premium care in central Vietnam, with the highest English proficiency, the most modern imaging equipment, and direct billing arrangements with most international insurance providers.

**Family Hospital Da Nang** (73 Nguyen Huu Tho Street) — locally known as Bệnh viện Gia Đình — is the second anchor and is popular among long-term expats who want quality care without Vinmec's price markup. It has a dedicated VIP and International wing and a strong reputation for maternity care, located near the airport.

**Hoan My Da Nang Hospital** (291 Nguyen Van Linh Street) is part of the largest private network in Vietnam and offers a balance of quality and affordability one tier below Vinmec and Family Hospital. It has a dedicated international patient desk, though English proficiency among general nursing staff is inconsistent.

**Family Medical Practice Da Nang** (50–52 Nguyen Van Linh Street) covers the same outpatient and minor emergency role as in Hanoi and HCMC. It is the right first call for non-urgent issues like fever, stomach problems, or minor injuries.

For Hoi An residents, the realistic choice for anything serious is to come to Da Nang. The local **Hoi An Hospital** is suitable for very minor issues, vaccinations, and stitches, but for surgery, complex diagnostics, or serious conditions, the 30-minute drive to Da Nang is the right call. **Pacific Hospital** in Hoi An has an international section but reviews are mixed, particularly around upfront payment requirements. For more on living in Hoi An specifically, see [Cost of Living in Hoi An](/vietnam/daily-life/cost-of-living-hoi-an-2026).

### Outside the major cities

If you live outside Hanoi, HCMC, or Da Nang, the realistic plan for any non-trivial medical issue is to travel to one of these three cities. There are private hospitals in Nha Trang, Vung Tau, and Phu Quoc with growing capacity, but the depth and consistency of expat-friendly care drops significantly outside the main hubs. Build this into your insurance choice and your living situation.

## Cost Reality

Costs in Vietnam are difficult to give as single numbers because they vary enormously by tier, by city, by whether you have insurance, and by whether you get quoted the local price or the international-patient price. The ranges below are broad and illustrative — always ask for a written estimate before any non-emergency procedure. For broader context on what other categories of monthly spending look like, see [Cost of Living in Vietnam](/vietnam/daily-life/cost-of-living-vietnam-2026).

**General consultation with a doctor:** Roughly 200,000–400,000 VND ($8–$16) at a public hospital, 400,000–1,200,000 VND ($16–$48) at a private hospital, and 1,500,000–2,400,000 VND ($60–$95) at an international hospital or international clinic. The international tier is roughly 50–70% lower than equivalent costs in the US or Europe.

**Specialist consultation:** Typically 30–80% higher than general consultation at the same tier.

**Common diagnostic tests:** A basic blood panel runs 300,000–800,000 VND ($12–$32) at a private hospital and 1,000,000–2,500,000 VND ($40–$100) at an international hospital. A standard ultrasound is in a similar range. A chest X-ray is usually 200,000–600,000 VND ($8–$24). MRI is the test where the gap widens most: roughly 2,000,000–5,000,000 VND ($80–$200) at a Vietnamese private hospital, 5,000,000–12,000,000 VND ($200–$480) at an international hospital.

**Dental care:** This is where Vietnam is dramatically cheaper than the West. A routine cleaning runs 500,000–1,500,000 VND ($20–$60). A standard filling is 600,000–2,000,000 VND ($24–$80). A root canal is 2,500,000–8,000,000 VND ($100–$320). A crown is 5,000,000–15,000,000 VND ($200–$600) for ceramic, more for premium materials. Implants run 25,000,000–70,000,000 VND ($1,000–$2,800) per tooth. Vietnam's dental sector attracts significant medical tourism for these reasons, particularly in HCMC and Hanoi.

**Emergency department visit:** A non-admitted ER visit at a private hospital typically lands in the 1,500,000–5,000,000 VND ($60–$200) range, plus the cost of any tests, medications, or procedures. International hospitals can be 2–3 times this. A serious case requiring admission can run from a few hundred dollars to several thousand depending on the length of stay and the procedures required.

**Hospital admission day rates:** A standard private room at a Vietnamese private hospital is roughly 1,500,000–4,500,000 VND ($60–$180) per day. International hospitals typically charge 3,500,000–10,000,000 VND ($140–$400) per day for a standard private room, with VIP suites going considerably higher.

**Surgery:** Surgical costs vary so widely that any single number is misleading. Common reference points: a routine appendectomy at a private hospital might run $1,500–$3,500; at an international hospital $3,000–$8,000. Knee or hip replacement at an international hospital is typically $8,000–$15,000. These are roughly 60–80% lower than US prices for comparable quality.

*Sources: Pacific Prime Vietnam healthcare cost reports (2024–2025), MedicalTourism.vn published rates, individual hospital published price schedules. Always confirm current rates directly with the hospital.*

## Health Insurance Reality

There is no single right answer for health insurance in Vietnam. The right answer depends on your visa status, your length of stay, your age, your dependents, and your appetite for risk. Below are the four real options, with the trade-offs of each.

**Option 1: Pay out of pocket, no insurance.** This is what a meaningful number of short-stay nomads and budget-conscious expats actually do. The logic is that a routine doctor visit and most prescription medications cost less than the monthly premium for travel insurance, so for a healthy person under 40 the math can work out. The risk is asymmetric: if you have a motorbike accident or get diagnosed with something serious, the bill at an international hospital can be tens of thousands of dollars, and a medical evacuation to Bangkok or Singapore without insurance can run $25,000 to over $100,000. Going uninsured is a defensible choice for short stays and low-risk lifestyles. It is a much harder choice for anyone with dependents, anyone over 50, or anyone who rides a motorbike daily — see the [Vietnam Motorbike Guide](/vietnam/daily-life/motorbike-guide-2026) and [Vietnam Driver's License Guide](/vietnam/daily-life/vietnam-drivers-license-2026) for context on the risks involved.

**Option 2: Travel medical insurance.** This is the default for digital nomads and most short-to-medium-term expats. It covers emergency medical treatment, hospitalisation, and medical evacuation, but it does not cover routine check-ups, dental cleanings, pre-existing conditions, or maternity care. The flagship product in this category is [SafetyWing Nomad Insurance](https://safetywing.com/?referenceID=26505542&utm_source=26505542&utm_medium=Ambassador), which starts at $56.28 per 4 weeks for travelers aged 10–39 and works on a subscription model that can be started while already abroad — useful if you arrived in Vietnam without coverage and decided you wanted some. Other products exist in this category with different coverage rules and price points. Travel medical insurance is the best fit for nomads who do not need preventive care and who want a financial backstop against the worst case.

**Option 3: International health insurance.** Plans from Cigna Global, Allianz Care, Bupa Global, Aetna International, and Pacific Cross are full-service health insurance — they cover routine care, preventive screenings, dental, maternity, and pre-existing conditions (depending on policy and underwriting). They cost 3–10 times more than travel medical insurance and require annual commitments. They are the right choice for long-term expats with families, for anyone over 50, for anyone with chronic conditions, and for anyone whose employer reimburses health insurance. Direct billing arrangements with the major Vietnamese international hospitals are standard for most of these providers.

**Option 4: Vietnamese national health insurance (BHYT).** This is the option most expat content sites get wrong, because the rules changed materially in 2025. Under the 2024 Social Insurance Law and Decree 158/2025/ND-CP, both effective 1 July 2025, foreign nationals working in Vietnam under a fixed-term labour contract of at least 12 months are subject to compulsory social insurance, which includes BHYT. There are three exemptions: intra-company transferees, workers who have reached the statutory retirement age at the time of signing the contract, and cases covered by an international treaty to which Vietnam is signatory. Total contributions run to roughly 30% of the salary used as the basis for social insurance, with about 9.5% paid by the employee and about 20.5% paid by the employer — the BHYT health insurance portion alone is 1.5% of salary on the employee side and 3% on the employer side. In return, the foreign worker receives a BHYT card and the same health insurance benefits as Vietnamese workers. If you are uncertain which work-authorisation pathway puts you into this category, see the [Vietnam Visa Guide](/vietnam/visa/visa-guide-2026) and the [TRC Application Guide](/vietnam/visa/trc-application-guide-2026) for the underlying visa structure.

For most of the past decade, the practical limitation of BHYT for foreigners was that it only meaningfully covered care at public hospitals — and most expats avoided public hospitals for the language and queueing reasons described above. **This changed on 1 July 2025**, when FV Hospital in HCMC was officially recognised by the Ho Chi Minh City Department of Health and Ho Chi Minh City Social Insurance as a designated primary healthcare facility under the BHYT system. As FV's official announcement explains, BHYT now covers 100% of eligible costs — within the entitlements set by the Ministry of Health — for services at FV's Emergency Department and all outpatient clinics, for patients who have registered FV as their primary healthcare facility, who are receiving emergency care, who hold a valid referral letter, or who are being treated for conditions listed in the List of Conditions Eligible for Treatment at the Primary Level under Circular 01/2025/TT-BYT. Coverage also extends to specific inpatient procedures including Cathlab procedures, knee/hip/shoulder replacement surgery, and certain ophthalmology surgeries. Patients can stack BHYT with private insurance and international insurance simultaneously.

This is a meaningful structural change. For foreign workers in HCMC who are mandatorily enrolled in BHYT under the new 2025 rules, FV Hospital is now the first international-tier private hospital in Vietnam where the BHYT card actually gets you access to international-standard care at significant cost reduction. Whether other international hospitals will follow FV's lead is unclear as of April 2026, but the precedent is set.

The honest summary on insurance: if you are a digital nomad on a tourist visa, travel medical insurance is the right default. If you are a long-term expat employed by a Vietnamese entity, you are now (since July 2025) required to participate in BHYT, and FV Hospital makes that more useful than it has historically been — but most long-term expats still maintain a parallel international or travel insurance policy because BHYT alone does not cover the full network of international hospitals or all services.

*Sources: Vietnam Social Insurance Law 2024 (Articles 33–34), Decree 158/2025/ND-CP, Vietnam Social Security (vss.gov.vn), FV Hospital official announcement (July 2025).*

## Emergency Situations

Vietnam's national emergency number for medical situations is **115**. It works from any phone, mobile or landline, and the call is free. There are also separate numbers for police (113) and fire (114).

The thing nobody mentions until it is too late: **emergency line operators in Vietnam generally speak only Vietnamese**. The UK government's official travel advice for Vietnam states this directly. If you need to call 115 and you do not speak Vietnamese, your realistic options are to ask a Vietnamese person nearby to make the call, to use Google Translate's voice feature to communicate the basic facts (location, what happened, how many injured), or — and this is what most experienced expats actually do — to call the direct 24/7 emergency hotline of the international hospital you would want to go to anyway. Vinmec, FV Hospital, Family Medical Practice, and Hanoi French Hospital all maintain English-speaking 24/7 emergency hotlines and can dispatch their own ambulances. This is often faster than 115, especially in major cities, and it ensures the ambulance takes you to the hospital you actually want.

A few things to know about the realistic emergency experience in Vietnam:

**Ambulance response times are inconsistent.** In central Hanoi, HCMC, and Da Nang, response times for both 115 and private hospital ambulances are usually within 15–30 minutes. Outside the central districts, in heavy traffic, or in rural areas, it can be much longer. If you can move the patient and you are in a major city, taking a Grab car directly to the emergency room of a known hospital is often faster than waiting for an ambulance. If the patient cannot be moved or the situation is genuinely life-threatening (loss of consciousness, severe bleeding, suspected stroke or heart attack), call for an ambulance and give your exact location with landmarks.

**The ambulance does not necessarily go where you want.** Public 115 ambulances will typically take patients to the nearest public hospital. If you want to be treated at FV or Vinmec, calling that hospital's private ambulance service directly is the only way to ensure you end up there.

**Medical evacuation is a real option for serious cases.** For conditions that exceed Vietnam's medical capacity, the standard evacuation destinations are Bangkok (typically Bumrungrad International Hospital), Singapore (Mount Elizabeth or Raffles Hospital), or your home country if your insurance covers it. Both Bangkok and Singapore are roughly 2–3 hours by air from Vietnam's main cities. Without insurance coverage, a single medical evacuation can cost $25,000 to over $100,000 depending on the destination and the medical team required. Most travel medical insurance and all serious international health insurance plans include evacuation coverage up to a stated limit — verify the limit before you need it.

**Build a personal emergency kit.** Keep digital and printed copies of: your passport biographical page and visa stamp, your insurance card and policy number with the 24/7 claims hotline, a list of your current medications with dosages, a list of allergies, your blood type if you know it, and emergency contact information for at least one person in Vietnam and one person in your home country. Save the direct emergency hotline numbers for the international hospital nearest to you in your phone.

## Common Pitfalls

Expats who get into trouble with Vietnamese healthcare usually do so for one of a few predictable reasons. Knowing them in advance helps.

**Prescription medication rules are looser in Vietnam than in most Western countries, but not for everything.** Many medications that require a prescription in the US or Europe — including some antibiotics — are available over the counter at Vietnamese pharmacies. This makes routine refills convenient and cheap. The exceptions matter: controlled substances, certain psychiatric medications, and some opioids are tightly regulated, and bringing them into Vietnam without proper documentation can create legal problems. If you take any controlled medication regularly, carry your home-country prescription and ideally a doctor's letter explaining the medical need.

**Private hospitals sometimes recommend tests that are not strictly necessary.** The incentive structure at private hospitals — where doctors and the hospital both benefit from additional procedures — occasionally leads to test recommendations that a foreign doctor might consider over-cautious. This is not unique to Vietnam, but the scale can be larger. If a recommended test feels excessive, it is reasonable to ask "what would change in my treatment plan if this test came back normal" before agreeing to it. A second opinion from a different hospital is cheap enough that it is often worth getting for any expensive recommendation.

**Public hospital workflow is different from what you are used to.** At Vietnamese public hospitals, registration, consultation, payment, lab tests, and pharmacy pickup are all separate windows in different parts of the building. There is no single point of contact who walks you through the process. If you are sent to a public hospital and do not speak Vietnamese, bring someone who does — even a Grab driver or hotel concierge who can help with translation can make a significant difference.

**Insurance pre-authorisation versus pay-and-claim is a huge practical difference.** Some international insurance plans have direct billing arrangements with specific Vietnamese hospitals — meaning the hospital bills the insurer directly and you pay nothing or only your co-pay at discharge. Others require you to pay everything upfront and submit receipts for reimbursement, which can mean fronting tens of thousands of dollars for a serious case. Before any planned procedure, call your insurer and ask: is this hospital in your direct billing network, and what pre-authorisation do I need? For emergencies, this is harder, but having a copy of your insurance card and policy number on you at all times is the minimum.

**Dental tourism is real and mostly works, with caveats.** Vietnam attracts foreigners who fly in specifically for dental work because the price difference is so dramatic. The quality at top dental clinics in HCMC and Hanoi (Westcoast International Dental Clinic, Elite Dental, and others) is genuinely high. The risks are: complications during recovery happen after you have flown home, follow-up appointments require return trips, and if something goes wrong with implant work years later, sorting out warranty and remediation across borders is difficult. Dental tourism makes sense for cleanings, fillings, and routine work; it is more complicated for major implant or cosmetic work where long-term follow-up is part of the value.

## Bottom Line

Vietnam's healthcare system is one of the country's underrated strengths for expats. The top private and international hospitals deliver care at a quality genuinely comparable to Western standards, at prices that make routine medical needs trivial and serious medical needs manageable. The 2025 reforms — both the mandatory inclusion of foreign workers in BHYT and FV Hospital's adoption of BHYT primary care status — are quietly making the system more accessible than it has ever been for the foreign community.

The right strategy depends on your circumstances. A short-stay nomad on a tourist visa is best served by travel medical insurance with clearly defined emergency and evacuation coverage, plus a clear plan for which international hospital to go to in each city you visit. A long-term expat employed by a Vietnamese entity will be enrolled in BHYT by their employer and should consider whether an additional international or top-up policy makes sense for their family situation. A retiree or family with dependents should seriously consider full international health insurance with direct billing at one of the major international hospitals.

Whatever your strategy, the most important thing you can do before you actually need care is to know which hospital you would go to, save its 24/7 emergency line in your phone, and confirm that your insurance is accepted there. Healthcare emergencies are not the time to be researching options.
