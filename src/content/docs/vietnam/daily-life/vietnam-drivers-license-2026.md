If you've been reading older expat blogs about converting your foreign driver's license to a Vietnamese one — almost all of them are out of date. **On March 1, 2025, Vietnam transferred driver's license issuance from the Ministry of Transport to the Ministry of Public Security.** That single change moved every conversion office, replaced the legal framework, introduced new application forms, lowered the fee, and added digital license integration through the VNeID national ID app.

This guide walks through the new process as it actually works in 2026, with verified office addresses for Hanoi, Ho Chi Minh City, and Da Nang (which now also serves Hoi An residents after the July 2025 administrative merger). For the legal background on why you need a Vietnamese license at all — IDP rules, the 1968 vs 1949 Vienna Convention trap, insurance implications — see our [complete motorbike guide](/vietnam/daily-life/motorbike-guide-2026). This article focuses on the operational how.

## What Changed in March 2025 (and Why Old Guides Are Wrong)

Before March 2025, foreign license conversion happened at the provincial Department of Transport (Sở Giao thông Vận tải, or Sở GTVT). You'd visit addresses like 16 Cao Ba Quat in Hanoi or 252 Ly Chinh Thang in HCMC, submit paperwork to Ministry of Transport staff, and receive a license issued under Circular 12/2017/TT-BGTVT.

That entire system is gone. As of March 1, 2025, under Government Decree 02/2025/ND-CP, the Ministry of Public Security (Bộ Công An) took over all driver's license testing, issuance, and conversion. The new legal framework is **Circular 12/2025/TT-BCA**, signed February 28, 2025 and effective March 1. The application form is now in Appendix XIII of that circular, not Appendix 20 of the old one. And the agency you deal with is the **Traffic Police Division (Phòng Cảnh sát giao thông, or PC08)** at the provincial level, plus authorized commune-level police offices.

A few additional changes worth knowing about up front:

- The standard issuance fee dropped from 135,000 VND to **115,000 VND per issuance** under Circular 154/2025/TT-BTC, effective January 1, 2026
- Foreigners converting based on visa or TRC validity are now **exempt from the driver health check** under Article 24 of Circular 12/2025
- Approved licenses are automatically synced to **VNeID**, Vietnam's national digital ID app, and the digital version is accepted by traffic police
- Processing time is now standardized at **5 working days** from valid submission

If a website, expat forum post, or agent tells you to go to the old Sở GTVT addresses or quotes the old 135,000 VND fee, that source is at least a year out of date. Be cautious about everything else they're telling you.

## Quick Decision: Do You Actually Need to Do This?

Before walking through the conversion process, three quick questions to make sure this is the right path for you:

**1. Are you eligible?** You need a residence document valid for at least 3 months from the date of application. Acceptable documents include a Temporary Residence Card (TRC), Permanent Residence Card, work visa (LD1/LD2), investor visa (DT1–DT4), family visa (TT), or diplomatic/official ID. **Standard 90-day e-visas and tourist visas (DL) do not qualify.** If you're on a tourist visa, your only legal options are an IDP from a 1968 Vienna Convention country or staying off motorbikes over 50cc entirely.

**2. Do you have a valid foreign license to convert?** Conversion (Path A below) requires an unexpired national driver's license from your home country, in good condition, with the vehicle classes clearly visible. Temporary licenses, IDPs alone, expired licenses, and damaged licenses cannot be converted. If you don't have a foreign license, your only option is taking the Vietnamese test from scratch (Path B), which is significantly harder.

**3. Is your foreign motorbike endorsement the right class?** If your home license only covers cars, converting it gives you a Vietnamese car license — not a motorbike license. To get the motorbike endorsement, you'd need to take the Vietnamese practical test for the A1 class. Many expats discover this only after submitting their paperwork. Check your home license carefully before starting.

If all three answers work, continue to Path A. If you have no convertible license, jump to Path B.

## Path A: Converting Your Foreign License (Most Common)

This is the route most expats take, and the process under Circular 12/2025 is genuinely simpler than the old system — fewer document requirements, faster processing, and a single point of contact at the Traffic Police.

### Documents You Need

Per Article 24 of Circular 12/2025/TT-BCA, you need to prepare one set containing:

1. **Application form** for foreign license conversion (the form in Appendix XIII of Circular 12/2025, available at the office or downloadable from the Cuc CSGT public services portal)
2. **Notarized Vietnamese translation** of your foreign driver's license, certified by a Vietnamese notary public office or by the Vietnamese embassy/consulate that prepared the translation, with the translator's seal stamped across the photocopy of your license
3. **Health certificate** from an approved medical facility — **this is waived if you're applying for a license that will match your visa or TRC validity**, which covers most expats. Bring it only if you specifically want a longer-term license, or for license categories that require it
4. **Certified copy of your residence document**: TRC, permanent residence card, diplomatic/official ID, or another residence permit valid for 3+ months
5. **Valid passport** with the Vietnam entry visa page

A few practical notes that save trouble. The translation must come from a licensed Vietnamese notary office (phòng công chứng) — translations done by general translation services or done abroad without consular legalization will be rejected. Cost is typically 100,000–200,000 VND per page and turnaround is 1–2 working days. The original foreign license stays with you throughout — unlike the old system, you keep your foreign license after conversion under Circular 12/2025.

### Where to Submit (Verified 2026 Addresses)

You submit your file at a Phòng Cảnh sát giao thông (PC08) office in your province of residence, or at any commune-level police office (Công an cấp xã) authorized to receive driver's license applications. As of early 2026, these are the verified primary intake points for the three main expat hubs:

**Hanoi** has two main PC08 motorbike license intake offices:

- Số 2 Phùng Hưng, phường Văn Quán, quận Hà Đông
- Số 253 Nguyễn Đức Thuận, thị trấn Trâu Quỳ, huyện Gia Lâm

In addition, Hanoi has 30+ commune and ward-level police offices authorized to receive driver's license applications. If you live far from Ha Dong or Gia Lam, ask your local ward police whether they're an authorized intake point.

**Ho Chi Minh City** consolidated to three primary PC08 offices when the Public Security takeover happened on March 1, 2025:

- 252 Lý Chính Thắng, Phường 9, Quận 3 (the old Sở GTVT location, now repurposed under PC08)
- 8 Nguyễn Ảnh Thủ, Phường Trung Mỹ Tây, Quận 12
- 111 Tân Sơn Nhì, Phường Tân Sơn Nhì, Quận Tân Phú

Note: HCMC's ward and district names changed under the July 2025 administrative reform that merged HCMC with Binh Duong and Ba Ria-Vung Tau, but the physical addresses above remain the same — only the underlying ward labels were renamed. Use the street addresses, not the district names, when navigating.

**Da Nang** has had two address changes in 2025, which has caused considerable confusion:

- From March 1, 2025: Da Nang City Police initially handled conversions at the city administrative one-stop center at 24 Trần Phú, Hải Châu
- **From April 21, 2025 onwards: 18 Võ An Ninh, phường Hòa Xuân, quận Cẩm Lệ** — this is the current Phòng CSGT headquarters and the verified intake point as of April 2026

The Da Nang office serves the entire merged Da Nang City, including the former Quang Nam province. **If you live in Hoi An**, this is now your conversion office — the old Quang Nam provincial offices in Tam Ky no longer process foreign license conversions. Hoi An residents should plan for a 30+ km trip into central Da Nang for this. Working hours are Monday to Friday, roughly 7:30–11:30 and 13:30–17:30, closed weekends.

*Sources: Tuoi Tre Vietnam (March 2025), Da Nang government portal (April 2025), HCMC PC08 official portal, Hanoi Phòng CSGT public information.*

### The Submission Process Step by Step

Once you're at the PC08 office with your documents:

1. **Take a number** at the reception desk. Most offices have a queue ticket system; some smaller commune offices handle walk-ins directly
2. **Submit your file** to the receiving officer. They'll verify each document against the original (you'll need to show originals for comparison even if you submitted certified copies)
3. **Get your photo taken on-site** — bring no photos of your own; they'll take a digital portrait at the office
4. **Pay the fee**: 115,000 VND as of January 2026, payable in cash. Some offices also accept e-wallet payment via the dichvucong.gov.vn portal
5. **Receive an appointment slip** with the date your license will be ready (typically 5 working days)

If the receiving officer flags any concerns about your immigration status, they have the authority to send your file for verification to the Immigration Department before processing — this can add 1–2 weeks. To avoid this, make sure your TRC or visa is clearly within validity and your passport stamps match your residence document.

After submission, your file gets transferred to the central Cục Cảnh sát giao thông (Traffic Police Department under the Ministry of Public Security) within one working day. The license is integrated into the national digital database within 3 working days, and the physical PET card is printed and ready for pickup within 5 working days from valid submission.

### Pickup and VNeID Sync

You can either return to the PC08 office to collect your physical license, or request postal delivery (around 20,000–40,000 VND extra). If you have a Level 2 verified VNeID account and a Vietnamese phone number, your license also automatically appears in the VNeID app within 3 working days of approval. Traffic police accept the digital VNeID version as legally equivalent to the physical card during stops.

The new Vietnamese license is valid for the **shortest** of three durations:

- The remaining validity of your TRC, visa, or residence document
- The remaining validity of your foreign license
- The maximum statutory term for that license class in Vietnam (10 years for A1)

This means if your TRC expires in 14 months, your new Vietnamese license also expires in 14 months — even if your home country license is valid for another decade. When your TRC is renewed, you'll need to update the Vietnamese license too.

- Day 1: Get Vietnamese translation of your foreign license (1–2 days)
- Day 2 or 3: Submit at PC08 office (1 hour at the office)
- Day 8 or 9: Pick up your new license (5 working days from submission)

Total elapsed time is usually about 1.5 weeks from start to finish.

## Path B: Taking the Vietnamese Test from Scratch

If you don't have a foreign license to convert, your only legal option is taking the Vietnamese theory and practical tests through a registered driving school, then receiving a fresh Vietnamese license. This path is significantly harder for non-Vietnamese speakers, and most expats who go this route either give up or hire a school that handles end-to-end coordination.

The honest reality of the theory test: **it is conducted only in Vietnamese, on a computer, with no English version, no translation app, and no interpreters allowed in the room**. The A1 motorbike test consists of 25 questions, of which you need 21 correct to pass. Vietnam also has a set of approximately 60 "critical questions" (câu hỏi điểm liệt) — getting any one of these wrong results in instant failure regardless of your other answers. Multiple agencies have claimed bilingual test interfaces are coming, but as of April 2026 none has materialized in practice.

The practical test is more accessible, though still challenging. For A1 (motorbike) you'll be tested on a figure-8 course, slow straight-line riding, obstacle avoidance, and uneven surface handling. You start with 100 points and lose 5 for each violation; 80 is the passing threshold. The figure-8 is the section that fails most foreign applicants, because the course is narrower and tighter than people expect.

Costs for Path B include school enrollment (typically $80–150 covering training, materials, and exam fees), plus state exam fees of 60,000 VND for theory and 70,000 VND for practical per attempt. A driving school will handle the document submission, schedule your tests, and (usually) provide some informal English support during preparation, even though the actual exam stays in Vietnamese.

Realistically, Path B only makes sense if you genuinely have no convertible foreign license, you're committed to Vietnam long-term, and either you can read enough Vietnamese to handle the test or you have a Vietnamese friend willing to help you study the official question bank for several weeks.

## Path C: Hiring an Agent

A middle path that many expats choose is hiring a Vietnamese visa or legal agent to handle the conversion paperwork. Agent fees in 2026 typically run $80–180 for end-to-end Path A coordination, including translation, document preparation, and accompanying you to the PC08 office for the photo and submission.

A legitimate agent saves time and reduces the stress of navigating Vietnamese-language paperwork, but you should know what a real agent does and does not do:

**What a legitimate agent does:**
- Translates your foreign license through their notary partner
- Helps complete the Vietnamese application form
- Schedules and accompanies you to the PC08 office
- Tracks your file and notifies you when the license is ready

**What a legitimate agent cannot do:**
- Get you a license without you being physically present for the photo
- Skip the PC08 office entirely
- Process applications in under 5 working days
- Get a license issued for someone whose visa/TRC doesn't qualify

**Red flags for fake licenses:** Any agent who says you don't need to appear in person, promises delivery in under 3 days, charges under $50 for "full service," or doesn't ask to see your original foreign license is selling fake documents. Fake Vietnamese licenses do circulate, and they fail when traffic police check the database against your name — at which point you face fines plus the original problem of being unlicensed. Stick to agents recommended by people you trust, and verify they're using a real notary office for translations.

## Insurance and Why This Matters

A Vietnamese license isn't just a legal document — it's the foundation of your insurance coverage. Both international travel insurers and domestic Vietnamese health insurers commonly deny motorbike accident claims for unlicensed drivers, regardless of fault. Our [motorbike guide](/vietnam/daily-life/motorbike-guide-2026) covers the insurance implications in detail, including why "I had an IDP" usually doesn't help if your IDP was issued under the wrong convention. The short version: if you're going to ride here long-term, the 1.5 weeks and $10–40 it takes to convert your license is one of the highest-ROI legal investments you can make in your time in Vietnam.

## License Categories: Which One You Get

Vietnam reorganized its license categories under the 2024 Road Traffic Order and Safety Law, in effect from January 1, 2025. The categories most relevant to expats:

- **A1**: motorbikes 50cc to 175cc — covers the vast majority of bikes expats ride (Honda Wave, Vision, Air Blade, SH 150, etc.)
- **A**: motorbikes over 175cc — needed for big bikes like the Honda CB500 or any "real" motorcycle
- **B1**: automatic transmission cars only, non-professional use
- **B**: manual and automatic cars, can be used for ride-share driving with appropriate visa

When you convert a foreign license, you receive the Vietnamese equivalent based on what your foreign license authorizes. A foreign motorbike-only license becomes A1 or A. A foreign car-only license becomes B1 or B but **not** any A class — you cannot ride a motorbike on a converted car license, and police treat this as unlicensed riding. To add a motorbike endorsement, you need to take the Vietnamese A1 practical test separately.

You can hold both A and B class licenses simultaneously, which is the normal setup for expats who drive both motorbikes and cars. The two licenses are issued separately and may be integrated into a single PET card or shown as two cards depending on your processing office.

## Renewal, Replacement, and Leaving Vietnam

**Renewal** of your Vietnamese license happens automatically when you renew your TRC or residence document. You bring the new TRC, your existing Vietnamese license, and your foreign license back to the PC08 office and request an updated card. The fee is the same 115,000 VND.

**If you lose your physical license**, you need a police report from the ward where it was lost before PC08 will issue a replacement. The replacement process takes the same 5 working days and costs 115,000 VND. Note that even if you lose the physical card, your VNeID digital version remains valid, which is one practical benefit of the new system.

**If you leave Vietnam permanently**, your Vietnamese license remains technically valid until the expiration tied to your former TRC, but it has no practical use abroad. Vietnam is a 1968 Vienna Convention signatory, so in theory a Vietnamese license could be used as the basis for a 1968 IDP for travel to other Convention countries, but this requires applying through Cục CSGT before you leave.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is the old Sở GTVT (Department of Transport) still where I go for driver's license conversion?** No. As of March 1, 2025, all driver's license functions transferred to the Ministry of Public Security. You now go to the Phòng Cảnh sát giao thông (PC08, the Traffic Police Division) of your provincial police, or to authorized commune-level police offices. If anyone tells you to go to a Sở GTVT address for license conversion, they're using information at least a year out of date.

**Can I convert my license on a tourist visa or 90-day e-visa?** No. You need a residence document valid for at least 3 months — TRC, work visa, investor visa, family visa, or diplomatic ID. Standard tourist visas and e-visas do not qualify under Article 24 of Circular 12/2025.

**Do I really not need a health check anymore?** Correct, **if** you're applying for a license that matches your TRC or visa validity, which is the standard case for expats. Article 24 of Circular 12/2025 specifically waives the health certificate for this scenario. You only need it if you want a longer-term license or fall into a special category.

**Does my license sync to VNeID automatically?** Yes, within 3 working days of approval, **if** you have a Level 2 verified VNeID account linked to a Vietnamese phone number. Many foreigners don't have VNeID set up, in which case you rely on the physical PET card. Setting up VNeID requires a Vietnamese ID number, which TRC holders can typically obtain.

**How much does it cost to convert my license in 2026?** The state fee is 115,000 VND ($4.50) under Circular 154/2025/TT-BTC effective January 1, 2026. With translation and photos, total out-of-pocket costs run $10–18 if you skip the optional health check, or $22–42 if you include it. Agents charge an additional $80–180 for end-to-end coordination.

**I live in Hoi An. Where do I go now?** Da Nang, specifically the Phòng CSGT office at 18 Võ An Ninh, phường Hòa Xuân, quận Cẩm Lệ. After the July 2025 merger of Quang Nam into Da Nang, the old Tam Ky offices no longer process foreign driver's license conversions. Plan for a 30+ km trip from Hoi An. See our [Cost of Living in Hoi An guide](/vietnam/daily-life/cost-of-living-hoi-an-2026) for context on the broader implications of the Quang Nam merger.

**Is my US, Canadian, Australian, or UK IDP valid in Vietnam in 2026?** No. Those countries are signatories to the 1949 Geneva Convention, not the 1968 Vienna Convention that Vietnam recognizes. For the full legal background on IDPs, see our [motorbike guide](/vietnam/daily-life/motorbike-guide-2026). The practical implication: if you're from one of those countries and want to ride legally, license conversion is your only path.

**Can I take the Vietnamese theory test in English?** No. As of April 2026, the official theory test is conducted only in Vietnamese on a government computer system. Some agencies have claimed bilingual interfaces are being developed, but none has been deployed. If you genuinely cannot convert a foreign license, hiring a driving school for Path B preparation is your only realistic option.
