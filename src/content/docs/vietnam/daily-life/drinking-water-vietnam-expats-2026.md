## The short answer

For most expat households in Vietnam, the best default is not to treat “city water” and “my kitchen tap” as the same decision.

A practical setup usually looks like this:

- **Short stays:** buy sealed small bottles or 5L bottles from a supermarket or delivery app.
- **Most long-term rentals:** use **18.5L/19L bottle delivery** for direct drinking, and use normal household water for washing and other non-drinking tasks.
- **Longer stays with kitchen control:** consider an **under-sink RO system** for direct drinking and for drinks or dishes where water taste matters.
- **Whole-house filtration:** usually only makes sense for a **house, villa, or a property with clear water issues across multiple taps**, not as the default move for a normal apartment renter.

For cooking, the clean split is:

- use **drinking-grade water** for direct drinking, tea, coffee, and dishes where the water becomes part of the final taste, such as rice or soup
- use **cold tap water that you heat yourself** for boiling tasks only if the building water looks and smells normal
- do **not** start from hot tap water for drinking or cooking

*Price examples below are approximate, checked against official brand pages and major retailer listings in April 2026. Promotions, delivery terms, deposits, and installation scope can change by city and seller.*

## What the official standard does and does not tell you

Vietnam does have an official domestic clean-water standard. That matters, because this topic should not be framed as if there is no regulatory baseline at all.

But that still does **not** answer the household question an expat actually has: **is the water coming out of this specific apartment or house the right source for direct drinking?**

That is where the practical layer starts.

The real decision can still be affected by:

- building storage tanks
- internal plumbing quality
- whether the apartment has been empty for a while
- whether the landlord installed a drinking-water system and actually maintains it
- whether the household trusts bottle delivery or a filter more than the building tap

That is why “there is an official standard” and “drink straight from the apartment tap” should not be collapsed into one recommendation.

## Option 1: sealed small bottles and 5L bottles

This is the easiest setup for a new arrival, a short stay, a serviced apartment, or anyone who does not want equipment, deposits, or maintenance.

Common use cases:

- first week after arrival
- Airbnb or serviced apartment stays
- backup water when delivery is delayed
- households that do not drink enough water to justify a dispenser or filter install

Typical 2026 price anchors:

- **500ml bottles:** around **5,000-6,000 VND each**
- **24-bottle 500ml case:** around **75,000-121,000 VND**, depending on brand and retailer
- **1.5L bottles:** around **8,000-12,000 VND each**, depending on brand, retailer, and promotion
- **12-bottle 1.5L case:** around **85,000-108,000 VND**
- **5L multi-bottle packs:** around **95,000 VND for 4 bottles** on common La Vie retail listings

Where to get it:

- WinMart
- Bách Hóa Xanh
- convenience stores
- same-day grocery delivery apps covered in our [grocery shopping guide](/vietnam/daily-life/grocery-shopping-vietnam-expats-2026)
- delivery apps covered in our [Vietnam essential apps guide](/vietnam/daily-life/vietnam-essential-apps-2026)

In major-city service areas, stocked bottled-water items can arrive fast enough for same-day use, so this is the best low-friction starting point.

Common brands readers will see often include La Vie, Aquafina, Vĩnh Hảo, and Satori.

Best for:

- direct drinking
- guest backup
- short-term stays
- households that want the least setup work

Weak points:

- the cost per liter is usually worse than 18.5L/19L delivery
- plastic use adds up quickly
- constant reordering gets old in a long-term rental

## Option 2: 18.5L/19L delivery and a dispenser

For many long-term renters, this is the most balanced setup.

It usually gives a better cost-per-liter than small bottles, needs much less installation effort than RO, and works well for direct drinking if the delivery source is trusted.

A practical 2026 anchor:

- current official La Vie large-bottle listings sit at roughly **60,000-73,000 VND per bottle**, depending on line and region
- some new-customer official offers bundle **3 bottles for about 201,000-216,000 VND**
- official product pages commonly require **at least 3 bottles per order**
- **bottle deposit or shell handling may apply** for new customers
- independent delivery services may add **stair-carry fees** if there is no elevator

Where to get it:

- official brand ordering pages such as La Vie’s large-bottle channel
- building Facebook groups or resident chats
- local water-delivery services such as iWater or The Water MAN
- neighborhood suppliers that already serve the building

This setup is best when the household wants:

- drinking water at home every day
- no kitchen modification
- lower hassle than buying small bottles every few days
- a workable middle ground between convenience and recurring cost

Two practical cautions matter here.

First, **official brand delivery and mixed reseller delivery are not the same thing**. If the household wants a clearly official channel, start with the brand’s own page.

Second, **Aquafina search results need extra care in the large-bottle/dispenser category**. Aquafina is easy to buy as sealed small bottled water, but “Aquafina dispenser” or “Aquafina 19L/20L” searches often run through reseller or mixed-brand pages. Verify the current source before assuming it is a brand-owned large-bottle program.

Best for:

- daily direct drinking
- long-term apartment renters
- households that do not want to install equipment
- families that already have a dispenser

Weak points:

- bottle storage takes space
- deposit rules and return logistics can be annoying for short stays
- service quality varies more than the brand name alone suggests

## Option 3: faucet add-ons vs under-sink RO systems

This category needs a clean split, because readers often put very different products under the word “filter.”

### Faucet add-ons and simple taste filters

These sit at the cheap, low-commitment end.

They can help with:

- taste
- smell
- visible fine sediment
- confidence for non-drinking kitchen use

They are **not** the same thing as a maintained direct-drinking system.

That makes them useful as a stopgap, but not a strong default answer for readers who want one source of water for direct drinking, tea, coffee, and cooking.

### Under-sink RO systems

This is the more serious long-stay option.

A practical 2026 anchor from current official A. O. Smith Vietnam listings:

- under-sink RO models currently run from roughly **12.8 million to 21.9 million VND**, depending on output, features, and format

Where to get it:

- A. O. Smith
- Karofi
- kitchen-appliance dealers
- building-approved installers

What to ask before buying:

- exact model
- what contaminants or conditions the system is meant to handle
- filter replacement schedule
- replacement cartridge cost
- whether installation changes the countertop or cabinet
- whether the rental contract allows the install
- whether the system has a separate drinking faucet

This option makes the most sense when:

- the household expects to stay **at least several more months**
- the kitchen belongs to the household, not a short-stay landlord setup
- the household wants one consistent source for direct drinking and taste-sensitive cooking
- buying bottles forever feels more annoying than paying upfront

Best for:

- direct drinking
- tea and coffee
- soup, stock, and rice
- long-term households that want less bottle handling

Weak points:

- higher upfront cost
- maintenance is now the household’s job
- if a landlord claims a system is already installed, that is only useful if the replacement schedule is real and current

## Option 4: whole-house filtration

Whole-house filtration solves a different problem.

This is not mainly about “what should be in my glass right now.” It is about the condition of water across the property:

- shower water
- scale and hardness
- sediment at multiple taps
- smell
- staining
- water hitting laundry, fixtures, and bathroom hardware too

That is why whole-house filtration usually makes more sense for:

- houses
- villas
- ground-floor properties
- households with visible water issues across more than one tap
- owner-occupied or very long-term homes

Practical 2026 price anchors from current Karofi official listings:

- entry-level whole-house models can start around **32.8 million VND**
- mid-tier examples sit around **43 million to 98 million VND**
- higher-end systems can move **well above 150 million VND**

Where to get it:

- whole-house filtration brands such as Karofi
- water-treatment installers that do a site survey first

This is usually a **quote-and-install** purchase, not a casual online checkout decision.

For a normal apartment renter, it is usually too much money and too much installation work unless there is a real whole-property water problem.

Best for:

- full-property water treatment
- houses with known sediment, hardness, or smell issues
- households that want shower, laundry, and kitchen water treated in one system

Weak points:

- high upfront cost
- setup complexity
- overkill for many apartment renters

## What to use for cooking vs drinking

This is where many readers want a simple answer. The most useful answer is not one universal rule, but a household split.

### Use drinking-grade water for:

- direct drinking
- ice
- tea
- coffee
- soup, stock, and rice if water taste clearly affects the result
- any situation where the household already does not trust the smell, taste, or clarity of the building tap

Drinking-grade water here means one of:

- sealed bottled water
- trusted 18.5L/19L delivery
- a maintained under-sink direct-drinking system

### Cold tap water that you heat yourself can be reasonable for some cooking if:

- the building water looks normal
- there is no rust smell, chlorine hit, or visible sediment after running it
- the task is a heavy-boil kitchen task rather than direct drinking

Examples include:

- boiling pasta
- blanching vegetables
- boiling eggs

That is a practical rule of thumb, not a universal guarantee.

### Use more caution if:

- the apartment has been empty for a while
- the first water from the kitchen tap looks cloudy or rusty
- there is a tank or plumbing smell
- the property is older and the household has low confidence in the plumbing
- the household is already buying delivery or RO water anyway

In those cases, using drinking-grade water for more kitchen tasks is the cleaner choice.

### One rule that should stay simple

Do **not** start from hot tap water for drinking or cooking. Start from cold water and heat it yourself.

## What to check before trusting a landlord-installed setup

If a listing says “filtered water included” or “drinking water from kitchen tap,” treat that as a claim that still needs a quick check.

Ask for:

- the brand and model
- the date of the last filter replacement
- the service provider’s receipt or maintenance record
- whether there is a separate drinking-water faucet
- whether the landlord or the tenant pays for cartridge changes

Also check basic reality on day one:

- run the cold water briefly and check smell and clarity
- look for rust tint or floating particles
- ask neighbors what they use for direct drinking
- ask whether large-bottle delivery is common in the building
- if the landlord says “everyone drinks from the tap,” do not treat that as proof by itself

If the rental has other maintenance shortcuts, it is safer to be skeptical here too. The same kind of soft promise shows up in other apartment issues covered in [Avoid Rental Scams in Vietnam](/vietnam/housing/avoid-rental-scams-vietnam-2026).

## Best setup by stay length and housing type

### Short stay, serviced apartment, or first week in Vietnam

Use sealed small bottles or 5L bottles.

This is the fastest setup, needs no deposit, and can usually be ordered the same day.

### Normal apartment renter staying several months

Use large-bottle delivery for direct drinking.

This is usually the best balance of cost, effort, and reliability if the household does not want to modify the kitchen.

### Long-term renter with kitchen control

Consider under-sink RO if the household wants one stable source for direct drinking and taste-sensitive cooking.

This becomes easier to justify the longer the stay is.

### House or villa with visible water issues across multiple taps

Look at whole-house filtration first, not just a kitchen drinking solution.

If showers, laundry, bathroom fixtures, and kitchen taps all show the same issue, a single drinking-water fix will not solve the full household problem.

## A practical default for most readers

If the goal is to make one good decision without overthinking it:

- start with **sealed small bottles** on arrival
- move to **18.5L/19L delivery** if staying longer and the building setup is ordinary
- move to **under-sink RO** only if the stay is long enough and the kitchen is under the household’s control
- keep **whole-house filtration** for houses or clear all-property water problems

That gives most expat households in Vietnam a water setup that is workable, realistic, and easy to maintain.
