Arriving in Vietnam without a working +84 phone number is like navigating the country blindfolded. You cannot hail a Grab, you cannot order food, and — most critically — you cannot register for Zalo, the app that effectively runs daily life in Vietnam. After the government's sweeping crackdown on unregistered SIM cards, buying connectivity is no longer as simple as handing $5 to a street vendor.

This guide gives you the exact blueprint for getting connected on day one, avoiding the airport tourist traps, and keeping your number alive when you leave the country.

## Quick Answer: Which SIM Card Should You Get in Vietnam?

- **Best overall:** Viettel — 54% market share, unmatched rural and mountain coverage
- **Best for cities on a budget:** Vinaphone — 10–15% cheaper than Viettel with comparable urban speeds
- **Best setup for long-term expats:** Local Viettel or Vinaphone eSIM from an official store, keeping your physical SIM slot free for home-country OTPs
- **Biggest trap:** Any SIM offered without a passport check will be blacklisted within 48 hours

## The Big Three Networks (2026 Reality)

Vietnam's mobile infrastructure is genuinely excellent, and data is among the cheapest in Southeast Asia. The right carrier depends entirely on where you are going.

### Viettel

The dominant network by every measure. Over 54% market share, 30,000+ 5G base stations deployed nationwide as of late 2025, and coverage that extends into rural and mountain areas where other carriers fade out entirely.

Real-world 5G download speeds in Hanoi, HCMC, and Da Nang city centres average 300–600 Mbps. Monthly prepaid packages with data and calling run 135,000–200,000 VND ($5.50–$8). If you are going anywhere outside a major city — the Ha Giang Loop, Phong Nha, the Central Highlands — Viettel is the only network worth having.

### Vinaphone

A strong second choice for city-focused stays. Coverage in Hanoi, HCMC, Da Nang, and major resort areas is excellent, and 5G speeds occasionally rival Viettel's in urban cores. Packages typically run 10–15% cheaper than equivalent Viettel plans. Signal weakens noticeably in rural northern provinces — for Ha Giang, Viettel is safer.

### Mobifone

Historically strong in the south, particularly HCMC and the Mekong Delta. Mobifone only launched commercial 5G in June 2025 and expansion is still primarily urban. Signal is unreliable in the north and spotty for long stretches in the mountains. Fine for a short city trip; not suitable if you plan to travel widely.

### A Note on MVNOs (Wintel, iTel, etc.)

Virtual operators offer very cheap "unlimited" plans but their registration apps have a documented history of failing to process foreign passports. Until this is independently verified as fixed, avoid them for your first week — the risk of getting stuck without a working SIM is not worth the $2 saving.

## Getting a SIM at the Airport

You need data the moment you land. Here is how to do it correctly.

**What to bring:** Your physical passport. Real-name registration is legally mandatory since 2023. No exceptions.

**The process:**
1. Walk through baggage claim to the official carrier kiosks in the arrivals hall
2. Look for large red and white Viettel signs or blue and white Vinaphone signs — these are the official counters
3. Hand over your passport. Staff will photograph both the data page and your face using a tablet, linking your identity to the Ministry of Information and Communications (MIC) database
4. The whole process takes 5–10 minutes

### The Tourist SIM Trap

Airport kiosks heavily push 15-day or 30-day "Tourist SIMs." Some of these packages have a hard expiry that cannot be extended by topping up — when the time runs out, the SIM is dead. If you plan to stay longer than a month, tell the staff explicitly: **"I need a regular prepaid SIM, not a tourist SIM."**

### The Pre-Activated Scam

If any vendor — at the airport or in a tourist area — offers you a SIM that works without asking for your passport, walk away. These cards are registered under stolen or fabricated IDs. In 2026, telecom networks actively scan for unregistered cards, and yours will be permanently disabled within 48 hours. The 350–500k VND "convenience fee" charged by Old Quarter phone shops for the same 150k VND official package is a separate but related trap — always use Google Maps to find an official Cửa hàng Viettel directly.

## eSIM Options: Travel vs. Local

### Third-Party Travel eSIMs (Airalo, Nomad, Holafly)

Buy before you fly, activate the moment the plane lands. These are genuinely useful for the first 24 hours.

**The key advantage:** Your home country physical SIM card stays active in your phone's SIM tray with Wi-Fi Calling enabled, so you can still receive bank OTPs from home without paying international roaming.

**The critical limitation:** Travel eSIMs are data-only. They do not give you a local +84 number. Without a +84 number you cannot register Zalo, cannot receive verification codes from local apps, and Grab drivers have no way to call you when they cannot find you in a crowd.

Travel eSIMs are a good bridge for the first day. They are not a complete solution for anyone staying more than a week.

### The Long-Term Expat Setup: Local eSIM

If your phone supports eSIM — most iPhones from iPhone XS onwards, most modern Android flagships — this is the definitive setup:

1. Go to an official standalone Viettel or Vinaphone store in the city (not an airport kiosk, not a third-party phone shop)
2. Register for a local eSIM with your passport
3. You now have a real +84 number for Zalo, Grab, and local app verification, while your physical SIM slot remains free for your home country card to receive OTPs

This dual-SIM arrangement eliminates the main anxiety of long-term expat life: never having to choose between local connectivity and home-country banking access.

**One important limitation:** iPhones purchased in mainland China, Hong Kong, or Macao may not support eSIM due to hardware restrictions. Check your model before visiting the store.

**Monthly ongoing cost:** 135,000–200,000 VND ($5.50–$8) for a Viettel plan with daily 5–8GB high-speed data plus calling minutes

### Account Management (The Essential Codes)

Save these before you lose signal:

- **Check balance:** Dial `*101#`
- **Check remaining data:** Text `KTTK` to `191` (reply will be in Vietnamese)
- **The better approach:** Download **My Viettel** or **My VNPT** immediately on Wi-Fi. Switch the language to English, monitor your expiry date, and top up with an international card via Traveloka if you do not yet have a local bank account.

## Daily Friction: Spam Calls and Indoor Dead Zones

**Spam calls:** Phone numbers in Vietnam are heavily recycled. A brand-new SIM routinely receives 5–10 spam or scam calls per day, usually from numbers beginning with 024 or 028 (Hanoi landlines). Do not answer unknown numbers in your first week. If your Grab driver cannot reach you they will use the app's in-built chat or Zalo — answering a random call risks inadvertent charges.

**High-rise dead zones:** In apartments above the 30th floor — common in Vinhomes and other premium complexes — indoor 4G signal frequently drops to zero. The fix is simple: enable **Wi-Fi Calling (VoWiFi)** in your phone settings. Both Viettel and Vinaphone support this natively, routing all calls and SMS through your home Wi-Fi router.

## Keeping Your Number Alive During Visa Runs

This is the problem most guides do not cover adequately. If your SIM has no activity for 60 days, the network recycles the number. Losing your Vietnamese number means losing access to Zalo, local banking apps, and any service tied to that +84 number — often unrecoverable without starting over.

**What to do before leaving Vietnam:**

**Step 1 — Enable international roaming while still in Vietnam.** This is critical: you cannot activate roaming once you have crossed the border. For Viettel, text `CVQT` to `138`, or dial `***138*1#`. Do this the day before you leave. Note that international roaming carries charges of approximately $5–$10/day if you actively use data — enable it purely to keep the line open and receive OTP texts, and turn off mobile data on the Viettel line while abroad to avoid unexpected charges.

**Step 2 — Top up before you fly.** Add 50,000–100,000 VND to your account. Each top-up extends your SIM's validity window by 30–60 days. You can top up remotely via the My Viettel app using an international card, so if the validity deadline approaches while you are abroad, you can push it back without returning to Vietnam.

## FAQ

**Do I need a Vietnamese SIM to use Grab?**

Grab can be registered with a foreign number, but drivers will call a local number when they cannot find you. Without a +84 number, pickups in busy areas become significantly harder.

**Can I buy a SIM card in Vietnam without a passport?**

No. Since 2023, real-name registration with a physical passport is legally required for all SIM purchases. Any vendor offering a SIM without this check is selling an illegal card.

**Is Viettel or Vinaphone better for Ha Giang?**

Viettel, unambiguously. Multiple traveler reports confirm Vinaphone and Mobifone have signal gaps on remote mountain roads in the north. For the Ha Giang Loop specifically, Viettel is the only reliable choice.

**How many SIMs can I register on one passport?**

Vietnamese regulations allow up to 3 prepaid SIMs per foreign passport. In practice, most expats only need one.
