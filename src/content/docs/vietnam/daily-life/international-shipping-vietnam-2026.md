## The short answer

If the shipment is cheap, light, and not urgent, Vietnam Post is usually the first place to check. If the shipment is urgent, valuable, or likely to trigger customs questions, DHL, FedEx, or UPS usually make more sense. If the route is China-linked or more regional, SF Express or J&T can be worth a quote. Ninja Van should not be treated as a main 2026 outbound option for ordinary senders, because its Vietnam site says Express Logistics service was discontinued from September 30, 2025, and the current public evidence is much weaker than for the other names in this comparison.

The price gap is large. Vietnam Post’s public ceiling for basic outbound letter-post still shows very low entry pricing, from about VND 15,000 to VND 23,000 for items up to 20g, then about VND 37,000 to VND 63,000 for 20g-100g, and about VND 90,000 to VND 155,000 for 100g-250g, depending on zone. At the premium end, DHL’s 2026 guide shows 0.5kg document exports starting around VND 1.37 million and rising above VND 3.0 million on farther zones, while FedEx’s 2026 export rates show 0.5kg document pricing starting around VND 1.31 million and rising to about VND 2.47 million across its listed destination groups. J&T’s publicly searchable card is older, but it still suggests the regional tier can sit well below the global majors on some small-document lanes. All of these numbers are approximate, and surcharges or destination rules can shift the final invoice.

## Which option fits which job

For ordinary papers, greeting cards, and low-value non-urgent items, the postal lane is usually the cheapest starting point. For original documents, urgent business material, or parcels where tracking and customs handling matter more than saving a few hundred thousand dong, the premium courier tier is usually safer. For selected China or Asia-heavy routes, or for ecommerce-style shipments where the sender is price sensitive but still wants a structured courier workflow, SF Express and J&T are worth checking. The mistake to avoid is treating every listed brand as an equal, walk-in, all-purpose option. They are not.

## Vietnam Post: the widest network, but not one single product

Vietnam Post is the easiest broad-access starting point because it has the public network, international-delivery pages, tracking, fee-estimate tools, and post-office lookup. That does not mean every branch handles every international lane equally well. In practice, larger district or city branches are usually a better first stop for tricky items than the smallest neighborhood counter. If forms, copies, or labels still need to be prepared first, see our [Print, Scan, and Courier Documents in Vietnam](/vietnam/digital-nomad/print-scan-courier-documents-vietnam-2026).

### Basic post and registered mail

Vietnam Post’s ordinary or basic post is the low-cost postal lane. Registered mail is the safer postal step up when tracking matters. The public basic-letter tariff is still the clearest low-end anchor: about VND 15,000-23,000 up to 20g, about VND 37,000-63,000 up to 100g, and about VND 90,000-155,000 up to 250g, depending on zone. That older public ceiling is useful for understanding the bottom of the market, but it should not be read as a live 2026 counter quote table for every international mailing case. Registered, parcel, and faster postal subclasses can price very differently at the counter.

### EMS, VNQuickpost, Economy, and partner-courier lanes

The faster Vietnam Post side is not one product either. EMS remains the standard faster postal express lane. VNQuickpost is Vietnam Post’s branded international express product. Economy is narrower and should be treated as a special-case option, not a fallback for everything. The current Economy page shows China common-goods lanes at about 3-5 days, some special-goods lanes at about 4-7 or 8-15 days, and U.S./EU ordinary-goods lanes at about 6-14 days for the U.S. and 6-18 days for the EU. Those windows exclude customs and count from partner handoff, not simply from the moment the parcel is accepted at the counter. The same page also shows that U.S./EU Economy is for goods, not documents, so it is the wrong lane for document shipping.

Vietnam Post also exposes partner-courier lanes, including DHL and UPS agent services. That is useful, especially outside a few core corporate offices, but it should not be flattened into “every Vietnam Post branch can do every partner service the same way.” If the parcel is urgent or the contents are awkward, ask first whether the branch is handling it as postal EMS, VNQuickpost, or a partner-courier shipment.

## DHL, FedEx, and UPS: pay more when speed and cleaner handling matter

This tier is for shipments where delay is expensive. DHL and FedEx both publish current 2026 Vietnam export rates, and both position their services around fast, trackable international delivery with online quoting and booking tools. As a practical starting point, a 0.5kg document is already in the premium bracket: roughly VND 1.37 million-3.04 million on DHL depending on zone, and roughly VND 1.31 million-2.47 million on FedEx depending on destination group. Those are reference rates before the usual extras such as fuel or special fees.

Use DHL or FedEx when the shipment is urgent, the declared contents are sensitive, or the sender wants a cleaner digital workflow. FedEx also warns that some services are zip-code dependent or not equally available everywhere. That matters if the destination is less common or remote.

UPS belongs in this premium group too, but it is a little different from DHL and FedEx in how many readers will encounter it in Vietnam. UPS is easier to reach in Vietnam than many readers assume, because its location page lists many Viettel Post authorized outlets. Many of those listings also state that they are not available for parcel drop off, so treat them as starting points to verify service, not as guaranteed one-step counters for every shipment.

## SF Express and J&T: worth checking on selected lanes

SF Express is a real outbound option from Vietnam, but it behaves more like a lane-structured cross-border carrier than a one-price-fits-all consumer counter brand. Its Vietnam-facing pages point users to a time-and-cost calculator, app-based ordering, service-coverage tools, customs-clearance pages, and support lines rather than to one flat public retail sheet. In practice, that makes SF most useful when the route already looks like a good fit for its network, especially on China-linked or broader Asia routes, and less useful when the sender wants a quick answer from a simple public rate card.

J&T is more openly consumer-facing. Its international page is aimed at individuals, shops, and businesses, and it points users to a price list, nearby post-office lookup, and consultant flow. The caution is freshness: the public card that is easy to find is a 2024 export sheet, not a 2026 one. It still helps as rough context only. For example, that older card shows 0.5kg document pricing from about VND 364,200 on some nearby lanes up to about VND 1,052,200 on some zone-based lanes, excluding VAT and fuel surcharge. That suggests J&T can sit between postal pricing and the global majors on some routes, but the final decision still needs a live quote.

## Why Ninja Van should not be a main recommendation

Ninja Van should be named because readers will see the brand and may assume it belongs in the main comparison set. But it should not be one of the core recommendations here. Its own Vietnam page says Express Logistics service was discontinued effective September 30, 2025. Unless there is a confirmed current business workflow for the exact lane in question, it is safer to treat Ninja Van as a weak or conditional lead rather than a standard consumer outbound choice from Vietnam.

## Customs and item types that cause the most friction

The categories that most often create trouble are food, cosmetics, liquids, batteries, branded goods, personal effects, and anything that looks even slightly regulated. Carrier rules often list categories that are restricted, contractual, or blocked outright, including perishables, personal effects, plants, tobacco, dangerous goods, and higher-risk valuables. Vietnam Post has also published customs-document reminders for goods sent to the U.K., U.S., and EU, including CN22 and CN23 content declarations, with the warning that incomplete declarations can cause returns and extra cost.

That does not mean those categories are never shippable. It means they are the worst place to rely on generic advice from a website summary or a social post. If the item contains food, powder, liquid, a battery, or a branded commercial product, ask the carrier to confirm the lane and paperwork before paying. For premium couriers, that usually means checking the live service rules. For postal lanes, it means checking what the destination country and the receiving customs system will accept, not only what the counter clerk first says.

## What to prepare before asking for a quote

Start with five things:

- the exact destination country and postcode
- whether the item is a document or goods
- actual weight and package dimensions
- a plain-English description of contents
- a rough value for customs purposes

Then compare the right level of service instead of comparing random brand names. Compare postal with postal, premium courier with premium courier, and lane-specific regional services with other lane-specific regional services. If payment may happen at a physical counter instead of through a digital booking flow, it also helps to know where a foreign card is likely to work and where cash or local payment is still easier; our [International Cards in Vietnam](/vietnam/banking/international-cards-vietnam-2026) covers that part.

## Final decision guide

Choose Vietnam Post first when the parcel is light, not urgent, and cost matters most.

Choose DHL, FedEx, or UPS first when speed, tracking quality, and smoother customs handling matter more than price.

Choose SF Express or J&T when the route, item type, and quote make them competitive, especially on China-linked or regional lanes.

Do not build the plan around Ninja Van unless the exact service is confirmed as current for the shipment in hand.
