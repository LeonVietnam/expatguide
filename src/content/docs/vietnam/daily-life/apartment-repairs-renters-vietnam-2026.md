## The short answer

For most small apartment repairs in Vietnam, do not pay for a fix blindly on day one. First sort the problem into one of three buckets: a tiny consumable or upkeep item inside the unit, an owner-side fixture or appliance problem, or a safety issue that needs immediate escalation. In most ordinary cases, the safest order is: document the issue, message the landlord or building contact first, get written approval if money may need to be reimbursed, then arrange the repair through the channel that actually controls the unit.

*Legal text and public service pages checked in April 2026. Repair responsibility still varies by lease wording, cause of damage, building rules, and whether the issue sits inside the unit or touches shared systems.*

## Step 1: sort the problem before calling anyone

The biggest mistake is treating every fault as the same kind of repair.

### Usually closer to minor tenant-side upkeep

These are the jobs most likely to be solved quickly without a lease fight:

- replacing light bulbs
- changing remote batteries
- clearing a simple hair-clogged floor drain or sink trap
- replacing a shower hose or small faucet head if the fix is obvious and cheap
- tightening a loose handle, shelf screw, or cabinet hinge
- replacing low-cost consumables that clearly wore out during daily use

Even here, it is still worth sending one short message and one photo first if the unit is furnished and the item came with the apartment.

### Usually closer to landlord-side or owner-side repair

Slow down before paying for these:

- water heater failure
- washing machine, refrigerator, microwave, or owner-supplied appliance faults
- recurring AC water drip, weak cooling, or bad smell
- broken door lock or smart-lock issue
- toilet leak that is not just a loose hose
- ceiling leak, wall seepage, or mold linked to the building
- electrical outlet, breaker, or wiring problems

These are the cases where the cause matters. The issue may be normal wear, hidden installation failure, building-system failure, or tenant-caused damage. That is why a quick approval trail matters.

### Treat as urgent, not as a normal small repair

Stop thinking about reimbursement first if there is:

- exposed wiring
- sparks, smoke, or burning smell
- water leaking near outlets, breakers, or appliances
- a door or lock failure that creates an immediate security problem
- a gas smell or gas-equipment problem
- a cracked glass panel, balcony issue, or other clear safety risk

In these cases, contact the building desk, landlord, or emergency service channel immediately and document every step afterward.

## What the law says, and what usually happens

### Official layer

The legal baseline is not “the landlord pays for everything” and it is not “the tenant pays for everything.”

The Civil Code baseline is that the landlord repairs damage or defects of the leased property except minor damage that tenants usually fix themselves, while the tenant also preserves the property, carries out maintenance and minor repair during use, and is not liable for normal wear and tear from ordinary use. Housing Law 2023 also gives the tenant a formal fallback: if the landlord fails to maintain the home after being asked, the tenant may arrange maintenance after written notice at least 15 days in advance stating the scope and expected cost, then seek reimbursement or rent deduction.

### Practical layer

Real rentals do not run on legal text alone.

In daily life, the first question is usually not “who is legally right in the abstract?” The first question is “who can get this fixed without creating a bigger deposit or safety problem?” In many buildings, the fastest working path is still landlord first, then building management if the building controls access or common systems, then a service provider once approval is clear.

For a cheap and obvious consumable, many tenants simply replace it and move on. For anything tied to owner-supplied equipment, wiring, plumbing, locks, AC, or wall and ceiling damage, paying first without written approval is where disputes start.

## Contact order that usually works best

### Regular owner-run rental

This is the safest default order:

1. message the landlord, agent, or person named in the lease
2. send photos and a short video
3. state whether the issue is blocking normal use or creating a safety risk
4. ask who should arrange the repair and who should pay
5. wait for written approval before spending money unless the issue is urgent

If the reply is vague, ask one narrow question: “Should this be arranged by the owner, building management, or the tenant?” That usually gets a cleaner answer than a long complaint message.

### Condo tower with management or reception

If the unit is in a managed building, add the building desk early.

A private-unit problem can still involve building rules, technician access, service-elevator timing, visitor registration, or common-system checks. A leak may actually come from another unit or a shared pipe. An AC or water problem may require entry rules that outside technicians cannot bypass on their own.

In this setup, the cleanest move is often to message the landlord and the building contact in the same thread or back-to-back.

### Serviced apartment

A serviced apartment often has a clearer maintenance desk than a standard owner-run lease, but do not assume the label alone solves the problem. Some serviced stock behaves like a mini hotel. Some behaves like a managed long-stay building. The useful question is still the same: who has authority to send maintenance and approve spending?

For a broader setup comparison, see [serviced apartments vs regular rentals](/vietnam/housing/serviced-apartment-vs-regular-rental-vietnam-2026).

## Before anyone starts work

Save the evidence first. That protects both reimbursement and the deposit later.

Keep:

- clear photos
- one short video showing the fault
- the date and time the issue was first reported
- the landlord or building reply
- any quote, invoice, or receipt
- before-and-after photos if the tenant paid

If the fix touches walls, ceiling, wiring, plumbing inside the wall, locks, or owner-supplied appliances, do not approve destructive work based on a voice call alone. Get the go-ahead in text.

This is the same habit that helps with larger housing disputes later. For the broader landlord-risk side, see [Avoid Rental Scams in Vietnam](/vietnam/housing/avoid-rental-scams-vietnam-2026).

## When self-arranging is usually fine, and when it is not

### Usually fine to self-arrange

A tenant can often handle the problem directly if all of these are true:

- the job is small and low-cost
- it does not affect wiring, hidden plumbing, or building structure
- it is clearly inside the unit
- the fix does not change the owner’s equipment in a lasting way
- there is little chance of later blame

Examples include a light bulb, a shower hose, a cheap drain clean, or a replacement remote battery.

### Better to wait for approval

Wait for approval first if any of these apply:

- the item came with the apartment and is not a cheap consumable
- the fix may require drilling, dismantling, or part replacement
- the problem could be linked to wear and tear rather than tenant use
- the bill may not be small
- the building may control technician entry
- the repair could later be used to justify deposit deductions

A recurring AC water drip is a good example. It may only need cleaning, but it may also point to installation or drain-line issues. The same fault can sit in different payment buckets depending on cause.

## Where to find help without pretending one channel fits everything

This article is not a national repair price guide, because public price evidence is not equally strong for every type of small repair. The public evidence is clearest for AC and appliance-related service.

### Channel 1: landlord or building-arranged technician

This is usually the safest first choice for owner-side fixtures, appliances, locks, leaks, and anything that may turn into a reimbursement fight. It is slower than solving the problem alone, but it creates the cleanest responsibility trail.

### Channel 2: service platform for AC or appliance jobs

For AC cleaning and some appliance-related work, public service platforms are easier to verify.

Examples visible in April 2026:

- bTaskee publicly shows air-conditioner cleaning in Ho Chi Minh City, Hanoi, Da Nang, Da Lat, Binh Duong, and Bien Hoa, with reference pricing that can start from about **216,000 VND** for some wall-mounted units and rise much higher by machine type and location
- Thợ Điện Máy XANH publicly lists wall AC cleaning at about **200,000 VND** and wall AC removal at about **250,000 VND**
- Điện Máy XANH’s public booking flow also says service requests are confirmed within about **4 hours** after submission on the cited city service page

Treat these as public examples for AC and appliance categories, not as market-wide rates for all repair jobs.

If the unit has repeated AC issues, also cross-check with [apartment AC decisions and repair red flags](/vietnam/housing/air-conditioning-vietnam-apartments-2026).

### Channel 3: local technician found through the building, neighbors, or the landlord

This is common in practice, especially outside big condo towers. It can be the fastest path for drain, lock, or plumbing jobs, but it is also where documentation matters most. If the tenant hires a local technician first and the landlord later disputes the cause or price, the tenant has less protection without prior written approval.

## A short message that works better than a long complaint

A short repair message usually gets a faster answer than a long backstory.

Use this structure:

- what is broken
- when it started
- whether it blocks normal use
- whether there is a safety risk
- two or three photos
- one direct question on who should arrange and pay

Example:

> The water heater stopped working this morning. The power is on, but there is no hot water. Photos and a short video are attached. Please confirm whether building maintenance or the owner should arrange repair, and whether the tenant should wait for your technician.

## What to do if the landlord stops responding

If the problem is minor and cheap, some tenants decide it is not worth a larger dispute and pay for the fix. That may be sensible for a bulb, hose, or drain clean. It is a poor habit for bigger repair items.

If the landlord goes silent on an owner-side repair:

1. resend the problem in writing with photos
2. state clearly what is unusable
3. ask for a reply by a specific date
4. keep all messages and receipts
5. use the legal fallback route only with care, especially if the cost is not small

The formal fallback exists, but it should be treated as protection in the background, not as the opening move for every everyday fault.

## The best repair strategy starts before move-in

Many repair fights are really move-in documentation failures.

At viewing and handover stage, record the condition of:

- AC cooling and drainage
- water heater
- locks and keys
- outlets and breakers
- taps, drains, and toilet flush
- owner-supplied appliances

That reduces the chance that an old problem gets pushed onto the tenant later. For the handover side, use the [rental viewing checklist](/vietnam/housing/rental-viewing-checklist-vietnam-2026).

## The clean rule of thumb

Use this rule when a small repair appears:

- **cheap consumable and no real dispute risk:** tenant can often fix it
- **owner-side fixture, appliance, lock, leak, or repeated fault:** report first and get approval
- **building-system or safety issue:** escalate immediately, document everything, and solve the hazard first
