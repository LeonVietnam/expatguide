**Living comfortably in Hoi An in 2026 costs roughly $1,000 to $2,000 per month for most expats and remote workers**, depending on whether you rent in An Bang Beach, Cam Chau, or further out. A lean budget can dip to $850 if you commit to a long-term lease and eat mostly local; a premium beachfront lifestyle climbs past $3,000. This guide breaks down every line item using 2025 Q4 to 2026 Q1 data, with the post-merger administrative changes you need to know.

One important update first: as of July 1, 2025, Hoi An is **no longer an independent city**. Under Vietnam's nationwide administrative reform (Resolution 202/2025/QH15), Quang Nam province was merged into Da Nang City, and the former Hoi An city was reorganized into three wards: Hoi An Ward (the Ancient Town core), Hoi An Dong Ward (Cam Chau, Cua Dai, Cam Thanh), and Hoi An Tay Ward (which now includes An Bang Beach). For day-to-day living this changes very little, but it does affect where you go for visa paperwork — covered below.

## How Much Does It Cost to Live in Hoi An Per Month?

Here are three realistic monthly budgets in USD, based on what each tier actually buys in Hoi An specifically (not generic Vietnam):

| Category | Lean ($850–1,100) | Comfortable ($1,500–2,000) | Premium ($2,800–3,500) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rent | $300–450 (studio, Cam Chau, long lease) | $650–850 (2BR house with garden) | $1,400–1,800 (beachfront villa) |

These ranges assume you stay at least three months. Shorter stays push every category higher because of weekly-rate premiums on housing and bikes.

## Housing: Where Expats Actually Live

Hoi An's expat housing market splits into four practical zones, each with a different cost profile and lifestyle. Almost no long-term renters live inside the Ancient Town itself — it's loud, tourist-saturated, and almost entirely converted to homestays and boutique hotels.

**Cam Chau** (now part of Hoi An Dong Ward) is the expat sweet spot: walkable to both the Ancient Town and An Bang Beach, with plenty of mid-range houses and small apartments. A furnished 1-bedroom apartment runs $300–450 per month on a long lease, and a 2-bedroom house with a small garden lands around $550–800.

**An Bang Beach** (now in Hoi An Tay Ward, formed from the old Cam An ward plus Thanh Ha, Tan An, and Cam Ha) is the most expensive area. Beach-adjacent studios start around $450, sea-view 1-bedrooms run $500–750, and a 2-bedroom villa close to the sand can hit $900–1,300. Demand spikes from January through April, when peak-season rates run roughly 25–35% above the off-season baseline.

**Cam Thanh** (also Hoi An Dong Ward) is the rural coconut-village area south of Cam Chau. This is where you find larger 3-bedroom houses with gardens and the occasional pool, in the $750–1,300 range. Trade-off: you'll need a motorbike for everything.

**Tra Que and the rural outskirts** offer the cheapest long-term rentals — full houses for $400–600 — but you're committing to a quiet, agricultural setting with limited dining and no walkable amenities.

*Sources: Bamboo Routes Hoi An rental market report (2026 Q1); cross-referenced with active listings in the "Hoi An Housing" and "Danang Hoi An Alt Accommodation" Facebook groups, March 2026.*

A few practical norms: deposits are typically one month for stays under three months and two months for six-month-plus leases. Three-month rentals carry roughly a 15–20% premium over annual contracts. Most expats find places through Facebook groups rather than agents — the two groups named above are the standard starting points. Walking into Cam Chau and asking at houses with "for rent" signs in Vietnamese still works and sometimes yields the best local prices.

One factor specific to Hoi An: **flooding season**. Parts of the Ancient Town and low-lying Cam Chau streets can sit under half a meter to a meter of water during October and November. Long-term renters specifically look for houses with elevated ground floors. If you're viewing a place outside flood season, ask the landlord — and the neighbors — directly how high the water came last year.

If you're sending rent transfers from abroad to a Vietnamese landlord, [Wise](https://wise.com/invite/dic/ganqiuh) handles VND transfers at the mid-market rate with much lower fees than traditional bank wires, which matters when you're moving $500+ every month.

## Food: Local Markets vs. Da Nang Runs

Hoi An's food costs split sharply between two worlds. Local Vietnamese meals — including Hoi An's specialties like cao lau, mi quang, and white rose dumplings — run $1.50–3.50 at neighborhood spots. A bowl of pho or a banh mi from a street vendor stays under $2. If you eat this way most days, your food budget can comfortably sit at $250–300 per month.

Mid-range expat-oriented cafes and restaurants (places like Nourish Eatery, Rosie's, and the various An Bang beach restaurants) charge $8–15 for a main course plus a drink. A single Western-style brunch with coffee runs $10–14. Coffee shop prices range from $1.50 for a local salt coffee to $4 for a flat white at expat cafes — relevant if you're working from cafes regularly.

Groceries are the area where Hoi An's smaller scale shows. Local markets — Hoi An Central Market and Ba Le Market — are 30–50% cheaper than Da Nang supermarkets for fresh produce, meat, and fish. For Western imports (cheese, wine, specific brands of cereal or sauces), most expats do periodic "Da Nang runs" to Lotte Mart or Go! (formerly Big C). Hoi An's small specialty grocers carry a limited Western selection at roughly a 20% markup over Da Nang prices.

*Sources: WanderOnLess Hoi An food guide (January 2026); price spot-checks at Hoi An Central Market and An Bang restaurants, March 2026.*

## Transportation: Yes, Grab Works Here

A common myth is that Grab doesn't operate in Hoi An. It does, and reliably, as of April 2026 — both GrabCar and GrabBike are available throughout the Hoi An wards and on the route to Da Nang. A GrabBike from the Ancient Town to An Bang Beach runs about 28,000 VND (around $1.10), and a GrabCar from Hoi An to Da Nang International Airport is roughly 350,000–550,000 VND ($14–22) depending on time of day. Xanh SM, the all-electric VinFast ride-hailing service, has grown rapidly in Da Nang and now serves Hoi An too — many expats prefer it for newer cars and more stable pricing.

That said, most long-term residents own or rent a motorbike. Monthly rentals for a Honda Vision or Air Blade run $60–90 from local shops, dropping to $50–70 if you commit to three months or more. Buying used: a serviceable Honda Wave runs $400–600, and a used Vision or Air Blade lands at $800–1,200. Petrol sits at roughly 23,500 VND per liter (about $0.95).

For airport runs to Da Nang International (DAD, about 30 km away), private transfers booked through hotels or Facebook groups run $14–22 for the same trip Grab quotes. The public bus (Route 02) is the cheap option at around 30,000 VND, but it's slow and inconsistent.

If you're new to riding in Vietnam, our [motorbike guide for foreigners](/vietnam/daily-life/motorbike-guide-2026) covers the licensing, insurance, and safety basics before you get on a bike here.

*Sources: Grab fare estimates from Hoi An residents reported on Ahoy Vietnam (January 2026); motorbike rental quotes from three Cam Chau shops, February 2026.*

## Utilities and Internet

Electricity is the single most variable cost in Hoi An, and the variation is seasonal rather than tier-based. Vietnam uses tiered residential pricing (roughly 1,800–3,200 VND per kWh depending on usage band). If you run air conditioning continuously from May through August, expect $80–150 per month. During the cooler months (December through February), the same household pays $20–40.

Water is negligible, typically $3–7 per month for a one or two-person household. Home fiber internet from FPT, Viettel, or VNPT runs 250,000–450,000 VND ($10–18) for plans with 150 Mbps or higher — enough for video calls and remote work. Mobile data on Viettel or Vinaphone runs about 120,000 VND ($5) per month for high-data plans. For more on choosing a mobile plan, see our [Vietnam SIM card guide](/vietnam/daily-life/vietnam-sim-card-guide-2026).

Power outages do happen, especially during the October–November storm season. They're usually short (under an hour), but if you work remotely it's worth either choosing a place with backup power or having a coworking membership as a fallback.

*Sources: EVN Vietnam residential tariff schedule (2026 Q1); Viettel and FPT published consumer plans, March 2026.*

## Healthcare: You'll Be Going to Da Nang

Hoi An has no international-standard hospital. For minor issues — basic blood tests, minor injuries, common prescriptions — Pacific Hospital (Thai Binh Duong) inside the former Hoi An city center handles routine triage, though English is limited. Local pharmacies are widely available and stock most common medications without prescription.

For anything serious, expats go to Da Nang, roughly a 30–40 minute drive. The two standard choices are **Vinmec Da Nang** (a private hospital chain with English-speaking staff, where specialist consultations run $50–100) and **Family Medical Practice Da Nang** (a long-established expat clinic where consultations run $80–130). Both accept international insurance directly in many cases.

This is where insurance matters. A 30-minute drive to a hospital in another city is fine for routine care, but it changes the calculation for emergencies — you need coverage that includes evacuation and direct billing at private hospitals, not just reimbursement after the fact. It covers hospital stays, emergency evacuation, and unexpected illness across most of the world, which suits the Hoi An reality of needing to reach Da Nang for serious care.

*Sources: Vinmec Da Nang and Family Medical Practice published consultation fees via Alea Care insurance aggregator (January 2026).*

## Coworking and Remote Work Infrastructure

Hoi An has a small but real remote work scene, anchored by **Hub Hoi An** in Cam Chau — the original coworking space in town, with strong community programming, business-grade fiber (combined connections delivering reliable 200+ Mbps), and backup systems for the storm season. Monthly memberships run roughly $170–200, though current pricing isn't published online and you should email them directly for the latest rates. Day passes are around $9.

Beyond Hub, several cafes serve as informal work spaces — Mia Coffee, Phin Coffee, and Dingo Deli all have reliable wifi and welcome laptop workers. The trade-off versus a coworking membership is power outage exposure: cafes without generators go dark during storms, while Hub stays operational.

Internet speeds in Hoi An are generally excellent for a town this size — fiber rollouts reach all the residential expat areas — but redundancy matters more here than in Da Nang or Hanoi. Most serious remote workers either pay for a coworking membership specifically for the backup power, or keep a strong mobile data plan as a tethering fallback.

*Sources: Hub Hoi An official site (April 2026); user reviews on Coworker.com and Coliving.com, 2025–2026.*

## Visas, Residence, and the Post-Merger Reality

Vietnam still does **not** offer a digital nomad visa as of April 2026, despite recurring rumors. Long-term residents in Hoi An typically use one of three paths: multi-entry e-visas (90 days), business visas with a sponsoring company, or a Temporary Residence Card (TRC) tied to a work permit, marriage, or investment.

The post-merger change you need to know: **the Quang Nam provincial immigration office no longer exists**. All TRC processing, visa extensions, and immigration paperwork for Hoi An residents now happens at the Da Nang Immigration Department at **78 Le Loi Street, Hai Chau Ward, Da Nang City**. Local ward police in Hoi An still handle temporary residence registration (khai bao tam tru), which your landlord usually files on your behalf.

TRC fees changed in 2026 too. Under Circular 28/2026/TT-BTC, effective April 1, 2026, the new fees are $145 (1–2 years), $155 (2–5 years), $165 (5–10 years), and $10 for renewals. Our [TRC application guide](/vietnam/visa/trc-application-guide-2026) walks through the documents, timelines, and common mistakes in detail.

For visa runs, Hoi An residents almost never use the Lao Bao land border (it's 6+ hours by road). The standard play is a short flight from Da Nang International Airport to Bangkok or Phnom Penh, returning the same day or the next morning with a fresh e-visa. Round-trip flights to Bangkok run $80–180 depending on season and how far in advance you book.

## Lifestyle, Gym, and Other Recurring Costs

Hoi An has a notable yoga and wellness scene, partly because of its long-term Western expat community. Drop-in yoga classes run $8–12, and unlimited monthly memberships are around $80. Gyms range from local neighborhood spots ($25–35 per month) to better-equipped places like SuperFit Hoi An ($40–55 per month).

Vietnamese language classes are widely available — group classes run $6–10 per hour, private tutoring $10–18 per hour. Massage and spa treatments are cheap by Western standards: a one-hour massage runs $10–15 at local spots and $25–40 at upscale places. Service laundry (wash, dry, fold) is the norm rather than home washing — most expats use neighborhood laundry shops at 20,000–30,000 VND ($0.80–1.20) per kilogram.

## Hoi An vs. Da Nang vs. Hanoi: The Honest Comparison

Hoi An is roughly 10–15% cheaper than central Da Nang for housing in equivalent quality, and noticeably cheaper than Hanoi or Ho Chi Minh City. But the savings come with trade-offs that matter more than the numbers suggest:

- **You will commute to Da Nang.** For hospitals, Western groceries, the airport, larger shopping malls, and any specialist services. Budget for this in time and money.
- **Tourism dictates the rhythm.** Hoi An's economy runs on tourism, which means peak season (January–April) raises prices on everything visible to tourists, and low season (September–November, overlapping with floods) thins out the dining and event scene considerably.
- **The community is small but deep.** There are fewer expats than in Da Nang or Hanoi, but the ones who stay tend to stay for years. If you want a quiet, slower-paced base with strong local community, that's a feature. If you want anonymity and a large dating pool, less so.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I live in Hoi An on $1,000 a month?** Yes, comfortably, if you rent long-term in Cam Chau or further out, eat mostly Vietnamese food, own or rent a motorbike, and don't run AC year-round. Short stays under three months will push you above $1,200.

**Is Hoi An a good base for remote work in 2026?** For people who value quiet, community, and beach access — yes. For people who need a large coworking ecosystem, multiple Western grocery options, and same-city hospital access — Da Nang is the better fit.

**How much does rent cost in Hoi An for foreigners?** Long-term furnished rentals run $300–450 for a studio in Cam Chau, $550–850 for a 2-bedroom house, and $900–1,800+ for beachfront villas in An Bang. Short-term (under three months) adds 15–20%.

**Do I need a motorbike to live in Hoi An?** Practically, yes, unless you stay inside walking distance of the Ancient Town or An Bang Beach and use Grab for everything else. Most long-term residents either rent monthly ($60–90) or buy used ($400–1,200).

**Has Hoi An changed since the July 2025 merger?** Administratively yes — it's now three wards within Da Nang City. Day to day, life looks the same. The main practical change is that visa and TRC paperwork now happens at the Da Nang Immigration Department on Le Loi Street, not at the former Quang Nam office.
