Forget the outdated blogs claiming you can live like royalty in Vietnam for $500 a month. Post-inflation realities, surging rents in expat hubs, and tightening visa structures have completely rewritten the financial playbook for 2026. This guide breaks down exactly what you will spend, where your money goes, and the hidden costs that most new arrivals fail to budget for.

---

## Quick Answer: How Much Does It Cost to Live in Vietnam in 2026?

- **Da Nang:** $1,100–$1,600/month for a comfortable beachside lifestyle
- **Hanoi:** $1,500–$2,200/month for a solid Western/local hybrid lifestyle in Tây Hồ
- **Ho Chi Minh City:** $1,800–$2,500/month is the new baseline for comfortable living in Thảo Điền or central districts
- **Biggest budget killers:** The electricity markup trap, mandatory visa runs ($400–$1,000/year), and the 183-day tax residency trigger

## Budget Tiers by City

Your cost of living depends heavily on how much of your Western lifestyle you want to maintain. Here is the raw breakdown.

### Hanoi

**Backpacker ($800–$1,100/month):** Renting a room in a shared house or a deep-alley studio ($250–$350). Eating 90% local street food — phở, cơm bình dân — and getting around on a used 50cc scooter or Grab bikes.

**Comfortable Expat ($1,500–$2,200/month):** A private 1-bedroom apartment in Tây Hồ ($550–$800). A 50/50 mix of local and mid-range Western or Japanese dining. Getting around via Grab cars or a rented modern scooter like a Honda Airblade.

**Premium ($3,000+/month):** A high-end Vinhomes 2-bedroom or West Lake view apartment ($1,200–$1,800). Fine dining, shopping at imported grocery stores, and taking Xanh SM rides everywhere.

### Ho Chi Minh City

Expect to pay 15–20% more than Hanoi across the board.

**Backpacker ($900–$1,200/month):** Budget studios in District 4 or Bình Thạnh.

**Comfortable Expat ($1,800–$2,500/month):** A 1-bedroom in Thảo Điền or bordering District 1/District 3 ($700–$1,000). Socialising and alcohol costs are noticeably higher here than in Hanoi.

**Premium ($3,500+/month):** Shared villas in Thảo Điền or luxury District 1 condos, paired with high-end gym memberships and frequent fine dining.

### Da Nang

**Backpacker ($600–$850/month):** A small studio in Sơn Trà or the far end of An Thượng ($250–$350).

**Comfortable Expat ($1,100–$1,600/month):** A modern 1-bedroom or 2-bedroom near the beach ($450–$600). Seafood and local lifestyle costs remain the cheapest of the three cities.

**Premium ($2,000+/month):** Villas with private pools or high-floor apartments with panoramic ocean views.

## Monthly Expense Breakdown

### Housing

A serviced apartment — utilities, Wi-Fi, cleaning included — typically costs $150–$250 more per month than a standard condo where you manage your own bills. For long stays over three months, switching to a standard apartment and managing utilities yourself usually saves enough to justify the hassle.

### Food and Dining

- **100% local street food:** $150–$250/month (30,000–50,000 VND per meal)
- **Mixed diet (local weekdays, Western weekends):** $350–$500/month
- **Primarily Western restaurants:** $600–$900+/month — a burger or pizza combo runs $10–$16, comparable to secondary cities in Europe or North America
- **Heavy delivery reliance (once daily):** $150–$250/month factoring in delivery fees and platform markups

### Transport

**App-hailing (Grab/Xanh SM):** Two short car rides per day costs $100–$150/month.

**Owning or renting a motorbike:** Monthly running costs are $25–$30 ($15 fuel, $5–$10 parking, $5 for oil changes). If you stay long-term, buying a bike pays for itself within three months compared to daily Grab usage.

### Medical, Insurance, and Dental

Healthcare in Vietnam offers a significant disparity between local clinics and international hospitals.

**Insurance:** A standard nomad health policy like [SafetyWing Nomad Insurance](https://safetywing.com/?referenceID=26505542&utm_source=26505542&utm_medium=Ambassador) costs approximately $56.28 per 4 weeks (ages 10–39), or around $73/month. Prices scale up with age. Review the current policy terms carefully, particularly for motorbike coverage conditions.

**Outpatient visits:** A consultation at a mid-tier private local hospital (Hoàn Mỹ) costs $15–$30. A top-tier international facility like FV Hospital or Vinmec runs $60–$120 before tests or medication.

**Dental:** Vietnam is well-regarded for dental costs. A standard teeth cleaning and polishing at a premium English-speaking clinic costs $15–$30. Many expats also get Invisalign or implants here at prices significantly lower than Western standards.

## Hidden Costs: What Blows Most Budgets

### 1. The 183-Day Tax Trap

Many digital nomads assume that doing E-visa runs every 90 days keeps them outside Vietnamese tax residency. This is only partially true.

Under Vietnamese tax law, you become a tax resident — liable for taxes on your **global income** — if any of the following apply:

- You spend 183 days or more in Vietnam within a calendar year, or within any consecutive 12-month period from your first entry date
- You hold a residential lease for 183 days or more (even across multiple short leases or hotel stays)
- You hold a Temporary Residence Card (TRC)

The lease trigger catches many people off guard. A 6-month apartment contract signed on day one, combined with a visa run, can technically establish tax residency even if you are not physically present for 183 days. In practice, the tax authority currently has limited capacity to track the global income of E-visa holders without local employer registration — but for anyone applying for a TRC or working locally, this becomes a hard legal reality. Hiring a local accountant for annual tax finalization costs $150–$300.

### 2. Visa Runs

Every 90 days on an E-visa means leaving Vietnam and re-entering. Depending on whether you take the bus from HCMC to Mộc Bài (Cambodia) or fly to Bangkok or Kuala Lumpur, the annualised cost lands between $400 and $1,000.

### 3. ATM Fees and Exchange Rate Losses

Most standard local bank ATMs (Vietcombank, Agribank, BIDV) cap withdrawals at 2–5 million VND per transaction ($80–$200), with fees of 22,000–55,000 VND charged per withdrawal. Some banks like VPBank allow up to 10 million VND per transaction. Withdrawing frequently in small amounts with a standard foreign bank card can cost $30–$50/month in cumulative fees and exchange rate margins. Using a Wise card registered outside Vietnam for ATM withdrawals is a significantly more cost-efficient alternative.

### 4. The Pet Premium

Very few apartments allow pets. Those that do often require an extra month's deposit or a 10–15% rent increase. Combined with heavy import taxes on premium pet food, expect to add $100–$150/month if you bring an animal.

### 5. First-Month Setup Costs

These one-time costs hit hard in month one and are rarely mentioned in cost-of-living estimates:

- Air purifier + two filter replacements (non-optional in Hanoi): ~$180 first year
- Quality motorcycle helmet and raincoat: ~$45
- Visa run (first one within 90 days): $80–$200

## Budget Traps: Where Expats Bleed Money

**The electricity markup.** Many serviced apartments embed an illegal fixed electricity rate of 4,000+ VND/kWh into the lease. Running AC heavily during summer at this rate costs $40–$80 more per month than the government-regulated rate. Prioritise apartments with an independent electricity meter. See our [Housing Guide](/vietnam/housing/avoid-rental-scams-vietnam-2026) for full details.

**The imported supermarket tax.** Buying cheese, wine, or standard vegetables at premium expat supermarkets like Annam Gourmet or L's Place inflates your grocery bill by 200–300% compared to local WinMart stores. The same block of imported cheddar costs four times more.

**The no-menu markup.** Sitting down at a local street stall and ordering without asking the price (*Bao nhiêu tiền?*) is a reliable way to receive a foreigner-adjusted bill. Ask first.

**Weekend domestic travel burn.** Vietnam's cheap domestic flights make weekend trips to Đà Lạt or Phú Quốc extremely tempting. A two-person weekend trip typically costs $250–$400 all-in. One per month adds $3,000–$5,000 to your annual budget — a figure that rarely appears in monthly cost-of-living estimates.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much does a cleaner cost in Vietnam?**

Using a local app like bTaskee for a standard 2-hour deep clean in Hanoi or HCMC costs 150,000–250,000 VND ($6–$10). This is one of the genuinely cheap services that long-term residents use regularly.

**Is Vietnam cheaper than Thailand for expats?**

At the comfortable expat level, Vietnam is marginally cheaper — primarily due to lower food costs and cheaper local services. The gap narrows significantly if you live a Western-heavy lifestyle in either country.

**What is the cheapest city in Vietnam for expats?**

Da Nang. The combination of lower rents, cheap seafood, and a compact layout where everything is reachable by motorbike makes it the highest-value city for expats on a $1,000–$1,500/month budget.

**Can you live comfortably in Vietnam on $1,500/month?**

Yes, in Hanoi or Da Nang. In HCMC, $1,500/month is tight for a comfortable single-occupancy lifestyle in an expat-friendly neighbourhood. Budget $1,800–$2,000 as a realistic baseline for HCMC.
