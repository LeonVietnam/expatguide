## The short answer

Most expats should not treat grocery shopping in Vietnam as a one-store decision. The practical setup is usually:

- **one mainstream chain for weekly restocks** — usually WinMart, Co.opmart, or Bách Hóa Xanh, depending on what is near home
- **one specialty stop for imported or Korean items** — usually Annam Gourmet or K-Market
- **one delivery fallback** — GrabMart or the chain’s own online store for top-ups, heavy items, or late orders
- **wet markets as a second-step option** — useful for fresh produce and local shopping habits, but not the easiest week-one choice if language, payment, or food-handling confidence is still low

For most new arrivals, the safest week-one move is to start with **WinMart or Co.opmart**, add **Bách Hóa Xanh** if a nearby branch looks produce-heavy, use **Annam Gourmet or K-Market** only for specific items, and treat **wet markets as optional** until the local routine is clearer.

A concrete week-one starting map:
- **WinMart:** winmart.vn or the WinMart app
- **Bách Hóa Xanh:** bachhoaxanh.com or the Bách Hóa Xanh app
- **Co.opmart:** cooponline.vn or the Co.op Online app
- **GrabMart:** the Grab app
- **Annam Gourmet:** shop.annam-gourmet.com
- **K-Market:** the K-Market store locator on k-market.vn
- **Wet markets:** search “chợ gần tôi” on Google Maps, or ask the building guard, landlord, or receptionist which nearby market residents actually use

*Price and delivery examples below are approximate and based on current store websites and policy pages checked in April 2026. Stock, distance, branch coverage, and promotions vary by address.*

## Which grocery option fits which job

### Use a mainstream chain for the default weekly shop

If the goal is one reasonably easy grocery run with predictable checkout, packaged staples, household items, and less language friction, start here.

- **WinMart** is a practical first check when the priority is broad everyday coverage, online ordering, and easier card-based payment.
- **Co.opmart / Co.op Online** is strong for standard family-style restocks and larger baskets.
- **Bách Hóa Xanh** is worth checking first when cooking at home is the priority and the nearby branch looks more food-first than household-first.

Current decision anchors:
- **WinMart:** a current 1L Vinamilk milk listing shows **38,300 VND**, with the site stating **free delivery from 300,000 VND within 5km** and otherwise charging by distance.
- **Co.op Online:** a current 10-egg Co.op Select listing shows roughly **29,500–30,700 VND**, and the delivery policy states **free delivery from 200,000 VND within 6km**.
- **Bách Hóa Xanh:** the delivery policy shows several speed options, from roughly **1 hour / 4 hours / 24 hours**, with current fees and promotions varying by order type and distance.

What this means in practice:
- For **local staples**, mainstream chains usually give the lowest-friction balance of price, stock visibility, and checkout ease.
- For a **small weekly shop**, these stores are usually the best first answer before trying more specialized formats.
- For **foreign card users**, chain websites and larger branches are still a safer bet than informal market shopping. For broader payment backup planning, see [International Cards in Vietnam](/vietnam/banking/international-cards-vietnam-2026).

## When to pick WinMart, Bách Hóa Xanh, or Co.opmart first

These chains are not identical.

### Pick WinMart first if the priority is convenience and clean fallback options

WinMart is often the easiest first chain for a new expat because the brand footprint is large, the online flow is clear, and the payment policy is explicit about international card networks for online checkout.

It is a good fit for:
- packaged groceries
- milk, eggs, snacks, drinks, and basic household supplies
- first-week shopping before a local routine is built
- orders where a rough **same-day / within-2-hours** target matters more than squeezing every last bit of value

It is a weaker fit when:
- the nearest branch is small and light on fresh items
- the shopping list is produce-heavy
- the goal is imported pantry shopping rather than a normal local-chain basket

### Pick Bách Hóa Xanh first if daily cooking matters more than imported choice

Bách Hóa Xanh leans more obviously into food and fresh categories than a generic supermarket-plus-household format.

It is a good fit for:
- vegetables, meat, eggs, tofu, and cooking basics
- short-notice restocks for home cooking
- neighborhoods where the nearest branch feels more like a fresh-food store than a mini mart

It is a weaker fit when:
- the list includes imported pantry items, cheese, cereal brands, or Western snacks
- the buyer wants the cleanest English-language experience
- the exact delivery fee matters, because current fee logic is promotion-heavy and can shift

### Pick Co.opmart first for a fuller household basket

Co.opmart works best when the trip is not just “buy tonight’s dinner” but “restock the kitchen and a few home basics in one run.”

It is a good fit for:
- larger carts
- family-style shopping
- buyers who want an established local supermarket format with official web and app ordering

It is a weaker fit when:
- the nearest branch is not close enough to make the basket efficient
- the need is urgent top-up convenience rather than a fuller shop
- the goal is Korean or premium imported shopping

## International supermarkets are not the default — they are the specialty layer

### Use Annam Gourmet for imported and premium groceries

Annam Gourmet is the better answer when the question is not “where should groceries be bought?” but “where can specific imported or premium items be found without too much hunting?”

Current decision anchors:
- Annam’s service page states delivery within a **10km radius** from the store handling the order.
- The same page states **free delivery from 1,000,000 VND within 4km** for standard customers, with stronger benefits for top-tier members.
- Current product pages show wide price spread even within everyday categories: local-style milk can still sit around **37,000–50,000 VND**, while imported or organic 1L milk can move closer to **90,000 VND**; 10-egg listings can sit around **40,000–50,000 VND**.

That makes Annam useful for:
- cheese, yogurt, deli items, imported pantry goods, and harder-to-find Western groceries
- buyers who care more about selection and product familiarity than about lowest basket cost
- planned top-up orders in Hanoi or HCMC where the branch network is actually nearby

It is not the best default for:
- lowest-cost weekly shopping
- “just get the basics” grocery runs
- readers outside the stronger branch zones of major cities

### Use K-Market when the shopping list is specifically Korean

K-Market is better treated as a Korean-food specialist than as a universal supermarket answer.

Use it for:
- Korean sauces, noodles, snacks, frozen items, side-dish ingredients, and pantry restocks
- expats whose regular home cooking already leans Korean
- shoppers willing to go to a specific branch instead of expecting one brand to cover all grocery needs

Do not treat it as the first answer for:
- a broad household restock
- low-cost local staples
- nationwide uniform availability

## Wet markets are useful, but not the easiest week-one option

Wet markets can be excellent for fresh produce, herbs, fish, meat, and local buying habits. They are also where confidence matters more.

The trade-off is simple:
- **best case:** better fresh selection for some local items, quick neighborhood shopping, and lower prices on some produce
- **hard part:** more variation in hygiene standards, product labeling, language, weighing, and payment style

Practical guidance for new expats:
- Go **early in the morning** if freshness and full selection matter.
- Bring a **small cash buffer**, not just cards.
- Start with a **small produce-only test run** before buying meat or seafood.
- Treat QR as a **possible bonus, not a guarantee**.

A rough public benchmark still helps. Current open-market price reporting for common items such as Chinese cabbage, eggplant, watermelon, and guava shows examples around **12,900–35,000 VND per kg**, but that is only a snapshot and should not be treated as a fixed wet-market price list.

Current reality is mixed. Traditional-market digital payment is expanding, and QR use is spreading beyond formal retail. But that does **not** mean every stall will take it, or that every first-time buyer will have a smooth shopping experience without cash or basic Vietnamese.

A reasonable starting approach is:
- use wet markets for **vegetables, herbs, fruit, and simple repeat buys**
- move into meat or seafood only after finding a stall that feels consistent
- keep the first visit short and observational rather than trying to replace the full supermarket shop on day one

## Delivery apps are for convenience, not for winning on total cost

### Use GrabMart for top-ups, urgent basics, and heavy items

GrabMart is strongest when the question is speed.

It is a good fit for:
- forgotten items
- same-day groceries during a workday
- heavy drinks, water, detergent, and other items better delivered than carried
- buyers who want to compare several nearby stores without leaving home

Current decision anchors:
- Grab positions GrabMart as grocery and essentials delivery across major Vietnamese cities.
- The service describes delivery in **under 1 hour** in many cases, but visible store options still depend on the delivery radius from the address.
- Total cost is usually **store price plus delivery and service fees**, so it is better for convenience than for lowest final bill.

Use it less for:
- careful budget shopping
- branch-specific product quality decisions
- a fully planned imported-food order where a specialty store’s own site may be clearer

### Use the chain’s own online store when the order is planned

If the basket is already known and the goal is to restock from one retailer, the chain’s own site is often the cleaner option.

That usually means:
- **WinMart** for a predictable mainstream basket and clearer online payment methods
- **Bách Hóa Xanh** for food-first restocks when the nearby branch supports the right items
- **Co.op Online** for fuller supermarket baskets with a scheduled time window
- **Annam Gourmet** for premium and imported shopping where branch radius is already known

This route is usually better than GrabMart when:
- the order is not urgent
- a free-delivery threshold is realistically reachable
- one store already covers most of the basket

## A simple grocery setup that works for most expats

### If the priority is “easy and predictable”
1. Start with **WinMart or Co.opmart** for the first two weeks.
2. Save **GrabMart** for top-ups, heavy items, or bad-weather days.
3. Add **Annam Gourmet** only when a specific imported gap appears.

### If the priority is “I cook at home most days”
1. Check the nearest **Bách Hóa Xanh** first.
2. Keep **WinMart or Co.opmart** as the packaged-staples backup.
3. Test one nearby **wet market** for vegetables and herbs after the basic supermarket routine works.

### If the priority is “I need Korean groceries regularly”
1. Keep **K-Market** as the specialty stop.
2. Use a mainstream chain for water, eggs, cleaning supplies, and standard basics.
3. Use delivery only when the basket is heavy or time is tight.

## The most common week-one mistake

The usual mistake is trying to force one format to do everything.

- Wet markets are not the best first answer for every new expat.
- Premium supermarkets are not the best place to buy the full weekly basket.
- Delivery apps are not the cheapest way to stock a kitchen.
- A small nearby branch is not automatically better than a slightly farther store that actually matches the shopping list.

The better approach is to build a **three-layer routine**:
1. one mainstream chain
2. one specialty backup if needed
3. one delivery fallback

That setup is faster, easier to repeat, and closer to how daily shopping in Vietnam actually works.

## What sits just outside this guide

This article focuses on grocery-first channels: mainstream chains, international supermarkets, wet markets, and delivery apps.

Other useful options exist, but they are not the main scope here:
- **convenience stores** for drinks, snacks, and emergency top-ups rather than real grocery runs
- **Shopee, Lazada, and other marketplaces** for bulk household items, pantry refills, or non-urgent deals rather than fresh-food shopping
