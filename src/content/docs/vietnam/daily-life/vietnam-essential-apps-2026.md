Landing in Vietnam without the right apps is like arriving without shoes. Some things you can sort out in a day; others will catch you off guard at the worst possible moment. This guide covers exactly what you need in your first week, in the order you will need it — with honest notes on what requires a local bank account and what works straight off the plane.

**The single most important thing to do on arrival:** Buy a local SIM card before leaving the arrivals hall. Viettel and Vinaphone both have counters at major airports. A real-name registered Vietnamese SIM costs $3–5 and requires your passport. It is the key that unlocks most of what follows — Zalo registration, Grab verification, bank OTP codes. Without it, your first week will be significantly harder.

## Ride-Hailing

**Grab — install this first**

Grab is the default for getting around Vietnam. Cars, motorbike taxis, and food delivery all in one app. You can pay directly with an international Visa or Mastercard — no local bank account needed. The English interface works well in all major cities.

One practical note: drivers often call to confirm the pickup location. If you do not speak Vietnamese, use the in-app chat with the auto-translate feature. Most drivers will figure it out.

**Xanh SM — install alongside Grab**

Xanh SM is VinGroup's electric ride-hailing fleet — the distinctive green cars and bikes you will see across Hanoi and HCMC. Accepts international cards, full English interface. The motorbikes are quieter and cleaner than petrol alternatives, which matters more than you expect once you experience Hanoi's air quality. Prices are roughly on par with Grab, sometimes slightly lower. Worth having as a parallel option, especially during rain when Grab demand surges.

**Be — emergency backup**

Vietnam's local ride-hailing alternative. Accepts international cards and also allows cash payment, which is useful on day one. Not as polished as Grab but worth having installed for peak surge periods.

**Baemin — do not install, it no longer exists**

Baemin exited the Vietnamese market in late 2023. Any guide still recommending it is outdated.

## Payments: The Honest Picture

This is the section most guides get wrong.

Vietnam has a highly developed digital payment ecosystem — MoMo, ZaloPay, and VNPay dominate local transactions. As a newly arrived foreigner without a local bank account, you cannot use any of them. All three require a Vietnamese bank account linked to a local ID or TRC. There is no workaround for the first week.

**What actually works without a local bank account:**

**Cash (VND) — non-negotiable**

Carry 1–2 million VND in small notes (50k and 100k denominations) at all times. Street food, local cafés, market stalls, and motorbike taxis often accept nothing else. Exchange at a bank or official counter for the best rates — avoid airport counters.

**International Visa/Mastercard — works at modern venues**

Accepted at chains (Starbucks, Circle K, GS25, Lotte Mart, larger restaurants). Does not work for Vietnam's QR payment system (VietQR), which is what local businesses use.

**Apple Pay / Google Pay — works where cards work**

Useful at the same venues as physical cards. Does not extend your reach to local QR payments.

Once you have a Vietnamese bank account, MoMo unlocks a huge range of daily transactions — bill payments, rent, splitting costs. See our [Banking Guide](/vietnam/banking/bank-account-guide-2026) for the full process.

## Air Quality

**AirVisual (IQAir) — essential for Hanoi, useful everywhere**

If you are moving to Hanoi, this app belongs on your home screen from day one. Hanoi consistently ranks among the world's most polluted major cities — in December 2025 it briefly held the top spot globally. Checking the AQI before leaving the apartment is a daily habit for long-term residents, not an overreaction.

A reading above 150 means wearing an N95 or KN95 mask outdoors is genuinely advisable. Above 200, reconsider outdoor plans entirely. The app requires no registration, is entirely in English, and works without a local account.

For HCMC and Da Nang, air quality is generally better, but the app remains useful during haze season and high-traffic periods.

## Maps and Navigation

**Google Maps — the only map app you need**

Accuracy in Vietnamese cities is excellent. Business listings, opening hours, and walking routes are all reliable. Download offline maps for your city before leaving the airport — useful when you are in a building with poor signal.

One known limitation: Google Maps cannot always distinguish between a car-accessible road and a narrow alley passable only by motorbike. In older residential areas of Hanoi (around Tây Hồ) and HCMC's District 1, check road width visually before committing. Your Grab driver uses the same app, so sharing your screen via Zalo works better than trying to explain verbally if they appear lost.

**Maps.me — skip it**

Mobile data in Vietnam costs $3–5 per month. Offline maps solve a problem that does not exist here, and Maps.me's database is significantly out of date for Vietnamese streets.

## Booking Flights and Hotels

**Traveloka — for domestic flights**

Traveloka is the dominant booking platform across Southeast Asia and handles Vietnam's domestic routes particularly well. Booking VietJet or Vietnam Airlines through Traveloka is more reliable than using the airlines' own apps, which have a documented history of charging international cards and then failing to issue tickets. Full English interface, fast confirmation, reliable customer support.

**Agoda — for hotels**

Agoda consistently has the widest hotel inventory in Vietnam, particularly for local boutique hotels and guesthouses that do not appear on international platforms. Prices typically run 10–15% lower than Booking.com for comparable properties in Vietnam. Worth checking both, but Agoda usually wins on price and selection locally.

**Airline apps — use only as a boarding pass viewer**

Download VietJet or Vietnam Airlines apps after booking elsewhere, purely to access your boarding pass. Do not book through them directly with an international card.

A note on Bamboo Airways: after a difficult period of restructuring, the airline resumed full domestic and international operations in late 2025 and is a viable option again. It appears on Traveloka.

## Food Delivery

**Grab Food — your default for the first week**

Built directly into the Grab app you already have. Accepts the same international card you used for the ride. The best English interface and menu translations of any Vietnamese delivery platform. For the first week, this is all you need.

**ShopeeFood — worth switching to after you settle in**

ShopeeFood has the widest restaurant selection and lowest delivery fees in Vietnam, but the app is heavily Vietnamese and cash-on-delivery is the only option without a local payment method. Once you have a bank account linked, it becomes the more economical choice for regular orders.

**Capichi — for higher-end or expat-focused eating**

A smaller platform targeting the expat, Japanese, and Korean resident market. Focuses on quality western restaurants, Japanese food, and health-focused options. Full English interface, accepts international cards. A reliable option for the first few days when you want to eat well without risk.

**GrabMart — for day-one essentials**

For your first few days — water, toilet paper, basic snacks — GrabMart inside the Grab app is the fastest option. The local WinMart and Bách Hóa Xanh apps require Vietnamese bank registration and are not worth the friction until you are settled.

## Shopping and Essentials

**Shopee — for everything you need to buy in week one**

A power adapter, a cheap helmet, bed sheets, a water filter, a fan — the things you realise you need two days after arriving. Getting to a physical store without transport or Vietnamese is painful. Shopee solves this.

Shopee is Vietnam's dominant e-commerce platform. In Hanoi and HCMC, same-day or next-day delivery is standard for most items. Critically for new arrivals: **Cash on Delivery (COD) is available on most listings**, which means you can order without a local bank account or payment method.

Practical note: the delivery driver will always call before arrival. Have Zalo ready to receive a location pin or send a simple confirmation. Have small change available — drivers rarely carry change for large notes.

The app interface mixes English and Vietnamese, but the search, cart, and checkout flow is manageable. Searching in English returns results for most products.

## Communication

**Zalo — you cannot function in Vietnam without this**

Zalo is Vietnam's primary messaging app, used for everything: landlord communication, package delivery coordination, restaurant reservations, property agents, building security. Your Vietnamese contacts are on Zalo, not WhatsApp.

**Registration — get the local SIM first, then install Zalo**

Do not attempt to register Zalo with your home country number. Zalo's 2025–2026 anti-fraud system is aggressive — foreign numbers frequently trigger account restrictions during or immediately after registration, and recovering a locked account without a Vietnamese ID is effectively impossible.

The correct sequence: airport → buy Viettel or Vinaphone SIM with your passport → install Zalo → register with your new +84 number. The whole process takes under 30 minutes and works reliably. This is one more reason the airport SIM purchase is the first thing you do.

**WhatsApp** — useful within expat circles and for staying in touch with international contacts, but has almost no penetration among local Vietnamese. Do not expect landlords or local contacts to use it.

**Google Translate — genuinely useful day-to-day**

The camera/Lens feature for real-time text translation handles menus, street signs, and documents reliably. Download the Vietnamese offline language pack before you arrive so it works without signal.

## Second-Hand and Classifieds

**Facebook Groups — best for expat-to-expat transactions**

For furniture, appliances, second-hand bikes, and local recommendations, Facebook groups are the most active channel in Vietnam. Key groups to join:

- **Hanoi Massive Community** — general expat hub for Hanoi
- **Hanoi Massive Housing / HCMC Housing** — apartment rentals and sublets
- **Hanoi/HCMC Expats** — city-specific general groups

Facebook Marketplace is also active for larger second-hand items. An air purifier is a practical early purchase for Hanoi — second-hand units in good condition come up regularly.

**Chợ Tốt — approach with caution**

Vietnam's equivalent of Craigslist. Almost entirely in Vietnamese, and the motorbike listings section has a high incidence of fraudulent ownership documents. Do not buy anything significant from Chợ Tốt in your first month without a trusted Vietnamese speaker present to verify documents in person.
