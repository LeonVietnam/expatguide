Public transport in Hanoi and HCMC is now good enough for some daily commutes, but it is still not a clean citywide replacement for Grab or a motorbike.

If both ends of the trip sit near a metro station or a direct bus corridor, public transport can be the cheapest low-stress option. If the commute involves weak last-mile access, late-night trips, heavy rain, school runs, or multiple transfers, most expats still end up using a mixed setup: metro or bus when it fits, GrabBike when it does not.

*Data note: fares, hours, and payment options below are approximate as of April 2026 and can change.*

## What is actually running in 2026

Hanoi currently has two urban rail services in use: Line 2A Cat Linh–Ha Dong and the currently operating section of Line 3.1 Nhon–Hanoi Station. HCMC currently has one metro line in daily service: Metro Line 1 Ben Thanh–Suoi Tien.

City buses remain the broadest public-transport layer in both cities. Vietnam Railways is still useful for intercity and a few regional edge cases, but it is not a substitute for dense inner-city public transport.

Official maps and rollout language can make both cities look more complete than they feel in everyday use. For commuting, corridor fit matters more than the network dream map.

## Hanoi: when the metro is enough

Hanoi Metro currently runs from about 05:30 to 22:00. Current fare anchors are roughly 9,000-19,000 VND per trip depending on line and distance, with day passes around 40,000 VND.

That makes the metro a strong option only when the route is repeatable and both ends of the commute sit close to the active lines. It works far better for a predictable home-to-work pattern than for random cross-city errands.

Officially, the system now uses newer digital ticketing and gate logic, especially on Line 3.1. Practically, first-time riders should still allow extra time and check current station or app instructions instead of relying on older blog posts.

For housing decisions, living near an actual station matters much more than living somewhere that only looks close on a city map. See [Best Neighborhoods in Hanoi](/vietnam/housing/best-neighborhoods-hanoi-expats-2026).

## Hanoi: buses are often the cheapest answer, not always the easiest

For pure cost, Hanoi buses are usually the strongest value. Current monthly pass anchors are about 140,000 VND for one route and 280,000 VND for all routes.

That only helps if the route is direct. A bus that is cheap on paper can still be a bad daily commute if it adds a long walk, crowded transfers, or a second ride in rain.

For many expats, the better Hanoi setup is not bus only or metro only. It is one direct bus, or metro plus a short GrabBike, with current route planning handled through operator tools and transport apps. See [Essential Apps in Vietnam](/vietnam/daily-life/vietnam-essential-apps-2026).

## HCMC: Metro Line 1 is excellent if your corridor matches it

Metro Line 1 is the biggest public-transport improvement in HCMC, but it is still one line, not a full network. It works best for trips that actually sit on the Ben Thanh–Binh Thanh–Thu Duc corridor.

Current fare anchors are roughly 7,000-20,000 VND cash, 6,000-19,000 VND cashless, 40,000 VND for a 1-day pass, 90,000 VND for a 3-day pass, and 300,000 VND for a monthly pass. End to end, the full line takes about half an hour.

As currently reported in early 2026, trains generally start around 05:00, run until 22:00 from Monday to Thursday, and until 23:00 from Friday to Sunday.

Officially, payment and app integration have improved a lot. Practically, it is still smart to keep a backup payment method and not assume every trip will feel like a fully unified subway system.

For apartment selection, living near Line 1 changes the daily-transport math much more than living near a random bus stop. See [Best Neighborhoods in HCMC](/vietnam/housing/best-neighborhoods-hcmc-expats-2026).

## HCMC buses: wider reach, slower reality

HCMC buses cover far more city than the metro. On subsidized routes, official fares are very low and commonly start around 5,000 VND on shorter routes, then rise by route length, so it is better not to think of the whole system as one neat flat-fare network.

What makes HCMC buses more usable than before is the feeder-bus layer around Metro Line 1 and tools like MultiGo. What still limits them is speed, heat, transfers, and uneven last-mile fit.

The city is also discussing broader bus-fare support in 2026, but that should not be read as a stable citywide free-fare reality yet. In practice, some routes and some promotions can be very cheap, but daily usefulness still depends much more on directness and transfer quality than on the headline fare.

If the bus route feeds Metro Line 1 cleanly, public transport can work well. If it requires two slow surface transfers, GrabBike often wins on time and stress even when it loses on price.

## Where Vietnam Railways fits

Use Vietnam Railways for intercity or regional station-to-station trips, not as the default answer to daily city commuting.

The official booking and timetable starting points are dsvn.vn and vr.com.vn. The right use case is a specific regional pattern where both ends sit near stations. The wrong use case is assuming national rail will replace buses, metro, or Grab for ordinary city movement.

For most expats in Hanoi and HCMC, rail is occasional infrastructure, not the base layer of daily transport.

## GrabBike, GrabCar, or a personal motorbike: when they still win

GrabBike still wins when speed matters more than comfort: short solo trips, station-to-door last mile, missed bus windows, and rush-hour point-to-point runs.

A short urban GrabBike trip often falls somewhere around the mid-teens to low tens of thousands of VND, while GrabCar sits in a higher price tier and gets expensive much faster in rain, traffic, or airport-style trips. For commuting every day, that gap adds up quickly.

A personal motorbike remains the most flexible option if the route changes often, the home/work pair sits off-corridor, or daily life includes shopping, school runs, and late nights. Basic long-term rentals often start from roughly 1.5 million to 2 million+ VND per month before fuel and parking. But that flexibility comes with licensing, safety, parking, and weather trade-offs. See [Motorbike Guide for Vietnam](/vietnam/daily-life/motorbike-guide-2026) before treating a bike as the obvious answer.

## Which option usually wins for daily commuting

If the goal is lowest cost, a direct bus route usually wins.

If the goal is the best balance between cost and predictability, metro wins when the corridor matches the trip.

If the goal is fastest point-to-point movement in heavy traffic, GrabBike usually wins.

If the goal is full flexibility across changing routes, late nights, and errands, a personal motorbike still wins.

If the goal is regional or intercity movement rather than city commuting, Vietnam Railways becomes relevant.

## What most expats should actually do

Choose transport in this order:

1. Check whether home and work can both sit near an active metro corridor.
2. If not, check whether one direct bus route solves most weekdays.
3. If not, assume a mixed setup: public transport when it fits, GrabBike when it does not.
4. Move to a personal motorbike only if the route pattern really demands it.

If budget matters most, public transport is much cheaper than repeated ride-hailing. See [Cost of Living in Vietnam](/vietnam/daily-life/cost-of-living-vietnam-2026).

For 2026, the realistic answer in Vietnam’s biggest cities is usually not public transport only or motorbike only. It is choosing the cheapest usable base layer for the route actually done every day, then keeping Grab as the fallback when the system stops being convenient.
