## Quick answer

Foreigners can usually get cash from ATMs in Vietnam, but the safest setup depends on the type of card and account being used.

For a foreign debit card, the main risks are not just the ATM fee. The full cost can include the Vietnamese ATM operator fee, the home bank's overseas withdrawal fee, the card issuer's FX markup, and any dynamic currency conversion screen the reader accepts by mistake.

For a foreigner with a Vietnamese bank account, the ATM itself may be less expensive, but account access can become more sensitive to passport updates, permitted-stay documents, local phone access, bank app setup, and biometric identity checks.

For 2026, the key point is this: Vietnam's biometric banking rules should not be read as a blanket rule that every foreign-card ATM withdrawal requires Vietnamese biometric verification. They matter more for local bank accounts, bank apps, card issuance, electronic card transactions, and identity updates.

The safest cash-access plan is:

- use bank-branded ATMs in staffed or visible locations;
- withdraw in VND and decline home-currency conversion when the screen is clear;
- test a smaller amount before relying on one machine for a large withdrawal;
- keep at least two payment options, ideally from different issuers;
- keep local bank account details, passport data, phone number, and biometric updates current if using a Vietnamese bank account.

For card-payment fallback, see our guide to [using international cards in Vietnam](/vietnam/banking/international-cards-vietnam-2026). For domestic QR and transfer fallback, see [sending money within Vietnam](/vietnam/banking/send-money-within-vietnam-2026).

A short-stay visitor with a foreign Visa or Mastercard card is not in the same position as a long-stay resident using a Vietnamese bank card. A person with a local bank account but outdated passport or stay-document records is different again.

The article treats these as separate scenarios because the risk is different.

## How ATM withdrawals in Vietnam work

A cash withdrawal can involve several parties at once:

1. the Vietnamese ATM operator;
2. the international card network;
3. the reader's home bank or card issuer;
4. the account behind the card;
5. the ATM screen choice for conversion;
6. the local bank account and bank app, if using a Vietnamese card or cardless withdrawal.

That is why one person may withdraw cash easily while another person using a different card at the same machine gets a lower limit, a fee warning, a declined transaction, or a fraud block.

For foreign-card users, the country-level answer is only the starting point. The real result is a machine-bank-card-issuer combination.

## ATM fees: what foreigners should expect

Vietnam ATM fees are not safe to summarize with one national number.

A foreign-card withdrawal can include up to four cost layers.

| Cost layer | Who charges it | What to check |
|---|---|---|
| Local ATM operator fee | Vietnamese ATM bank | Fee screen before confirming the withdrawal |
| Overseas ATM fee | Home bank or card issuer | Card issuer's fee schedule |
| FX markup | Home bank, card issuer, or card network | Card terms and account currency |
| Dynamic currency conversion | ATM operator / DCC provider | Whether the ATM offers to convert into home currency |

The local ATM fee is only one piece. A machine that shows a lower Vietnamese-side fee may still be expensive if the card issuer adds a high overseas withdrawal fee or poor exchange rate. A machine that offers home-currency conversion may also be expensive even if the withdrawal itself works.

For cost planning, use the fee stack below instead of one national ATM price.

| Cost item | 2026 planning treatment | Verified / safer anchor |
|---|---|---|
| Vietnamese ATM operator fee | Not safe to give as one national range; check the ATM screen before confirming | VietinBank states that foreign-issued Visa, Mastercard, and DCI cards are charged 4%, minimum 80,000 VND, at VietinBank ATMs from 16 November 2025 |
| Other Vietnamese bank ATM operator fees | Treat as bank- and machine-specific unless the bank publishes a current foreign-card fee | Unknown in this draft unless the ATM screen or bank page confirms it |
| Home-bank overseas ATM fee | Depends on the issuing bank and card product | Check the card issuer's current fee schedule |
| FX markup | Depends on issuer, account currency, and network handling | Check the posted transaction after withdrawal |
| Dynamic currency conversion | Optional screen choice when offered; can make the transaction more expensive | Choose VND / decline conversion when the screen is clear |

This means the reader-usable cost band is not a single Vietnam-wide number. The safer planning range is a variable stack: Vietnamese ATM fee, issuer fee, FX treatment, and any DCC cost. The only hard bank-specific anchor used here is the VietinBank foreign-issued-card example above.

## Withdrawal limits: do not plan around one national number

ATM withdrawal limits in Vietnam are layered.

A withdrawal can be limited by:

- the ATM screen limit;
- the Vietnamese ATM operator;
- the cash loaded in the machine;
- the reader's home bank daily limit;
- card-network risk controls;
- card type;
- fraud detection;
- local account status, if using a Vietnamese bank card.

For foreign cards, the screen may show a per-transaction limit that is lower than the card's home-bank limit. Trying multiple withdrawals can sometimes work, but it can also trigger a fraud block or daily cap.

For local bank cards, the limit may depend on the Vietnamese bank account, card tier, ATM network, and bank app settings.

The safe planning rule is simple: do not arrive in Vietnam needing one ATM transaction to solve a large cash requirement. For rent deposits, school fees, business payments, or moving costs, use a bank transfer, local account, or planned cash strategy instead of relying on one machine.

## Should you accept the ATM conversion rate?

Usually, no.

When an ATM offers to charge the card in the reader's home currency, that is dynamic currency conversion. It may look reassuring because the screen shows a familiar currency, but it can lock in a weaker conversion than the card issuer would otherwise use.

The safer default is:

1. choose VND;
2. decline home-currency conversion;
3. continue only if the fee and currency screen are clear;
4. cancel and try another ATM if the screen makes the conversion or fee unclear.

Declining conversion should not, by itself, stop an international cash withdrawal. The reader may still face the ATM operator fee, home-bank fee, and issuer FX treatment.

## Safer and riskier ATM locations

There is no official Vietnam-wide safety ranking for ATM locations. This section is a practical risk-reduction checklist, not a guarantee.

| Location type | Practical fit | Main caution |
|---|---|---|
| Bank-branch ATM during business hours | Best first choice for large or first-time withdrawals | Still check fee and DCC screens |
| ATM inside a mall or large commercial building | Good visibility and easier backup if something goes wrong | May still have low limits or fees |
| Airport ATM | Convenient for arrival cash | Not necessarily cheapest; withdraw only what is needed for arrival |
| Hotel or serviced apartment area ATM | Convenient if in a visible, monitored location | Do not assume the fee is lower |
| Isolated street ATM | Use only when necessary | Higher practical risk if card is captured or screen is unclear |
| Damaged, loose, or poorly lit ATM | Avoid | Cancel and use another machine |

For Hanoi, Ho Chi Minh City (Saigon), and Da Nang, the same practical rule applies: a bank-branded ATM in a staffed or visible location is a better first choice than an isolated machine on a tourist street late at night.

Before inserting the card, check for obvious damage, loose card-reader parts, blocked cameras, unusual attachments, or a keypad that feels wrong. If anything looks off, cancel before starting.

## What to do if an ATM keeps the card or cash does not dispense

Card capture and failed withdrawals are uncommon but serious enough to plan for.

If the ATM keeps the card:

1. do not leave the area immediately if the machine is attached to an open branch;
2. note the ATM bank, branch, address, time, and machine ID if visible;
3. take a photo of the ATM location if safe;
4. call the card issuer to freeze or monitor the card;
5. ask the ATM bank whether branch recovery is possible;
6. assume same-day recovery is not guaranteed.

For planning, treat branch recovery as a same-business-day to later-business-day issue, not as an immediate fix. If the card is foreign-issued, the ATM bank may not be able to return it quickly or at all, depending on the bank's procedure and the card issuer's rules.

If the account is charged but no cash comes out:

1. keep the receipt if one prints;
2. photograph the error screen only if it is safe and does not expose sensitive data;
3. note the exact amount, time, and machine;
4. contact the issuing bank first;
5. report the ATM bank and machine ID;
6. keep records until the reversal or dispute is resolved.

Issuer dispute or reversal timing varies by card issuer, ATM bank, and transaction network. Do not plan around a same-day reversal; treat it as a days-to-weeks risk and verify the formal timing with the card issuer.

This is another reason to avoid using a final remaining card at an isolated ATM outside branch hours.

## Using a Vietnamese bank card at local ATMs

A Vietnamese bank account can make daily cash access easier for long-stay expats, but it is not automatic.

The advantages can include:

- local ATM network access;

## Decision 2345 and biometric rules: what they mean for ATM users

Decision No. 2345/QD-NHNN is Vietnam's State Bank decision on safety and security measures for online payment and bank card payment. It took effect from 1 July 2024 and replaced Decision No. 630/QD-NHNN.

For ATM users, the important point is not to overread it.

Decision 2345 should not be explained as a blanket rule that all ATM cash withdrawals by foreigners require Vietnamese biometric verification. That would be too broad.

A safer reading is:

- it matters for online payment and bank card payment security;
- it matters for local bank app and device authentication in some contexts;
- it matters more for Vietnamese bank-account users than for a short-stay visitor using a foreign-issued physical card at an ATM;
- it belongs beside later payment-account and bank-card rules when explaining local account access.

If the reader is using only a foreign-issued card at an ATM, the main practical questions are still card acceptance, ATM fee, DCC, withdrawal limit, card issuer fraud controls, and whether the machine dispenses cash.

If the reader has a Vietnamese bank account, the biometric layer can become more important.

## Circular 18, Circular 45, and the physical-card ATM nuance

Circular No. 18/2024/TT-NHNN on bank card operations, effective from 1 July 2024, governs bank card activities in Vietnam. For ATM users, one practical point is that card cash withdrawals inside Vietnam are in VND.

Circular 45/2025/TT-NHNN amends the bank-card framework and is effective from 5 January 2026. It is relevant to card issuance and biometric checks in specified card-related contexts.

The nuance matters:

- do not treat the 2026 card biometric rules as a blanket ATM ban;
- do not treat the physical-card ATM point as a blanket exemption from all identity or account restrictions;
- local account status, card issuance, app access, phone access, passport updates, and permitted-stay records can still matter.

In practical terms, a foreigner using a Vietnamese bank account should keep identity records current with the bank. A foreigner relying only on a foreign-issued physical card should focus more on ATM fee, limit, DCC, and issuer controls.

## Local bank accounts, passports, and biometric updates

For foreigners with Vietnamese bank accounts, the biggest cash-access risk is often not the ATM machine itself. It is the bank account behind the card.

Problems can appear when:

- the passport on file has changed;
- the permitted-stay document has expired or was not updated;
- the phone number linked to the account no longer works;
- the banking app requires biometric or device re-verification;
- the customer is outside Vietnam when the bank requests an in-person update;
- the app fails facial recognition or cannot match the customer profile.

If the account is important for rent, salary, tuition, or emergency cash, update bank records before leaving Vietnam for a long trip. Do not wait until the app is blocked or the card fails.

Foreigners who rely on a Vietnamese bank account should keep:

- current passport;
- valid visa, TRC, or other permitted-stay proof where relevant;
- active Vietnamese phone number;
- bank app login;
- branch contact route;
- backup card or cash source.

For phone-number setup and SIM continuity, see [Vietnam SIM card guide](/vietnam/daily-life/vietnam-sim-card-guide-2026).

## Cardless ATM, QR, and domestic transfer fallback

Cardless ATM withdrawals, QR payment, and domestic transfers can be useful in Vietnam, but they are not universal fallbacks for every foreigner.

They usually require at least one of these:

- a Vietnamese bank account;
- a supported Vietnamese banking app;
- a working local phone number;
- compatible QR or wallet setup;
- supported regional cross-border QR arrangement;
- successful app authentication.

A foreign-card-only visitor should not assume they can scan every VietQR code or withdraw cash through a Vietnamese bank app. A long-stay expat with the right local bank setup may find QR and bank transfer more useful than cash for many daily payments.

## Backup cash-access setup

A good 2026 setup does not rely on one ATM, one card, or one app.

A safer setup is:

| Need | Safer setup |
|---|---|
| Arrival cash | Withdraw a modest amount at a visible airport or bank ATM, then compare city options later |
| Daily spending | Use cash, card, and QR depending on merchant type |
| Rent or deposit | Prefer planned transfer or local bank route, not one ATM withdrawal |
| Emergency backup | Carry at least two cards from different issuers |
| Local account user | Keep passport, stay document, phone number, and biometric/app access updated |
| DCC risk | Choose VND / decline conversion when clear |
| ATM failure | Keep receipt, ATM ID, and issuer contact details |

A multi-currency backup card such as a [Wise multi-currency card](https://wise.com/invite/dic/ganqiuh) can be useful as one extra card in the wallet where available for the reader's issuing country and subject to Wise's current regional card terms, especially for readers who manage money in more than one currency. It does not remove Vietnamese ATM operator fees, does not make every ATM cheaper, and does not solve Vietnamese bank biometric or local account requirements.

## What to do at the ATM

Use this checklist before confirming a withdrawal.

1. Choose a bank-branded ATM in a visible location.
2. Avoid damaged, loose, or poorly lit machines.
3. Start with a smaller test withdrawal if using a new bank or card.
4. Read the fee screen.
5. Choose VND or decline home-currency conversion when the screen is clear.
6. Cancel if the fee, conversion, or language is confusing.
7. Take the card and cash before walking away.
8. Keep the receipt for larger withdrawals or failed attempts.
9. Check the posted transaction in the card app.
10. Try another bank if the first machine gives a low limit, high fee, or unclear conversion prompt.

## Bottom line

ATMs in Vietnam are usable for many foreigners, but they are not one uniform system.

For foreign cards, the main job is to avoid unnecessary fees, bad conversion, low limits, and failed withdrawals. For local bank users, the main job is to keep the account, card, phone number, app, passport, stay document, and biometric status aligned.

The safest rule is to treat cash access as a setup, not a single machine: one visible ATM, one clear VND transaction, one backup card, and one non-cash fallback.
