## The short answer

For most foreigners who already have a Vietnamese bank account and working mobile banking, the safest default for domestic transfers is usually a **bank app transfer**, often by scanning **VietQR** or using a **NAPAS 247** instant-transfer route when the sender bank and recipient bank support it.

For smaller consumer payments, wallets and payment apps such as **MoMo**, **ZaloPay**, and **VNPay** can be useful, but they are not the same as a bank transfer. They depend on provider KYC, linked bank accounts or cards, phone-number setup, fees, limits, and current app rules.

For rent, deposits, high-value services, or any payment that may later become a dispute, use a bank transfer where the recipient name, bank, account number, amount, and transaction record are clear. Do not rely only on a chat screenshot or a wallet notification for important payments.

| Situation | Usually use first | Why | Main caveat |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paying rent or a deposit | Bank app transfer to the landlord's verified bank account | Clearer account name and transaction proof | Confirm the recipient name before sending |
| Paying a small shop with a printed QR | VietQR or the shop's requested QR route | Fast and common for local payments | Check what kind of QR it is before scanning |
| Sending money to a local friend | Bank app transfer or wallet transfer if both sides already use it | Convenient for small personal transfers | Wallet access and limits vary |
| Paying an online seller | Bank transfer only after verifying recipient; COD/card if available | Reduces wrong-recipient and scam risk | Avoid pressure to transfer before checking details |
| Paying a bill or app-supported service | Bank app, MoMo, ZaloPay, or VNPay if the biller is listed | Useful for recurring payments | Confirm biller, customer code, and fee screen |
| Sending a larger domestic amount | Bank app if limit allows, or bank counter | Better proof and support route | NAPAS network limit is not the same as your bank-app limit |
| No Vietnamese bank account yet | Cash, card, or recipient-assisted payment | More realistic before local setup works | Do not assume wallets will work without KYC and linking |

This guide covers domestic VND payments inside Vietnam. It does not cover international remittances, crypto, business merchant acquiring, payroll, tax reporting, or corporate accounting. For account setup, use the [Vietnam bank account guide](/vietnam/banking/bank-account-guide-2026). For foreign card fallback, use [International Cards in Vietnam](/vietnam/banking/international-cards-vietnam-2026).

## First, identify what kind of payment you are making

A lot of payment confusion in Vietnam comes from one word: **QR**.

A QR code in Vietnam can mean several different things:

- a **VietQR bank-account QR**, which usually fills in a bank account, account name, and amount inside a bank app;
- a **wallet QR**, used inside an app such as MoMo, ZaloPay, or another wallet;
- a **merchant QR**, where the merchant's payment provider processes the transaction;
- a **gateway QR**, where a biller, e-commerce seller, or service platform routes payment through a processor;
- a **personal QR**, generated by an individual bank account or wallet.

Before sending money, ask two questions:

1. **Where is the money leaving from?**  
   A Vietnamese bank account, wallet balance, linked card, foreign card, or cash?

2. **Where is the money going?**  
   A bank account, wallet account, merchant payment gateway, biller, or individual seller?

If both sides are bank accounts, bank app transfer or VietQR is usually the cleanest route. If both sides already use the same wallet, a wallet transfer may be fine for smaller informal payments. If the recipient is a landlord, school, clinic, agent, or service provider, a named bank-account transfer usually gives better proof.

## How the main systems fit together

Vietnam's domestic payment setup has several layers. They often appear together inside one phone app, but they are not the same thing.

| Layer | What it is | What the user sees |
|---|---|---|
| Bank app transfer | The sender's bank app moves money from the sender's Vietnamese bank account | Account number, bank name, recipient name, transfer memo, QR scan |
| NAPAS 247 | A domestic instant-transfer network used by many banks and payment providers | “Fast transfer,” “24/7 transfer,” “NAPAS 247,” or instant interbank route |
| VietQR | A QR-based bank-transfer/payment standard and user flow | Scan QR, recipient details appear, confirm inside bank app |
| MoMo | E-wallet and consumer payment app | Wallet balance, linked bank/card, wallet transfers, bill payments, QR payments |
| ZaloPay | E-wallet and payment app integrated with Zalo ecosystem | Wallet transfers, bank-account transfer features, biller and merchant payments |
| VNPay | Payment intermediary / wallet / QR and gateway ecosystem | VNPay-QR, merchant payments, app or gateway flows |
| Cash/card | Fallback outside local transfer rails | Useful when local bank or wallet setup is not working |

The key distinction is this: **NAPAS and VietQR are not consumer wallets**. NAPAS is the payment-network layer. VietQR is a QR-based transfer/payment flow. MoMo, ZaloPay, and VNPay are app/provider layers with their own registration, KYC, fees, and limits.

## VietQR: when it is the right choice

VietQR is often the most convenient way to send money to a bank account in Vietnam because scanning the QR can fill in the recipient's bank and account details automatically.

It is a good fit when:

- the recipient provides a printed or generated VietQR code;
- the sender's bank app can scan the code;
- the recipient name appears correctly before confirmation;
- the amount and memo are clear;
- the payment is domestic VND;
- the sender wants cleaner proof than cash.

It is less suitable when:

- the QR opens only inside a wallet the sender has not set up;
- the recipient name does not match the expected person or business;
- the QR has been sent by an unfamiliar online seller under pressure;
- the amount is large and the sender's bank limit is uncertain;
- the sender needs stronger contractual proof for a deposit or refund-sensitive transaction.

Before confirming a VietQR payment, check:

- bank name;
- account name;
- account number, if shown;
- amount;
- transfer memo;
- whether the app says the transfer is free or shows a fee;
- whether the confirmation screen is still inside the correct bank app.

If the recipient name looks wrong, stop. Ask the recipient to confirm the account details in writing.

## NAPAS 247: what it means for the reader

NAPAS 247 is important because it supports instant domestic transfers across participating banks and payment providers. In everyday use, the reader may not open a “NAPAS app.” Instead, the sender sees the route inside a bank app, ATM, counter channel, or payment interface.

At the system level, NAPAS 247 supports transfers under 500m VND per transaction and can operate 24/7, including weekends and holidays. These figures are based on the NAPAS official Digital Payment Handbook: https://en.napas.com.vn/digital-payment-handbook.

In practice, that does not mean every foreign user can send any amount under 500m VND from any bank app. The real limit can be lower because of:

- the sender bank's daily limit;
- the sender's customer tier;
- app security settings;
- biometric or OTP requirements;
- new-device checks;
- account status;
- anti-fraud controls;
- recipient-bank availability;
- temporary maintenance.

For a large transfer, do not rely only on the NAPAS network maximum. Check the bank app's limit before the deadline. For rent, deposits, tuition, medical bills, or other important transfers, consider visiting the bank branch or asking the bank how to raise the limit before sending.

## Bank app transfers: the default for formal payments

A bank app transfer is usually the best default when the sender already has a Vietnamese bank account and the payment matters.

Use it for:

- rent;
- housing deposits;
- school or clinic payments;
- service providers;
- larger local purchases;
- agents or formal invoices;
- paying a known recipient where a clear record matters.

The main advantages are:

- the recipient account name is usually visible before confirmation;
- the transaction record is inside the bank app;
- the sender can download or screenshot a transaction detail;
- support escalation starts with the sender's bank;
- proof is easier to match with a contract, invoice, Zalo message, or email.

The main limitations are:

- foreign users may need a local phone number and active mobile banking;
- biometric or security checks can block app access;
- daily transfer limits may be lower than expected;
- new-device login or app updates can trigger extra checks;
- some issues require a branch visit.

Public fee examples vary by bank. Vietcombank's 2026 VCB Digibank fee schedule lists ordinary transfers to Vietcombank, other Vietnamese banks, and 24/7 transfers to other banks as free, while some special-route rows show 9,000 VND or percentage-based fees. Techcombank's December 2025 non-tier e-banking fee schedule lists same-system, interbank, and 24/7 transfers by card or account number as free through e-banking, while counter routes can carry percentage-based fees. VPBank states that interbank transfers through Internet Banking, Mobile Banking, or e-wallet channels are free, while fee-based counter or ATM channels follow each bank's fee schedule. Treat these as current public anchors only; banks can change fee schedules, and the in-app confirmation screen controls the actual transaction. If a bank app suddenly fails before an important payment, avoid improvising with a large wallet payment unless the recipient specifically accepts it and the proof is adequate. Use the bank counter, ask the bank to fix mobile banking, or pay cash only with a written receipt.

## MoMo: useful, but not a universal foreigner solution

MoMo is one of Vietnam's most visible consumer payment apps. It can be useful for smaller payments, billers, wallet transfers, and app-based payments if the account is already verified and the funding source works.

Official/provider layer: MoMo provider pages publish wallet verification and limit information, including foreign-passport verification guidance and limits for verified linked accounts.

Practical layer: this does not mean every foreigner can activate every MoMo function. Wallet use can depend on:

- local phone-number setup;
- identity verification;
- foreign passport capture quality;
- linked bank account or card;
- biometric verification;
- app version;
- provider support;
- whether the specific service requires a type of verification the foreign user can complete.

Use MoMo for:

- smaller daily payments where the merchant accepts it;
- billers listed inside the app;
- transfers to contacts who already use MoMo;
- top-ups or small app-based payments.

Avoid treating MoMo as the default for:

- rent deposits;
- large transfers;
- payments to unfamiliar sellers;
- any transfer where bank-account proof matters;
- urgent payments before the app has been tested.

Provider-published limits can change. MoMo's Help Center page “Hạn mức chuyển/nhận tiền mỗi ngày là bao nhiêu?” currently gives examples such as 30m VND/day for transfers and 100m VND/day for receiving money for verified linked accounts: https://www.momo.vn/hoi-dap/han-muc-chuyen-nhan-tien-moi-ngay-la-bao-nhieu. Separate MoMo limit pages list wallet-balance and withdrawal/payment limits. Treat these as MoMo-specific app anchors, not national wallet rules.

## ZaloPay: useful when the recipient or biller already fits the ZaloPay flow

ZaloPay can be useful because many local users are already inside the Zalo ecosystem. It may be convenient for smaller payments, billers, and supported wallet or bank-account transfer flows.

Official/provider layer: ZaloPay publishes fee schedules and transfer-service pages, including a free monthly transfer quota for some flows and post-quota fees. The fee anchor is ZaloPay's official intermediary-payment service fee schedule: https://zalopay.vn/quy-dinh/bieu-phi-su-dung-dich-vu-trung-gian-thanh-toan-zalopay.

Practical layer: the fee schedule does not prove that every foreigner can use every function. Full functionality can still depend on verification, linked funding source, current app rules, and the exact transaction route.

Use ZaloPay for:

- smaller consumer payments;
- billers listed inside the app;
- transfers where both sides already use the flow;
- merchant payments where ZaloPay is clearly accepted.

Be careful with:

- bank-account transfer features you have not tested;
- post-quota fees;
- card-funded wallet payments;
- payments to unfamiliar sellers;
- formal payments where a bank transfer record is better.

Do not assume a “free transfer” headline applies to every amount, every funding source, or every month. Check the confirmation screen before sending.

## VNPay and VNPay-QR: common payment context, but check the exact route

VNPay appears in Vietnam as a payment intermediary, wallet, QR, app, and gateway context. For many foreigners, the most common encounter is a VNPay-QR or gateway payment at a merchant, biller, or online checkout.

Official/provider layer: VNPAY terms allow service fees and state that fee schedules may be announced and adjusted. The fee anchor is VNPAY's e-wallet terms page: https://vidientu-static.vnpay.vn/mobile-terms-en.html.

Practical layer: the user experience depends on whether the payment is happening through a bank app, a VNPay wallet, a merchant QR, a biller gateway, or another supported route. These are not always interchangeable.

Use VNPay-related routes when:

- the merchant or biller clearly supports them;
- the app shows the correct merchant and amount;
- the sender understands the funding source;
- the confirmation screen shows any fee before payment.

Do not treat VNPay as a universal replacement for bank transfer. For rent, deposits, or high-value transfers, a named bank-account transfer is usually easier to document.

## What to use for common expat situations

### Rent and deposits

Use a bank transfer to the landlord's verified bank account whenever possible. Ask for the bank name, account number, account holder name, amount, and memo. Keep the transaction detail and a message from the landlord confirming receipt.

Avoid:

- paying a large deposit to a personal wallet unless the landlord specifically requests it and the proof is clear;
- paying cash without a written receipt;
- transferring to an agent's account unless the contract and authorization are clear;
- sending to an account name that does not match the expected landlord, company, or property manager.

For broader rental risk, see [Avoid Rental Scams in Vietnam](/vietnam/housing/avoid-rental-scams-vietnam-2026).

### Utilities and building fees

For electricity, internet, water, and building fees, the safest method depends on who controls the bill.

Use the biller's official channel, the building's stated payment route, or a bank/wallet bill-payment function only after checking the customer code and amount. If paying a landlord or building manager, ask for written confirmation that the amount is credited to the correct bill period.

For electricity-bill setup as a renter, see [Electricity Bills for Renters in Vietnam](/vietnam/housing/electricity-bills-renters-2026).

### Food delivery, groceries, and small daily purchases

For small daily payments, any working method accepted by the seller may be fine:

- cash;
- bank QR;
- wallet QR;
- card where accepted;
- in-app payment.

The risk is lower because the amounts are smaller, but the same rule still applies: check the amount and recipient before confirming. For daily app context, see [Food Delivery in Vietnam](/vietnam/daily-life/food-delivery-vietnam-expats-2026) and [Grocery Shopping in Vietnam](/vietnam/daily-life/grocery-shopping-vietnam-expats-2026).

### Paying friends

For splitting dinner, paying a local friend, or reimbursing someone, use the method both sides already trust:

- bank transfer if both have local bank accounts;
- VietQR if the friend sends a bank QR;
- MoMo or ZaloPay if both users already have verified wallets;
- cash for very small amounts.

A small test transfer is useful before sending a larger amount to a new recipient.

### Paying online sellers

Online sellers are the highest-risk casual-transfer scenario. A QR code, bank account, or wallet name does not prove the seller is legitimate.

Before paying:

- check whether the seller is a real shop, marketplace, or individual;
- compare the account name with the business name;
- avoid pressure tactics;
- use COD or card checkout when available;
- keep chat history and payment details;
- avoid large prepayments to unfamiliar accounts.

If the account name or QR looks unrelated, stop.

## Safety checklist before sending money

Before confirming any domestic transfer, check:

- recipient name;
- bank or wallet name;
- account number or wallet ID, if shown;
- amount;
- currency, usually VND for domestic transfers;
- transfer memo;
- fee;
- whether the confirmation screen is inside the expected bank app or wallet;
- whether the payment is reversible, refundable, or final in practice.

For rent, deposits, services, clinics, schools, agents, and large purchases, also keep:

- transaction ID;
- transaction detail screenshot or PDF;
- invoice, contract, or payment request;
- chat/email confirming the amount and recipient account;
- receipt or written confirmation after payment.

A screenshot saying “processing” is not the same as a completed transfer. Wait for the final transaction detail when proof matters.

## What to do if a transfer fails

If the money is not deducted, do not retry repeatedly at high value. Check:

- internet connection;
- bank maintenance notice;
- app version;
- OTP or biometric prompt;
- daily transfer limit;
- recipient bank status;
- account balance;
- whether the QR or account details are valid.

If the money is deducted but the recipient says they did not receive it:

1. Save the transaction detail.
2. Contact the sending bank or app support.
3. Give the transaction ID, time, amount, recipient bank/account, and screenshot.
4. Ask the recipient to check their bank account, not only chat notifications.
5. Avoid sending a second payment until the first status is clear unless the sender accepts the risk.

## What to do if money was sent to the wrong person

Do not assume a completed transfer can be reversed.

Act quickly:

- contact the sending bank or app support;
- provide the transaction ID and recipient details;
- ask the recipient, if known, to return the money;
- keep all messages;
- escalate through the bank or competent authority if the amount is significant.

The practical outcome can depend on bank handling, recipient cooperation, speed of reporting, and legal escalation. The safest protection is still prevention: check the recipient name before confirming.

## When cash or card is still the right choice

Cash or card is not outdated in Vietnam. It is a useful fallback when:

- the foreigner has not opened a local bank account;
- mobile banking is locked;
- a wallet is not verified;
- the seller does not accept bank QR;
- the transaction is too small for transfer effort;
- a foreign card is accepted and the amount is clear;
- the bank app is under maintenance.

Cash is weak for disputes unless there is a written receipt. Card is useful at formal merchants, but many small sellers still prefer cash or bank QR. For card acceptance and backup planning, see [International Cards in Vietnam](/vietnam/banking/international-cards-vietnam-2026).

## The safest setup for a foreign resident

A practical domestic-payment setup in Vietnam is:

1. **VND cash for small and backup payments.**
2. **One foreign card for hotels, malls, online checkout, and emergencies.**
3. **A Vietnamese SIM that stays active.**
4. **A Vietnamese bank account with mobile banking.**
5. **VietQR/NAPAS transfer tested with a small amount.**
6. **Optional wallets such as MoMo, ZaloPay, or VNPay only after verification and linking work.**
7. **A saved proof habit for rent, deposits, services, and online sellers.**

The right method is not always the most convenient one. For small daily payments, convenience matters. For rent, deposits, large transfers, or unfamiliar recipients, recipient verification and proof matter more.
